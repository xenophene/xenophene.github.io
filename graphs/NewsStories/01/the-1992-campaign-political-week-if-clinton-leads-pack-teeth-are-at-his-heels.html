THE 1992 CAMPAIGN: Political Week; If Clinton Leads Pack, Teeth Are at His Heels

By ROBIN TONER
Published: January 14, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

If the buzz inside the Capital Beltway can be believed, the Democrats may well have a candidate before they ever have a real campaign. Gov. Bill Clinton of Arkansas, by virtue of his early strength in organizing and fund raising, not to mention message-making and pundit-stroking, has become the hot candidate of the post-Cuomo period.

Some of the more cautious Democrats note that at this point in 1984, Walter F. Mondale was a much more prohibitive front-runner and former Senator Gary Hart barely showed a pulse, yet Mr. Hart proceeded to win New Hampshire and nearly destroy Mr. Mondale's campaign.

Front-runners, in other words, are often bleeders, particularly front-runners who acquire that status with no input from the voters, like Mr. Mondale or Senator Edmund S. Muskie of Maine in 1972.

Still, the Democrats have constructed not only a primary calendar but also a state of mind for 1992 that could allow a well-organized Southerner like Mr. Clinton to wrap up the nomination early. But quickly settling on an untested nominee from an under-financed, struggling field carries risks of its own.

"It's an extraordinary myth that all the party needs is an early nominee," said Harrison Hickman, the poll taker for Senator Bob Kerrey of Nebraska. An early nominee might have more time to organize against President Bush, but he misses the seasoning of a tough campaign and is open to the pre-convention jitters that produced the panicky "Anybody But Carter" movement in 1976.

The scenario for an early Clinton victory was fueled by recent polls showing him at the head of the pack in New Hampshire, neck and neck with a neighboring son, former Senator Paul E. Tsongas of Massachusetts. If Mr. Clinton wins there, many Democrats believe, it will be hard to stop him.

The major reason is March 10, Super Tuesday, which involves a large number of delegates, as its name suggests, and has a strongly Southern cast.

Twelve states hold primaries or caucuses that day, eight in Southern or border states. Mr. Clinton's fund-raising edge is also important; because of its sheer size and expensive media markets, Super Tuesday demands super money to compete.

Between New Hampshire and Super Tuesday, Democrats have only a handful of opportunities to change the zeitgeist of the race.

Still, as Thomas E. Donilon, a veteran Democratic strategist puts it, "It's late, but it's early." In other words, it is just five weeks before New Hampshire, but real people are only beginning to pay attention.

This week will be important to Mr. Clinton, as he tries to maintain the image of a sleek front-runner with a national campaign and the ever elusive "Big Mo," as George Bush called it in 1980 before he lost it to Ronald Reagan.

Mr. Clinton was in Chicago today, and will head on to California, Nevada and Florida, then back to Boston and New Hampshire. Next Sunday could be his first, full taste of the downside of front-runner status: Most of his rivals will probably be gunning for him in the televised debate that night in Manchester.

Chief among them will be Mr. Kerrey, who is clearly -- perhaps desperately -- looking for his opening and can ill afford another week of underachieving.

The Senator from Nebraska was widely predicted to be one of the most formidable competitors. But he entered the race late, drifted for most of the fall, and has gone through the kind of downgrading by the press and the political professionals that comes to candidates who fail to live up to expectations.

He has now reorganized his campaign and signed on Doak and Shrum, the consulting firm that produced television ads for Representative Richard A. Gephardt of Missouri in 1988 -- emotional appeals to economic nationalism that ran under the theme, "It's your fight, too."

Mr. Kerrey is on the air in New Hampshire with a similarly trade-oriented message that urges "Fight back, America." The question is whether he has time to pull off this transformation.

Senator Tom Harkin's rallying cry for traditional Democratic values certainly has its appeal in the party, but the Iowan has yet to begin his television campaign in New Hampshire and has shown little movement in the state.

Mr. Tsongas still has to persuade many in his own party that his candidacy has credibility beyond New Hampshire, and former Gov. Edmund G. Brown Jr. of California is running more of a crusade than a campaign for the nomination.

In other words, Mr. Clinton may be breaking from the pack -- but this has not been the most powerful pack. These are the candidates who entered the race when Mr. Bush was at the top of the public opinion polls; they may be risk-takers, but with the exception of Mr. Brown, they are all novices to Presidential campaigns, all largely unknown, all forced to scramble for money.

Down the road is President Bush, who might look vulnerable now but already says, bluntly, that he will do what is necessary to win. His advisers are expert at raising the risk factor for a little known Democratic challenger.

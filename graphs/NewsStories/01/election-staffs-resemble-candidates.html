Election Staffs Resemble Candidates

By ELIZABETH KOLBERT
Published: January 05, 1992

Correction Appended

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

It is said that by some strange quirk of biology, dog owners and their pets come to resemble each other. The same, it seems, can be said of candidates and the members of their campaign staffs.

Six weeks before the New Hampshire primary, each of the major candidates for President appears to have put together a staff that imitates, for good or for ill, his own strengths and weaknesses. Gov. Bill Clinton of Arkansas, for instance, has a reputation for being well-organized and disciplined; so, too, do his staff members.

Senator Bob Kerrey of Nebraska has a tendency to re-invent himself. He ran for governor with little political experience, then abruptly left office after one term and a few years later decided to re-enter politics by running for the Senate. Recently his staff underwent a remodeling of its own when a new campaign manager was brought in.

Although campaign staff members tend to work in obscurity, the behind-the-scenes team that produces a candidate's slogans and puts the spin on his mistakes is crucial to his chances. In the last Presidential campaign, President Bush's staff, led by James A. Baker 3d and Lee Atwater, was widely credited with helping to engineer his victory. Inexperienced Political Workers

At this point, some of the country's most seasoned political workers are sitting out the Presidential race. Roger Ailes, for example, who helped mastermind Mr. Bush's campaign in 1988, is not expected to play a role in the 1992 campaign. Nor is Bob Squier, one of Washington's most prominent Democratic consultants. This means that several campaigns are being led by men and women -- mostly men -- with little experience in Presidential politics.

Heading into the primary season, here's what the campaign staffs for the major candidates look like: Bill Clinton

At this point, Governor Clinton has not only the most well-organized, but also the largest staff of any of the Democratic candidates.

Mr. Clinton had hired a media consultant, Frank Greer, long before many of the other candidates had even found a campaign director. Mr. Greer's media credits include several successful Senate and governor races, including L. Douglas Wilder's 1990 campaign for governor of Virginia.

Mr. Clinton's campaign manager is David Wilhelm, a Chicago native who has run races for several Illinois Democrats. The campaign director is Bruce Lindsey, an Arkansan and a longtime friend of Mr. Clinton's. Stan Greenberg, a prominent Washington poll taker, is tracking public opinion for the campaign.

Among the top advisers to Mr. Clinton are Robert Reich, a Harvard University economist, and David Osborne, the author of several books on reorganizing government. Bob Kerrey

A staff shake-up over the last two weeks has put the Kerrey campaign under new management.

Tad Devine, an aide to Michael S. Dukakis in the 1988 campaign, is the new campaign director. He is replacing Sue Casey, a former aide to Gary Hart in his short-lived Presidential bid in 1988. Although Mr. Kerrey denies it, aides acknowledge that the shake-up was intended to boost a campaign perceived to be sagging.

Bill Shore, another former aide to Mr. Hart, is remaining as the campaign's political director. Bill Hoppner, Mr. Kerrey's chief of staff when he was Governor of Nebraska, is staying on as campaign chairman, but will be less involved in the day-to-day operation.

Joe Rothstein, a Washington consultant, is the campaign's expert on the news media and advertising. Polling for the campaign is being done by Harrison Hickman, a Washington poll taker.

Some of the top advisers to Mr. Kerrey are Mike McCurry, former communications director for the Democratic National Committee, and Bill Carrick, a Los Angeles political consultant. Tom Harkin

Senator Tom Harkin of Iowa entered the Presidential race with his liberal, New Deal-style platform already staked out and has hired relatively few outsiders to help sell his message to the public. He relies heavily on people who have worked for him in the past.

The campaign director is Tim Raftis, an Iowa native who ran Mr. Harkin's Senate campaign in 1990. The deputy campaign director is Kay Casstevens, Mr. Harkin's chief aide in the Senate.

Last month Mr. Harkin finally added a media consultant and a poll taker. The media consultant is Ken Swope, who produced some of the commercials for Mr. Dukakis in the 1988 primary campaign. The poll taker is Paul Harstad, another native Iowan.

Among his top advisers, Mr. Harkin has Toby Moffett, a former Congressman from Connecticut. Paul E. Tsongas

Paul E. Tsongas, a former Senator from Massachusetts, is running his door-to-door-style campaign from his hometown, Lowell, Mass., and his staff members reflect the campaign's homespun approach.

The campaign manager is Dennis Kanin, who ran Mr. Tsongas's Congressional and Senate offices and then left Washington with him to join the same law firm. The political director is Paul Schone, who was Mr. Tsongas's political director in his 1978 Senate race.

Mr. Tsongas's television commercials, some of which are already on the air in New Hampshire, are being produced by Michael Shea and Fred Woods, Boston media consultants. Tubby Harrison, who is also from Boston, is doing the campaign's polling.

Among his top advisers, Mr. Tsongas lists Lester Thurow, dean of the Sloan School of Management at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology. L. Douglas Wilder

Governor Wilder of Virginia has increasingly been casting his Presidential campaign as an appeal to black voters, and the members of his campaign staff reflect this shifting emphasis.

Mr. Wilder recently hired Joe Johnson, a former aide to the Rev. Jesse Jackson, to be his campaign manager.

Mr. Wilder's political director is Ron Lester, a poll taker. Joe Trippi of Virginia is the campaign's media consultant.

Mr. Wilder's main adviser is Paul Goldman, chairman of the Virginia Democratic Party. Edmund G. Brown Jr.

In keeping with his quirky, "insurgent" campaign, Edmund G. Brown Jr., the former Governor of California, has a quirky, "insurgent" staff, which seems to consist largely of unpaid volunteers. His campaign manager is Jodie Evans, who worked for him when he was Governor. Patrick H. Caddell, who helped mastermind the outsider's strategy that Jimmy Carter used to win the White House in 1976, is an unpaid adviser to the campaign. George Bush

Chosen more on the basis of personal closeness than ideology or even campaign experience, the President's campaign team was announced even before Mr. Bush had officially declared himself a candidate for re-election; indeed, he still has not done so.

The campaign chairman and chief political strategist is Robert M. Teeter, a poll-taker and a close adviser to the President. Frederic V. Malek, a businessman and former Nixon White House aide, is campaign manager and will have the nuts-and-bolts responsibility for the campaign organization. The general campaign chairman is Commerce Secretary Robert Mosbacher; he will not be active in the day-to-day operations, but will perform a more formal role.

Mary Matalin, a protegee of Mr. Atwater, who died last year of a brain tumor, will be the political director. Ms. Matalin is the former chief of staff at the Republican National Committee.

A top adviser to the campaign is Charles Black, a Republican consultant who is the founding chairman of the National Conservative Political Action Committee.

Correction: January 14, 1992, Tuesday A chart on Jan. 5 listing advisers to Presidential candidates misstated the year of Edmund G. Brown Jr.'s last Presidential campaign. It was 1984, not 1988.

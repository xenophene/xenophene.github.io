Election Panel Is Hoping To Replenish Its Funds

By GWEN IFILL
Published: January 04, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

The Federal Election Commission, hoping to stave off a $150 million shortage projected by 1996 in the taxpayer-financed campaign fund, announced today that it would begin a public relations effort and an appeal to Congress to replenish its coffers.

Joan Aikens, the chairwoman of the commission, said the Presidential Election Campaign Fund would probably not run out of money in the 1992 campaign season, but only because Presidential candidates had made lower demands on the public financing mechanism this year.

"The candidates this year have started later," she said. "There are fewer candidates running, and they have raised less money."

To raise more money for the fund before April 15, when income taxes are due, the commission has hired a public relations firm to produce $120,000 in broadcasting and print advertising intended to encourage taxpayers to check off a box on their tax returns that directs the Government to divert $1 to the campaign fund.

At the same time, the commission is asking Congress to consider raising the checkoff amount or allocating special grants to head off the expected deficit.

A $1 contribution does not increase or decrease tax liability, and the money for the campaign fund is drawn from the Treasury Department's general fund once every four years. But the number of taxpayers who have opted to contribute to the fund has declined steadily since 1988.

Gov. Bill Clinton of Arkansas has the lead among the Democratic candidates in the race for the Federal matching funds disbursed by the the election commission. Fund-raising reports made public on Thursday showed that the Clinton campaign raised $3.3 million last year, $2 million of it December.

The Governor requested $831,209 in matching funds for February and had previously received $579,364, which was paid out this week.

Senator Tom Harkin of Iowa, the second most successful Democratic fund-raiser, submitted a request for $269,469 in matching funds for February to be added to the $1 million paid out to his campaign this week. He raised more than $2 million last year.

Senator Bob Kerrey of Nebraska requested $258,565 for his campaign for February, which would be added to the $574,597 he qualified for last month.

Lenora Fulani, the Presidential candidate of the New Alliance Party, received $624,497 in matching funds last month and has requested $141,269 more for next month.

President Bush's fund-raising and requests for matching funds total more than $3.6 million to date, far outstripping that any of the Democratic candidates.

Federal financing from the campaign fund is used to match contributions of $250 or less, dollar for dollar.

Two-thirds of the money in the campaign finance fund is to be reserved for the general election campaign this year, and only 21 percent of the money is to be expended in the primaries. The rest is spent on party conventions. 'Structural Flaw' in Program

Mrs. Aikens said the fund would continue to be stretched in future years, in part because of what she described as a "structural flaw" in the program that has limited the check-off amount to $1 while inflation continued to rise.

"In 1974 the statutory limit was established at $20 million," she said at a news conference today. "This year each party nominee will receive $55 million, representing a 280 percent increase in payout since 1974."

If more candidates run in 1996, as is likely if an incumbent is not running for re-election, even more pressure would be put on the fund to increase contributions. If that occurred, Mrs. Aiken said, "there may not be any public money available for the primary election campaigns in 1996, not because of a lack of taxpayer participation but because of the effects of inflation."

Last month the Democratic National Committee filed suit to force the Treasury Department to change the way money flows into the campaign fund so that a shortage could be avoided this year. Even though the election commission says there is little likelihood of a shortage occurring, a Democratic official said the rules should be changed. No Lobbying Effort Planned

Mrs. Aikens said the commission would not lobby for any rules changes and would take no position in the Democrats' dispute with the Treasury Department. "We send legislative recomendations to the Congress every year," she said. "What they choose to do with it is entirely up to them."

She was unwilling to predict whether the public relations effort would result in any significant increase in contributions to the campaign fund.

In the television advertisements a woman, a black couple, an Asian man and an elderly man are seen filling out their tax forms as an announcer's voice informs them of the tax check-off option and gives them a toll-free number to call for more information.

Presidential candidates qualify for public financing if they agree to limit the amount of private money they receive in primary campaigns and reject all private financing during the general election.

To qualify for matching funds, Presidential candidates must raise $100,000 in contributions from individuals from diverse areas, with at least $5,000 coming from a minimum of 20 states.

By Sam Howe Verhovek
Published: January 05, 1992

More Than a Backdrop

As a 27-year-old Democratic district leader in October 1977, Fernando Ferrer said he was "elated" when Jimmy Carter's motorcade pulled up to the rubble of Charlotte Street in the South Bronx, turning it into a famous symbol of urban America in need of help.

But by April 1988, when he was Borough President of the Bronx, Mr. Ferrer declared that he had grown weary of national politicians' using his borough as a photo opportunity without providing enough support from Washington.

So he spearheaded a debate on urban issues that he plans to repeat this year, at which he and other borough leaders will press candidates for specific pledges on what they would do for America's cities.

"The point of all this," Mr. Ferrer said, "is instead of taking a picture and using the Bronx as a campaign stop, I'm saying, 'Come here, stay awhile and say exactly what you're going to do for the Bronx and all the Bronxes of this land.' "

Mr. Ferrer said he had already received oral commitments from Senator Bob Kerrey of Nebraska, Senator Tom Harkin of Iowa and former Senator Paul Tsongas of Massachusetts.

How much of a difference it makes remains to be seen. The 1988 debate at Fordham University was attended by the Rev. Jesse Jackson, Senator Albert Gore Jr. of Tennessee and Senator Paul Simon of Illinois, but not by the Democratic front-runner and eventual nominee, Michael S. Dukakis, who begged off because of a fund-raiser that night in Washington. And the three who did come wound up disagreeing about nothing except handgun legislation.

The debate is expected to be held shortly before the April 7 primary, said Clint Roswell, a spokesman for Mr. Ferrer. If strong Republican challengers to President Bush emerge, he added, the office would be more than happy to organize a G.O.P. debate as well. Ignoring Cuomo's 'No'

Gov. Mario M. Cuomo may not be running for President, but out there people are still trying to run for him.

A Chicago political consultant, Phil Krone, acting on a suggestion by Mike Royko, the columnist, has organized a Draft Cuomo drive, with a number, (800) 92C-UOMO), staffed by volunteers who instruct callers on how to complete a write-in ballot in whatever state they live.

A call to the number the other day was answered by Jeanine Pedersen, a graduate student in history at the University of Illinois, who described the operation as "very much a grass-roots movement." Ms. Pedersen, who said she wants to run as an officially uncommitted but sentimentally pro-Cuomo delegate in the Illinois primary, said she and other volunteers were keeping lists of the callers just in case the Cuomo drive takes off.

"I believe we should go back to the way things used to be historically, which is, people were drafted to run," added Ms. Pedersen, who complained that modern-day conventions have been turned into a prearranged "media circus."

In New Hampshire, other Cuomo fans have started their own Cuomo write-in bid in the Feb. 18 primary. They were buoyed by a recent poll of 408 likely primary voters in the state -- taken more than a week after Mr. Cuomo pulled out -- showing the Governor ahead of all six candidates with 37 percent of the vote. Gov. Bill Clinton of Arkansas was second with 14 percent.

Volunteers in Chicago, as well as aides to Mr. Cuomo, say that all these efforts are receiving no help at all from the Governor's staff or the New York Democratic Party. Mr. Cuomo has repeatedly dismissed speculation about being drafted as a candidate.

Still, true believers note that Governor Cuomo has issued no cease-and-desist orders to them. And John Marino, the New York State Democratic chairman, who was assigned the exhaustive, quixotic mission of organizing a campaign until Mr. Cuomo pulled out Dec. 20, said he would allow party headquarters to be used as a site for "discussion" of a possible favorite-son bid by Mr. Cuomo in New York State.

"I think we're in the category of a dream here," he said in an interview. "But I have a right to dream." Campaigning for Something

Alan G. Hevesi is not a man without a country, but he is a man without a candidacy. So far anyway.

That has not stopped Mr. Hevesi from making a determined fund-raising effort for what he says will probably be a bid for citywide office in 1993. He pulled in more than $45,000 at a Dec. 17 breakfast meeting in Manhattan. He just doesn't know yet what office he might seek.

Mr. Hevesi, a 51-year-old Assemblyman from Queens, ran a distant second to Elizabeth Holtzman, who won the general election, in the 1989 Democratic primary for New York City Comptroller. He may run again for that office.

Then again, he may run for City Council President, a post that the incumbent, Andrew J. Stein, is expected to vacate in order to run for Mayor. And who knows? Mr. Hevesi might even run for Mayor.

"I don't want to preclude any possibilities," Mr. Hevesi said in an interview, adding that he would take his time making a decision. "The only predictable thing is that something unpredictable will happen."

In Move to Spur Economy, Bush Is Urged to Order 90-Day Ban on New Federal Rules

By ROBERT PEAR
Published: January 21, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

President Bush's senior advisers have recommended that he stop all Government agencies from issuing new rules for three months as part of a broad campaign to revive the economy by reducing the burden of Federal regulation, White House officials said today.

The recommendation, most likely to be included in Mr. Bush's State of the Union Message on Jan. 28, comes from Vice President Dan Quayle and other officials, including C. Boyden Gray, the White House counsel; Richard G. Darman, Director of the Office of Management and Budget, and Michael J. Boskin, chairman of the President's Council of Economic Advisers.

Rules required by statute and those considered most likely to have a beneficial effect on the economy would be exempt from the ban, Administration officials said. An Across-the-Board Ban

"What we envision is a moratorium on proposing new regulations, across the board in every agency, except those rules required by statute or those that stimulate economic growth," a White House official said, confirming a report in today's issue of The Wall Street Journal. "This will send a clear signal to agencies that the President wants them to re-examine all regulations and their impact on the economy."

The moratorium would affect most of the 4,800 regulations now being developed by Federal agencies.

White House officials said Mr. Bush was troubled by studies documenting a resurgence of Federal regulations since he became President in January 1989. The number of pages in the Federal Register, a crude but useful indicator of regulatory activity, increased last year by 26 percent, to 67,715 pages, the highest number since 1980, the final year of the Carter Administration.

As Vice President, Mr. Bush was in charge of President Ronald Reagan's Task Force on Regulatory Relief, which blocked hundreds of rules deemed burdensome to business, consumers, universities or local governments. But as President, Mr. Bush has allowed such rules to proliferate.

White House officials listed seven broad areas in which they now hope to reduce Federal regulation on entrepreneurs and businesses: environmental protection (including fuel economy standards for automobiles); energy (natural gas, electricity); transportation (trucking, ocean shipping, airlines); exports; communications (cellular telephones, cable television); biotechnology, and "access to capital."

Gary D. Bass, director of O.M.B. Watch, a consumer group that monitors the Office of Management and Budget, said the moratorium was "an outrageous attempt by the Administration to appeal to business interests in an election year."

Mr. Bass also said the effects of the moratoriium would run beyond three months because "agencies will be afraid to initiate regulations to protect workers, consumers and the public in general."

Representative John Conyers Jr., the Michigan Democrat who heads the House Government Operations Committee, which supervises Federal regulatory policy, said, "Particularly in light of what appears to be the total lack of any economic analysis to support this proposal, it is likely only to increase the perception that President Bush is desperate, rudderless and lacking in any original program to get the economy back on track." Improper Interference Charged

Mr. Conyers and Senator John Glenn, the Ohio Democrat who is chairman of the Committee on Governmental Affairs, have charged that the White House improperly interferred in the rule-making of Federal agencies.

White House officials said the 90-day ban on new regulations could be imposed by the President alone without action by Congress.

"For tax policy we have to go to Congress, and for monetary policy we have to depend on the Federal Reserve, but regulatory policy is one area where the President can act unilaterally," said a White House official who spoke on condition of anonymity.

Administration officials said they did not have a reliable estimate of benefits from the moratorium. One White House official said it could save businesses $10 billion to $100 billion a year. But Gail E. Shearer, manager of policy analysis for Consumers Union, said the Administration "undervalues the benefits of regulations that protect health and safety."

It is not clear who would decide which regulations are exempt from the 90-day ban.

Robert L. Clarke, the Comptroller of the Currency, and Edward M. Emmett, a member of the Interstate Commerce Commission, welcomed the prospect of a moratorium. Mr. Clarke said his agency was drafting "an absolute mountain of regulations, guidelines and restrictions" to be imposed on banks.

Mr. Emmett estimated that the nation could save $40 billion a year by curtailing trucking regulations. "We still have vestiges of a regulatory scheme from the 1930's," he said.

Murray L. Weidenbaum, who was chairman of Mr. Reagan's Council of Economic Advisers in 1981 and 1982, and James C. Miller 3d, who was Mr. Reagan's budget director from 1985 to 1988, applauded the idea of a ban on new rules.

"It will signal employers and producers that the Bush Administration is seriously concerned about the costs of regulation," said Mr. Weidenbaum, now a professor at Washington University in St. Louis. "It will also give regulatory agencies a more adequate opportunity to carefully develop the regulations that are coming out at prodigious rates."

The action recommended to Mr. Bush is reminiscent of a 1980 campaign pledge by Mr. Reagan, who said he would declare a moratorium on new regulations. As President, he killed many pending rules and ordered rigorous comparison of the costs and benefits of all proposed rules.

THE 1992 CAMPAIGN: DEMOCRATS; Clinton Seizes on Asia Trip To Depict Bush as Beatable

By GWEN IFILL
Published: January 15, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

Gov. Bill Clinton of Arkansas, who was helped by encouraging early polls in New Hampshire and a spate of good press notices, is seizing on President Bush's flawed Japan foreign trade mission as evidence that the Republicans are beatable this year and that he is the one to do it.

In recent days, the Presidential contender has begun attacking the Bush Administration with new vigor, casting himself as the change-oriented candidate and -- as the chief beneficiary of the early political handicapping -- sparing little attention for his Democratic opponents.

Although the voters who came here to see Mr. Clinton seemed more curious than committed for the moment, many seemed receptive to the notion he is promoting that electability should be the deciding factor in choosing a Democratic nominee.

And the President's trip to Japan gave Mr. Clinton a handy club with which to drive his point home. Trip as 'an Embarrassment'

"The President goes to Japan with the heads of our auto companies -- the evidence of our own failures -- to complain to them that Americans are buying Japanese cars instead of American cars," Mr. Clinton said. "The message seems to be: 'Please don't send these cars over here anymore; our people just can't help themselves.' "

The entire trip was "an embarrassment," the Governor said. "It was an opportunity lost."

Mr. Clinton draws appreciative laughter and applause when he makes these digs at Mr. Bush to the highly partisan audiences he addresses in New Hampshire, which will hold the nation's first Presidential primary next month. Many of those who come to hear him speak say they have been attracted by his television advertising and the assiduous advance work of his New Hampshire staff, which blankets targeted voters with mailings and leaflets.

There is little doubt that New Hampshire voters, sobered by a strained economy and unmoved by a campaign that was slow to start, have begun paying attention now that political commercials for Mr. Clinton, Senator Bob Kerrey of Nebraska and former Senator Paul E. Tsongas of Massachusetts are being aired with regularity.

Staff members for Mr. Clinton, who broadcasts the telephone number of his Manchester headquarters in his advertising so voters can call for copies of his economic plan, said they had received 1,300 phone calls from interested viewers on the first two days the commercial was broadcast. Issue of Electability

There is little doubt that electability has become a deciding factor for many Democrats in New Hampshire and beyond.

"Basically, I want a winner," said Alan Lambert, a rare-books dealer from Hancock who came to see Mr. Clinton open his Keene headquarters on Sunday. Mr. Lambert said he supported Bruce Babbitt, the former Governor of Arizona in 1988.

But even those who are impressed enough with what they have heard about Mr. Clinton to attend his campaign events say they are not ready to commit themselves yet.

"I'm not terribly impressed," said Rick Levine, who runs a stereo business and lost a race for New Hampshire state representative last month. "From Day 1 they said Tsongas is unelectable, that we cannot even discuss another Greek liberal from the Northeast."

After hearing Mr. Clinton in Keene, Mr. Levine said the Governor did not seem serious enough to warrant all the favorable attention he had received. "I'm really aggravated that they're all treating him as though this is the de facto candidate and that after New Hampshire all of the other guys will fall by the wayside," he said "The media is spoon-feeding us a candidate."

The legendary political contrariness of New Hampshire voters is not the only potential problem awaiting Mr. Clinton. In a state where state-imposed taxes are considered anathema, he has stumbled on occasion when he begins talking about how to pay for improved education.

Arkansas uses state taxes to support local education, while New Hampshire relies on local property taxes. In his speeches, Mr. Clinton stops just short of criticizing New Hampshire's economic policies, but he points out the inequities he sees in a financing formula that allows people who live in more affluent areas to afford better schools.

Mr. Clinton's standing as the candidate to beat has been enhanced in recent days by attacks from his Democratic competitors. Mr. Kerrey, for example, has harshly criticized the Governor for offering only the broad-brush outlines of a health-care plan.

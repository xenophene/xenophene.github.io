1992 CAMPAIGN: Players; Onetime Insider Adapts To Low-Key Campaign

By KAREN DE WITT
Published: January 27, 1992

Correction Appended

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

Peggy Connolly, once a consummate Washington insider as spokeswoman for the Democratic National Campaign Committee, left the capital in 1988 right after George Bush's election. She said she believed that a Democrat would not be elected President until the year 2000.

But today, Ms. Connolly, 36 years old, is press secretary to Paul E. Tsongas, former Senator from Massachusetts, who is seeking the Democratic nomination. Mr. Tsongas was the first Democrat to challenge President Bush.

"I had been frustrated with Washington and with the Democrats who were afraid to take risks and refused to be critical of Reagan," said Ms. Connolly, who had returned to her hometown, Westford, Mass., to care for her father. "They were fearful of the polls, which said that he was enjoying this tremendous popularity. But the polls said people liked Reagan, but they didn't like his policies. And it was frustrating to me that the Democrats wouldn't tie those two things together."

Ms. Connolly said Mr. Tsongas's announcement in April did just that. "I picked up The Boston Globe one day, and it said this guy was really gutsy to say that George Bush has to be challenged," she recalled.

She joined the campaign on April 7, 1991, but said she was unprepared for Mr. Tsongas's low-key strategy, which incorporated none of the smooth packaging and image making that she had been used to. The Potomac point of view did not dissipate easily, she said, particularly in the summer, when Mr. Tsongas received little press attention and Ms. Connolly felt responsible.

"I would come in and tell Paul that he was losing ground in the eyes of the national media, and he would reassure me that everything was going to be fine," Ms. Connolly recalled. "He would offer me the perspective of his past political campaigns, saying this was nothing compared to his Senate race. He's never demanded headlines or national columns. It's a whole new experience."

She is respected by journalists for her candid responses, conceding that her candidate lacks dazzle even while pushing his positive points.

Ms. Connolly said she "fell into politics" when she went to Washington shortly after graduating from Northeastern University in Boston in 1982.

Occasionally, her Washington instincts get the upper hand. She has tried to lessen Mr. Tsongas's use of chalkboards and flip charts to explain his economic plan because they make him look more professorial then Presidential, but she conceded that she had not been entirely successful.

Ms. Connolly is the youngest of six children in the family of a machinist in Westford, 10 miles west of Mr. Tsongas's hometown, Lowell. She worked as a reporter at The Lowell Sun, but did not meet Mr. Tsongas until she became his press secretary.

Like any good press secretary, she said she was sure Mr. Tsongas would win. "I believe in following your political gut," she said. "After all, in the office pool in 1988, I was the only one who chose Dan Quayle as Vice President."

Correction: February 5, 1992, Wednesday An article on Jan. 27 about Peggy Connolly, press secretary for the Presidential campaign of Paul E. Tsongas and former spokeswoman for the Democratic National Campaign Committee, misstated her age. She is 34.

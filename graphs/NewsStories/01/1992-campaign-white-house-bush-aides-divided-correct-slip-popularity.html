THE 1992 CAMPAIGN: White House; BUSH AIDES DIVIDED ON HOW TO CORRECT SLIP IN POPULARITY

By ANDREW ROSENTHAL
Published: January 21, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

The struggle over how to stop President Bush's political slide has fanned staff rivalries in the White House and intensified a broader debate over campaign tactics and economic policy, Administration officials and Republican strategists said today.

Inside the White House, they said, there is a lingering sense of confusion as Samuel K. Skinner, the new chief of staff, struggles to gain control of the policy apparatus and dominate Richard G. Darman, the powerful White House budget director.

Their conflict, concealed by a public veneer of cordiality, is linked to a disagreement over whether Mr. Bush has a public relations problem or a fundamental flaw in his domestic policy apparatus. Need for Action Is Seen

So far, Mr. Bush is said to have accepted the contention, held primarily by Mr. Darman, that his political problems are more a matter of communications than substance. The White House's efforts to come to grips with the political slide have produced a sporadic series of economic initiatives as well as political gestures.

But there is a general agreement among Administration officials, campaign aides and Republican strategists that whatever the diagnosis, Mr. Bush is struggling to catch up at a time when he should be commanding the political field and that he must do something.

Prodded by Mr. Skinner to abandon a position that the economy was basically sound and would heal itself, Mr. Bush last week made several trips intended to demonstrate his concern for the average American, but instead left the impression of a President apologizing for the state of the economy.

In another move intended to show that the President was grappling with the economic issues, the White House today announced proposals to halt the issuing of all new Government regulations for three months. [ Page A19. ]

On Tuesday, Mr. Bush will unveil a proposal to spend more on preschool children in the Head Start program, a move that officials said would increase financing for the popular program by more than $500 million in the 1993 fiscal year.

But many officials regard this approach as piecemeal and are frustrated that Mr. Bush bowed to Mr. Darman's insistence that the formal announcement of the economic growth package be held for the State of the Union Message next Tuesday.

To help cure the disarray that has struck the White House only five weeks before the New Hamshire primary, the first in the nation, Mr. Bush met with Mr. Skinner to receive his first plan to reorganize the White House staff, an approach that is expected to be unveiled this week. Officials said the plan will focus on communications rather than policymaking. New Slide in Polls

The sense of political shakiness in the White House was reinforced tonight as CBS News disclosed the results of a new poll in which approval of Mr. Bush's handling of foreign policy, his strongest suit, had fallen below 50 percent for the first time. The drop, to 48 percent, was recorded after the President's trip to Japan and represented an 11-point decline from a New York Times/CBS News Poll a week earlier.

The CBS poll of 2,897 adults, conducted on Jan. 14-19, had a margin of sampling error of plus or minus two percentage points. It showed that public approval of Mr. Bush's handling of the economy had slipped to 19 percent, from 23 percent in the previous poll. Jimmy Carter, whose lowest score for economic policy was 15 percent in July 1979, was the last President with an economic rating in this range.

The latest poll comes as the White House puts the finishing touches on Mr. Bush's State of the Union Message, which is being presented by the White House as the pivotal moment in both the last year of Mr. Bush's first Administration and in setting the stage for his re-election campaign.

White House and campaign aides are counting on the speech to give a lift to Mr. Bush's struggling campaign for the New Hampshire primary on Feb. 18, but his top advisers remain divided over whether Mr. Bush's economic growth plan goes far enough.

Having lost the battle over the details of Mr. Bush's growth proposals, conservatives in the Administration and the Republican Party are already arguing behind the scenes that the plan will not go far enough either toward improving the economy or correcting the politically damaging impression that Mr. Bush is too detached from the concerns of average citizens. Emphasizing Communication

Other officials contend that there is little Mr. Bush can do to right the economy, a view supported by many economists, and that the main problem is to make voters believe that he is trying.

"Bush seems to have bought the line that it's a communications problem, not a policy problem," one official said. "We're floundering. You have no growth for two years and the White House sits around saying 'Gee, we're falling. We haven't communicated. We've got to show we care.' "

Some officials and Republican strategists say the President has staked a dangerously high amount of his dwindling political capital on the State of the Union Message address, which rarely produces dramatic headlines.

"The way they have this set up, this speech has got to be better than Bush's speech at the Republican National Convention in '88 and in a venue totally unlike a group of 5,000 rabid Republicans," said a senior Republican strategist with close ties to the White House.

Behind the scenes, the relationship between Mr. Skinner and Mr. Darman bears the marks of a classic White House power struggle, with Mr. Skinner insisting that he and Mr. Darman get along fine while at the same time making it clear that he intends to break what White House aides described as Mr. Darman's "chokehold" on domestic policy.

Mr. Darman has been uncharacteristically quiet at staff meetings, officials said. But they said he scored a big victory last month by persuading Mr. Bush to hold his major economic proposals for the State of the Union Message and not promote some of them in advance, as others, including Housing Secretary Jack F. Kemp, Vice President Dan Quayle, and Robert M. Teeter, the campaign chairman, had been arguing.

Since then, officials said, Mr. Darman has appeared to be preparing the ground to blame Mr. Skinner if Mr. Bush's economic proposals do not go over well. At a meeting in Mr. Skinner's office last week to review the State of the Union plans, Mr. Darman announced that "he had produced an incredibly substantive budget and if that was not clear, it was a communications problem, not a policy matter," one official said.

Another official said, "If I was Sam Skinner, I'd be suspicious."

But officials said Mr. Skinner had not directly challenged Mr. Darman. "Sam either believes Darman's argument that we've just got a communications problem, or he's not willing to challenge Dick," an official said.

Mr. Skinner has let it be known that he is unhappy with the communications staff at the White House, currently headed by David Demarest, and is expected to announce a major shakeup this week, officials said. "Some people are saying internally that this is simply shooting the messenger," one official said.

Another said: "Historically, communications is always blamed when the White House message is weak. The communications system is a good one, but it has to have some grist to run."

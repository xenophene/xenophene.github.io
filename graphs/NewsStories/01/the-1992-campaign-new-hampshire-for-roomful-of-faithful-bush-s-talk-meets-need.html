THE 1992 CAMPAIGN: New Hampshire; For Roomful of Faithful, Bush's Talk Meets Need

By R. W. APPLE
Published: January 30, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

The blue Bush placards and brochures were stacked in the kitchen, and the 20-odd people gathered in Newbold and Nancy LeRoy's living room to watch the State of the Union Message Tuesday night were all die-hard Republicans, ready to give the President the benefit of the doubt -- several doubts, in fact.

But as Mr. Bush started to speak, even in this group, not at all enamored of insurgents like Patrick J. Buchanan or Democrats like Bill Clinton, there were a couple of gibes at the image on the television screen.

"He looks nervous," someone said.

"Oughta be," another replied.

When the President remarked good-naturedly that he wished he could have his wife, Barbara, deliver his speech, a man sitting on the floor commented: "Now he should sit down. He was joking. I wish he was serious."

But after that, everyone watched attentively. Times are bad in New Hampshire, as the whole country knows by now, and the people who had braved the LeRoys' icy driveway wanted to hear every word. Only whispered asides and a couple of whoops of delight -- one when Mr. Bush said he would make interest on student loans tax-deductible -- broke the hourlong quiet. Party Foot Soldiers

Nancy LeRoy conceded, "We're not typical voters in this room." But they were precisely the kinds of loyal party foot soldiers whose enthusiastic support Mr. Bush needs to retain if he is to convincingly beat back Mr. Buchanan's stiff challenge in the primary election on Feb. 18.

At the end, all agreed that the President had given a good speech and all expressed hope that he had got through to the state's voters.

"I think he's touched on most of the things that have been wrong," said Peter Karagianis. Mark Thurston, who operates a marina on Lake Winnipesaukee, said Mr. Bush had made "legitimate, usable, tangible shifts" that would appeal to the hard-pressed people of New Hampshire.

But there was a bit of New England stoicism in Mr. Thurston's reaction, too. Unlike some, he clearly did not expect the moon from Mr. Bush.

"Look, you heard the man talking about cutting the military back further, and that's good," he said. "But that obviously hurts our region. So it's an inevitability, this contraction we're going through, and we'll just have to get to the other end. It's not much fun, but then. . . ."

And there was also some New England skepticism on display as well. Puzzled by the Timing

State Representative Carl R. Johnson of Meredith said he doubted that the President's proposals would have much impact here by November. He said he wondered why Mr. Bush had waited so long, "why he didn't make these decisions six months ago."

The reason, suggested Ed Covino, a hotel manager, was that the President had been preoccupied with the war in the Persian Gulf and the revolution in the Soviet Union. That was fine with him, Mr. Covino said, adding: "It would have been very foolish for any President not to have taken a once-in-a-lifetime shot at making the world a safer place for us and our kids."

Mr. LeRoy, a civil engineer who, like Mr. Bush, went to Andover and Yale, though a good bit later, said, "I'm a little bit dubious of George's capacity to influence Congress." Some Want a Hard Line

So what should Mr. Bush do? Some, like Gov. Judd Gregg, who said in an interview the other day that he hoped the President "will put his thumb in Congress's eye," advocated a hard line.

Mr. Thurston said, "He'll have to call the Democratic leadership to blame for this stagnation," and Francis LeBranch, applauded Mr. Bush for "drawing a line in the sand here."

But "they won't pass his program," Mr. LeBranch said, because that would help "keep the Republicans in the White House for another 20 years." After March 21, Mr. Bush's deadline for Congressional action, he said, "They'll keep wrangling, and the people will get more disillusioned."

Bob Powers, who runs a scenic railway near the lake, said he hoped the President and Congress would work together, so he welcomed Mr. Bush's appeal for bipartisan cooperation with the Democrats to put the economy right. 'You Got to Compromise'

"In your marriage, your business, with your kids, you got to compromise," Mr. Powers added. "Same thing here. I like his saying, let's pull together the way we did in Desert Storm. George Bush has never been an extremist, not a reactionary, not from the far right or the far left."

Gail Thomas, who teaches at the New Hampshire Technical College in Laconoia, said many of her students, some of whom are unemployed adults seeking new skills, "simply can't afford to go to the doctor or to take their kids." She described herself as "middle class or lower middle class or something -- we keep slipping" -- and said that she and her husband had trouble paying their own medical bills when their insurance did not cover everything.

"I don't want a Federal program," she said, "but I need to hear more from the President. Tax credits are fine, but still, where do poor people find the actual cash, money in hand, to pay for their health care?"

There were other questions as well. But at least in one living room in the shadow of the White Mountains, Mr. Bush seemed to have stilled most of the apprehensions of his backers at a crucial moment in his Presidency.

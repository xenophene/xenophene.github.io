1992 CAMPAIGN: Endorsements; Elaborate Ritual Mates Candidates and Backers

By KAREN DE WITT
Published: January 21, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

Once every four years, people like Louis L. Goldstein, Joseph E. Gardner and Don Fraser migrate to the center of the political universe. Governors call them at home. Presidential candidates visit them at work. Rich and powerful friends take them out to expensive meals.

All three have modest profiles on the national scene but considerable regional fame and popularity. In a Presidential season, that makes them choice quarry for candidates hunting endorsements.

Mr. Goldstein, the State Comptroller of Maryland for nearly 50 years, endorsed Gov. Bill Clinton of Arkansas. Mr. Gardner, commissioner of the Metropolitan Water Reclamation District of Greater Chicago, endorsed Senator Bob Kerrey of Nebraska, and Mr. Fraser, Mayor of Minneapolis, endorsed Senator Tom Harkin of Iowa.

The competition for endorsements, now in full swing, is an elaborate courting ritual that is one of the few surviving vestiges of old-time politics. Like an Umbrella

It has been years since a politician could deliver thousands of votes through his political machine as Mayor Richard J. Daley of Chicago did in the 1960's, but endorsements are assiduously sought nonetheless. Like umbrellas just before the rain, it is better to have one than not.

Endorsements from public officials can broaden a candidate's appeal in a party, provide a stamp of approval, increase fund-raising power, generate momentum for a campaign and provide sophisticated political organizations that can be used by the candidate's own campaign.

"Legislative endorsements and endorsements by other political figures send a signal that serious people think well of a particular candidate," said Thomas E. Mann, director of governmental affairs at the Brookings Institution.

In particular, endorsements help in wooing the elected and party officials who make up 15 to 20 percent of the delegates to the Democratic National Convention. Endorsements provide credibility and a kind of political vetting of candidates with whom such delegates may be unfamiliar.

But it must be done early. After the New Hampshire primary, one or two strong candidates have usually emerged and campaigns have been refined and defined enough to develop a dynamic that lessens the importance of individual endorsements.

Getting an early endorsement is as delicate as courtship and as structured as diagramming a sentence. And every connection, every friend, every acquaintance of an acquaintance is used.

Mr. Goldstein had been acquainted with Mr. Clinton through several state and national professional organizations, but it was Michael H. Davis, a lawyer with one of the Maryland's most prestigious law firms, Venable, Baetjer & Howard, who approached Mr. Goldstein in December about endorsing Mr. Clinton. Mr. Davis then arranged for Mr. Goldstein, along with several other Maryland Democratic politicians, to meet with Mr. Clinton. That led to endorsements by Mr. Goldstein and other Democratic leaders, including Nathan Lundow, chairman of the Maryland Democratic Party.

Mr. Fraser had a personal connection with Mr. Harkin, having served in Congress with him. They also served as co-chairmen of the Center for International Policy in Washington, an organization that is concerned with human rights and international affairs. Candidates Play Big Role

Usually, at this early stage in the campaign, the candidate must come in and make the final pitch.

That is how Mr. Kerrey won the support of Mr. Gardner, who in addition to his water district job was the executive director of Operation Push until last month.

Mr. Gardner was the deputy national field director for Jesse Jackson's 1988 campaign for the Democratic Presidential nomination. He is well-connected among black organizations in Chicago and has close ties to black politicians and leaders across the country.

Though Mr. Gardner was approached first by other candidates, Mr. Kerrey won the battle. At a second meeting with Mr. Kerrey, Mr. Gardner was asked if he would be a senior adviser to the campaign on urban affairs. Shortly afterward, Mr. Gardner resigned his position with PUSH to join the Kerrey campaign.

Mr. Clinton appears to have gathered the most endorsements, with Mr. Kerrey next and Mr. Harkin in third place.

For Mr. Clinton, this is welcome but perilous news: Too many endorsements too early may give the impression that a candidate is the tool of specialized interests. Two of this year's Democratic Presidential contenders, Paul E. Tsongas, the former Senator from Massachusetts, and Edmund G. Brown Jr., the former California Governor, are eschewing endorsments altogether.

Changes in the Democratic Party since 1968 have undermined the strength of endorsements because the power of bosses and party discipline were diminished to bring more diversity into the party. Television has also changed the environment for both political parties, with a candidate's 30-second television commercial or points scored during a televised debate now able to overwhelm gains made through even the most sought-after endorsement.

"The only endorsements that really count in the end are the individual votes in the primary and caucus states," said Ted Van Dyk, a Democratic consultant.

Do not tell this to Mr. Goldstein. At 78, he still makes some 300 speeches across Maryland each year, and he has been at 11 Democratic National Conventions, either as a delegate or a member of the platform committee.

Now he is working for Mr. Clinton. "We'll be having coffees and teas, bull roasts and oyster roasts, and as soon as spring comes, we'll have crab feasts and bring out the people and get their support," he said. "We're going to go out there and sell Bill Clinton. Yessiree."

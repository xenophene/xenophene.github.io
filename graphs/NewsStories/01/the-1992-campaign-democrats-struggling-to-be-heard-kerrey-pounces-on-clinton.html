THE 1992 CAMPAIGN: Democrats; Struggling to Be Heard, Kerrey Pounces on Clinton

By ELIZABETH KOLBERT
Published: January 30, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

In most sports, when the front-runner stumbles, his competitors gain a clear opportunity. In politics, however, it is trickier. Because when the front-runner stumbles, he takes all the television cameras with him.

This is the awkward situation now facing Senator Bob Kerrey of Nebraska. For the last several days, he has been trying to draw Gov. Bill Clinton of Arkansas into a public debate on issues like health care and the environment. But as Mr. Clinton's campaign suffered under accusations that the Governor had carried on a 12-year extramarital affair, he ignored the gibes, and Mr. Kerrey's campaign suffered even more -- from lack of exposure.

Yesterday, Mr. Kerrey apparently decided that if the press would not take up his challenge, he would take up its.

At news conferences first in New Hampshire and later in New York, Mr. Kerrey issued his first public comments critical of Mr. Clinton's relationship with Gennifer Flowers, who says she was the Governor's lover. It was not the accusations of marital infidelity that disturbed Mr. Kerrey, though. It was the insensitive comments Mr. Clinton had made about Governor Mario M. Cuomo. A Change of Tone

Given the range of invective political opponents have been known to heap on one another, Mr. Kerrey's criticism of Mr. Clinton still left him well on the tame side of his profession. But taken together with other remarks he has made in recent days, it is an unmistakable change in tone for Mr. Kerrey, who has often seemed to be trying to run for President without hurting his rivals' feelings.

Opinion polls show that so far Mr. Kerrey has reaped no discernible benefit from Mr. Clinton's contretemps with reporters. Indeed, some polls show the Senator has lost support in recent days. With less than three weeks to go before the New Hampshire primary, the Kerrey campaign is hoping to reverse this slide through an open "engagement" with Mr. Clinton, who is still leading the Democratic field in most New Hampshire polls.

Such an engagement, aides to the Senator say, would at once help to set the two men apart, and provide Mr. Kerrey with some much-needed attention. A top aide to Mr. Kerrey explained, "We're looking for a tactical engagement that will carry over into some news cycles."

By yesterday evening, Mr. Kerrey could claim at least some modest success in this endeavor.

In tapes Ms. Flowers played for reporters on Monday, Mr. Clinton suggested that Governor Cuomo "acts like" a member of the Mafia. He later apologized for the remark, saying that he meant only to imply that Governor Cuomo "was a tough and worthy competitor."

Yesterday, Mr. Kerrey took Mr. Clinton on, saying he thought his apology "was too generic and inadequate."

It did not, he said, "express an understanding of the offense given to those who were mentioned by the tape, not just Governor Cuomo but the Italian-American population."

At an appearance yesterday afternoon in Hampton, N.H., Mr. Clinton responded to Mr. Kerrey. "Of all the things I might be faulted for in my public life," he said, "being insensitive to any kind of minority or ethnic group is not one of them, ever."

And at a fund-raising event tonight in West Orange, N.J., Governor Clinton apologized to Italian-Americans and anyone else who might have been offended by the remark. "Wounds were caused," he said. "I want to apologize again for any hurt that was caused to any Italian-American in this region, state or country or anyone else offended."

Mr. Kerrey's efforts to "engage" Mr. Clinton directly began last week. At a stop in Maine on Friday, for example, he said: "I've got big disagreements with Governor Clinton's environmental record, and significant disagreements over health care." Later that day, he said: "I'm for national health insurance and he's pretending to be."

His most recent television commercials are intended to underscore the theme of difference. In one that began airing in New Hampshire this week, Mr. Kerrey says: "The other candidates in this race have figured out that national health insurance is popular, so they're talking as if they support it. But they don't." 'A Counterfeit Proposal'

"I hope," he concludes in the commercial, "that the people of New Hampshire in this campaign will not accept a counterfeit proposal." Mr. Kerrey supports a national health insurance plan that would be financed though the Federal Government.

Mr. Clinton, in contrast, often says that he supports national health care, but his proposals explicitly avoid any form of nationalized care.

At the same time that Mr. Kerrey is seeking a clear confrontation with Mr. Clinton, he is also seeking a clearer formulation of his own views. Over the last two weeks, his standard speech has been undergoing continual revisions, until now it barely resembles the original. Gone are the abstruse references to "generic technologies," and in their place are repeated invocations of the "urgency" of the task at hand.

His new speech has won good reviews at recent appearances, and aides to Mr. Kerrey say they are encouraged that after a tentative start he is finding a voice. But the campaign has yet to work out all of its glitches.

On Tuesday night, for example, Mr. Kerrey and an entourage of aides went to watch President Bush's State of the Union Message at the home of an unemployed worker in Manchester, N.H. The occasion, chronicled by several television crews, seemed like the perfect opportunity for the Senator to demonstrate his support among victims of the recession and to score some points against the Republicans.

But when asked after spending the evening with Mr. Kerrey whom he planned to vote for in the primary, the unemployed worker, Frank Adamske, a registered Republican, said: "Pat Buchanan."

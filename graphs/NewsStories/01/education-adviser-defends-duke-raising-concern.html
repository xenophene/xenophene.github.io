Education Adviser Defends Duke, Raising Concern

By KAREN DE WITT
Published: January 19, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

An appointee to a national education panel has written newspaper columns supporting the message of David Duke, the former Ku Klux Klan leader and a Republican Presidential candidate, but Education Secretary Lamar Alexander has no plans to remove him, a Government spokeswoman said today.

John C. Hirschfeld, president and chief executive officer of The Champaign-Urbana News-Gazette, expressed support for Mr. Duke's message last November in two columns for the paper. Mr. Alexander appointed Mr. Hirschfeld to the National Advisory Committee on Accreditation and Institutional Eligibility in October.

The appointment of Mr. Hirschfeld, who is also a lawyer, has raised concern among some people in higher education because his advisory panel is considering whether groups that approve colleges' academic standing should use the recruitment of minority students and faculty members as one standard for approval.

In one column, Mr. Hirschfeld denounced Mr. Duke's membership in the Klan as "despicable" but said it was "only a subterfuge for liberal opposition" to Mr. Duke's message.

"What terrifies the liberals is not the Klan, but the message Duke is carrying," he wrote. Alexander Aides Concerned

Mr. Hirschfeld wrote that Mr. Duke has "touched the pulse of this country" with his opposition to higher taxes, illegal immigration and crime. Mr. Hirschfeld also criticized President Bush for "abandoning" Mr. Duke in his campaign to become Louisiana's governor.

The Jan. 15 issue of The Chronicle of Higher Education reported that the articles had made department officials concerned that Mr. Hirschfeld's appointment might cause political embarrassment to Mr. Alexander. The officials, it reported, were discussing ways to deal with the situation, from ignoring it to asking Mr. Hirschfeld to resign.

Etta Fielek, the Education Department spokeswoman, said today: "No action is anticipated against him. No one from the Department has spoken to him."

Ms. Fielek said that Secretary Alexander had not read Mr. Hirschfeld's columns. Free Speech and Responsibility

"I don't agree with what he said in his column, but he does have First Amendment rights to say it, " said Ms Fielek.

Mr. Hirschfeld could not be reached for comment today, a spokesman at his newspaper said.

Patricia A. McGuire, president of Trinity College in Washington is a member of the Middle States Commission on Higher Education States Association, a college accrediting group that has been under fire from the Education Department for using minority recruitment as one measure of performance. The advisory panel Mr. Hirschfeld serves on has recommended that Secretary Alexander renew Federal recognition of the Middle States association, but for less than the usual five-year period.

Ms. McGuire said that President Bush and Secretary Alexander should "distance themselves from any kind of support of the message of David Duke."

"Mr. Hirschfeld certainly does have the right to freedom of speech but he is in a position of considerable responsibility that affects not only him and his personal rights but the rights of millions of students all over the country," she said.

Ms. McGuire went on to say that President Bush and Secretary Alexander, in "affirming their commitment to equity and diversity, should certainly investigate this immediately and take appropriate action."

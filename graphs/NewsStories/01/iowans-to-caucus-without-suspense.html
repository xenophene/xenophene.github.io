Iowans to Caucus Without Suspense

By R. W. APPLE Jr
Published: January 13, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

What always fascinated the country about the Iowa political caucuses was their predilection for underdogs, like George McGovern in 1972, Jimmy Carter in 1976, Gary Hart in 1984 and Pat Robertson in 1988.

But this year, the underdogs have been shut out. The powers that be have construed the rules in such a way that the Presidential long shots are concentrating on New Hampshire. When Iowa speaks on Feb. 10, few people elsewhere will listen, because everyone knows precisely what will happen.

"The 1992 Iowa caucuses have been rigged for George Bush and Tom Harkin," wrote David Yepsen, the chief political reporter for the state's leading newspaper, The Des Moines Register. So little interest has been aroused that the paper canceled the candidates' debate that it had scheduled for Friday night. How It Works

Iowa's caucus system differs markedly from the primaries used by most states. Party members gather in homes or other precinct meeting places to announce their preferences or declare themselves uncommitted. A head count is taken, and the supporters of candidates who get less than 15 percent of the total are given an opportunity to regroup. Delegates to district conventions are allocated on the basis of precinct strength, those conventions choose delegates to state conventions, and the state conventions, one for each party, choose national convention delegates.

There is no written ballot, and without an informal straw poll at the start of the meetings it is difficult to determine the relative strength of all candidates on a statewide basis.

More than anything else, it is the candidacy of Senator Harkin, the state's pre-eminent Democrat, that has kept the other candidates -- and the reporters and television satellite trucks -- out of Iowa. His announcement last September, and his effective control of the state party machinery, guarantee that he will get the most caucus votes.

Some other candidate might well have tried to finish a surprising second; that was enough to set Mr. McGovern's campaign alight in New Hampshire in 1972. But the whole campaign got started so late that the careful, precinct-by-precinct organization that has been the hallmark of Iowa campaigns was out of the question, and with money so tight, a big television effort was unlikely.

Just to make sure, the Republicans announced early that they would not hold a straw ballot at the beginning of their caucuses, which might have shown Presidential weakness. The Democrats, for their part, resisted suggestions that they hold a straw poll, which would have made it clearer which candidate was second strongest and given the candidates a greater incentive to make a run here.

The Democrats also resisted pressure from a few mavericks like Ed Campbell, a longtime party worker, to have the caucuses vote by secret ballot instead of public declaration.

"The intimidation factor is significant," Mr. Campbell said, not only because many active Democrats owe jobs or other favors to Mr. Harkin but also because "people don't like to be asked, 'What, you're not for Tom?' "

As things now stand, "undecided" is likely to finish second, and it seems likely that none of the other candidates will meet the 15 percent threshhold needed to gain delegates to the Democratic National Convention. The Iowa Poll reported late last month that Mr. Harkin had 68 percent, with Senator Bob Kerrey of Nebraska next, with 10 percent.

On the Republican side, neither David Duke of Louisiana, the former Ku Klux Klan member, nor Patrick J. Buchanan, the conservative columnist, is mounting an effort here. The President will thus win all the delegates, even though this has never been one of his best states; he finished behind Senator Bob Dole of Kansas and Mr. Robertson, the evangelist, in 1988. The Kerrey Factor

J. P. Steffen, the executive director of the Iowa Democratic Party, said he thought some other candidate might still stage "a sneak attack" in the last two weeks before the caucuses. He added, "The other campaigns are trying to figure out how to get second place without seeming to have put any effort into it."

Coming from a neighboring state, Mr. Kerrey would be the most likely candidate to show strength against Mr. Harkin. He is especially well-known in the considerable stretches of Iowa that get Omaha television broadcasts. But he is also the most vulnerable in that he could be damaged heavily by a third- or fourth-place finish in his region.

James Pribyl, a Des Moines lobbyist who is coordinating the Kerrey effort in neighboring South Dakota, said there would be no late Kerrey campaign in Iowa.

"It's not a level playing field," he said. "It seems to me personally that if the party wanted to encourage other candidates to be active here they should have set up a straw poll at a minimum, or that plus a secret ballot. But even then where would the money have come from? Does anyone really want to spend three-quarters of a million dollars trying to put a dent in Tom Harkin?

"In a sense, Tom Harkin is doing the rest of the field a favor by giving them an excuse not to spend money in Iowa that they don't really have." Clinton Could Benefit

Gov. Bill Clinton of Arkansas would gain most from shoving Mr. Kerrey into third place, and he might be helped here by his standing in the national polls and the good press notices he has received over the last few months. But to try and fail would he hurtful, and his supporters have told him not to risk it.

All six major candidates -- including Gov. L. Douglas Wilder of Virginia, who has since withdrawn -- came to the state for the party's Jefferson-Jackson dinner on Nov. 23, but most haven't been back. Former Senator Paul E. Tsongas of Massachusetts has a local office, but the phone there is answered most of the time by a machine. The Des Moines hotels, car-rental agencies and restaurants have been hard hit.

Mr. Harkin, of course, keeps coming back, and not just in his capacity as a Senator. Having driven away most of the competition, he has to make sure that nothing goes wrong, that no backlash develops and that he at least approaches his own, self-proclaimed goal of "doing better than anyone has ever done before." The target would be Mr. Carter's showing in 1980, when he polled 59 percent against Senator Edward M. Kennedy of Massachusetts.

To get what an aide says is "the best we can do here -- no story," Mr. Harkin is reportedly spending $200,000. A weak showing would certainly cripple him in New Hampshire. But because he is a favorite son, even a powerful showing here is unlikely to help him in the first primary, on Feb. 18.

So Friday he was at the Iowa Methodist Hospital in Des Moines, talking about health insurance, and then off on yet another swing around the state.

Former Gov. Edmund G. Brown Jr. of California is waging the only other active campaign, and he has barely begun. He was here this week, traveling by motorcade to all the main cities and to a few smaller spots like Spirit Lake, seeking free television exposure, giving newspaper interviews, drinking gallons of coffee at sparsely attended receptions, attacking Mr. Kerrey and Mr. Harkin for having voted to give themselves raises, and emphasizing health care.

"It's like walking through a forest trying to rouse people," he said in Fort Dodge. "I've come here to give people a choice, and I find a fair amount of discontent that this has been made into a non-event. But I wonder whether people will buck the reigning king of Iowa politics to come out on a cold evening to support an outsider."

The expectation is that few will, and the turnout will be very low.

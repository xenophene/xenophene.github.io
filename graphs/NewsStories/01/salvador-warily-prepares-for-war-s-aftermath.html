Salvador Warily Prepares for War's Aftermath

By TIM GOLDEN
Published: January 19, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

AS though the civil war in El Salvador were dying a human death, faces from the whole course of the conflict seemed to flash before Government and rebel leaders as they made their ceremonious peace here last week. A Catholic Church human rights advocate sat at the treaty signing not far from a conservative businessman whose family was reputed to have financed paramilitary death squads. A few feet away, a retired Army colonel who has become one of the country's more garrulous right-wing politicians could look over at one of the guerrilla commanders whom he had once fought.

Across the ideological spectrum, no one save a few bewildered young guerrillas and a few more military officers acted as though there might have been anything they had lost. Dr. Charles Clements, an American physician who spent a year tending to rebel wounded on a strategic Salvadoran volcano, had a pleasant chat with one Salvadoran Army general; Jennifer Jean Casolo, an American religious worker who was once jailed by the Salvadoran security forces on charges of stockpiling weapons for the guerrillas, shook hands with another. The United States Ambassador to El Salvador, William G. Walker, wrapped an arm around Joaquin Villalobos, the guerrilla commander whom American officials had most despised, and talked about having lunch back in San Salvador one day soon.

Yet if the breadth of the celebration was startling after so many months of bitter, forced compromise, it also seemed to give way easily to sober considerations of what lay ahead. All of the conciliatory words between the rebel leadership and President Alfredo Cristiani suggested a gathering tolerance on both sides; they did not change any minds about what further reforms El Salvador should undertake or how. Even as the bloodletting was brought to a formal halt after 75,000 deaths, there was a sense among people who had participated or merely suffered in it that a far less violent struggle for political control would not necessarily be much less intense.

Along with the recognition that the war could not be won and had become a severe political liability to whomever was seen to perpetuate it, a confluence of changes helped stop the fighting. As the Bush Administration quietly abandoned the Reagan quest to vanquish a perceived Marxist threat, Congressional Democrats reduced American military aid. With the evaporation of Cuba's allies in Eastern Europe, the Salvadoran rebels began to distance themselves from Cuba's Government. And even before their election defeat in early 1990, Nicaragua's Sandinistas could no longer afford the Salvadorans uncompromising support. But while those shifts helped moderate Salvadoran politics, they did less to alter the economic inequalities and democratic failings that have always been the war's essential fuel.

There is little question that if fully implemented the treaty would bring many changes. Mr. Cristiani has long blamed the outbreak of war primarily on the repression -- much of it sponsored or carried out by his more radical allies -- that virtually shut off the possibility of legal politics to the democratic left. And as he has quietly allowed wider margins of press freedom and political activity, he has agreed to measures that should lift the remaining political restraints and halve the military's size to about 31,000 troops.

Nonetheless, some critics of the Government argued that the treaty would not end extremist violence, particularly from the right, the impunity of the military officer corps, or the corruption that has fed on more than $4 billion in United States aid over the last decade. Nor, as Mr. Villalobos hinted after the treaty was signed, would it necessarily keep the 6,000 or so armed rebels from responding in kind to any violence that might take place between the start of the formal cease-fire on Feb. 1 and the destruction of the insurgents' arms in late October.

The treaty promises land to peasants in rebel-held zones and the possibility of partial ownership for some workers in state enterprises that are to be privatized. It is also certain to give a new thrust to the economy, which has managed surprising growth over the last two years despite the war's enormous strain. By itself, however, the settlement does little to resolve a disparity of incomes that the war has left only more skewed, or to raise real wages that have fallen almost every year since 1980. Though many of El Salvador's rich have grown richer, only 46 of them paid taxes on income of more than $2,500, according to Christian Democratic Party figures.

How effectively or democratically the political party that emerges from the Farabundo Marti National Liberation Front will pursue economic and social change can only be guessed. Several rebel officials said their party may not run a presidential candidate in the 1994 elections. Instead, they would back a coalition of leftist and centrist parties they hope will stretch as far as the liberal faction of the divided Christian Democrats.

Some analysts believe that the former guerrillas could become the country's most disciplined and organized political force almost instantly and still not far surpass the low levels of support they have received in opinion surveys. Fragile Changes

The rebels have always dismissed such polls as distorted by the fears that the war has so ingrained in Salvadorans. But it would not be at all surprising if they found that their most effective tactics were the sort of street demonstrations and civil disobedience that the military and rightists attacked violently a decade ago.

It may be the clearest measure of the fragility of change that both sides seized on the presence of foreign dignitaries at their peace-making to plead for help in verifying the agreements and rebuilding the country. Already, the United Nations is sending more than 1,000 military and police observers to monitor the treaty's implementation. The many pledges of help, however, seemed to add up to far less than the $1.8 billion that Mr. Cristiani said reconstruction would cost.

Secretary of State James A. Baker 3d was among the guests in Mexico City unwilling to commit to any specific levels of aid, and there seemed to be an unusual degree of bipartisanship to his circumspection. "This is not an environment in which anyone in the Administration or Congress is thinking about making money available, because it is needed elsewhere," said Representative Robert G. Torricelli, a New Jersey Democrat who is chairman of the House Subcommittee on Western Hemisphere Affairs and supports reconstruction aid. "It has unfortunately become something of a national tradition that we are willing to spend billions to win a military conflict but withhold millions that would insure that peace is sustained."

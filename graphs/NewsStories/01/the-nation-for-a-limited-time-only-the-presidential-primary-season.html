THE NATION; For a Limited Time Only: The Presidential Primary Season

By ELIZABETH KOLBERT
Published: January 05, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

FIRST there were no candidates willing to enter the race. Then a non-candidate, Gov. Mario M. Cuomo, stole the limelight. Then the holidays came, and who had time to worry about a bunch of Presidential contenders slogging around in New Hampshire?

Now 1992 has arrived and it seems as if the race for the Democratic nomination is just beginning. In fact, it's almost over.

After years of complaining that the nominating process drags on too long, Americans are about to get the kind of primary campaign that they said they always wanted: a short one. The hesitant beginning not only gave the campaign a late start, it also set conditions for an early finish. None of the candidates has had time to assemble a large war chest or a far-flung organization, so none of them is likely to survive more than a few primary losses.

"This is less like a playoff series, and more like sudden death," said Ann Lewis, a Democratic consultant. "The winner is most likely to be the person who starts scoring early, and who doesn't make any mistakes."

On top of the financial constraints, scheduling changes, too, are promoting a short primary race this year. So many states have moved their primary dates up that the election schedule, once weighted toward spring, now has a decidedly wintry feel. Most political analysts predict that, barring any bombshells or surprise late entrances, the Democratic nomination will be sewn up by the time tulip season rolls around.

"If you were going to bet, you'd bet you will know the nominee by April," said Geoffrey Garin, another Democratic consultant. "The field is going to get smaller pretty quickly." Heading for New Hampshire

This week, the campaign season begins in earnest with a mass political migration to New Hampshire, the site of the first primary. The six major Democratic candidates are fighting it out there in what for some is likely to be a make-or-break battle. They are: former Gov. Jerry Brown of California, Gov. Bill Clinton of Arkansas, Senator Tom Harkin of Iowa, Senator Bob Kerrey of Nebraska, former Senator Paul Tsongas of Massachusetts and Gov. L. Douglas Wilder of Virginia. Dr. Lenora B. Fulani, of the New Alliance Party, will also be running as a Democrat in New Hampshire.

Patrick Buchanan, the conservative columnist, will be in New Hampshire, too, trying to wrestle Republican delegates away from President Bush. While Mr. Buchanan is given virtually no chance of winning the nomination, he could embarrass Mr. Bush with a strong showing on Feb. 18. He made his first request for Federal matching funds last week and declared that he would match Mr. Bush's spending in New Hampshire "dollar for dollar."

With the caveat that a campaign can be thrown into turmoil at any moment, here is a schedule of key dates for the weeks ahead: Feb. 10: The Iowa caucuses. Any Iowa Democrat who wants to participate may go to a school or a living room and, in a public vote, raise a hand for his or her candidate. In the past the caucus has been a critical early test. But with Senator Harkin, an Iowa native, in the race this year, the caucus is viewed as little more than a warm-up exercise. Still, a strong second-place showing could bolster the candidacy of a non-native, while a weaker-than-expected win by Mr. Harkin would be a serious setback. Feb. 18: The New Hampshire primary. The nation's earliest Presidential primary has taken on near-mythic significance this year. For any Democrat besides Governor Clinton, whose base is in the South, a poor showing in the state could prove fatal. "The current crop of candidates will be cut in half by the morning after New Hampshire," said Hank Morris, a New York-based Democratic consultant.

On the Republican side, supporters of President Bush are concerned that Mr. Buchanan could receive a strong protest vote in the recession-racked state. Buchanan forces meanwhile are working to diminish expectations. "If Pat gets 10, 15, 20 percent, it will be a devastating blow," said Paul Nagy, Mr. Buchanan's New Hampshire director. Battling for 0.4 Percent

Feb. 25: The South Dakota primary. Only four-tenths of 1 percent of the national convention delegates are at stake in the race, but it is the last chance for candidates to score an early win before the main primary season begins. March 3: Several states hold primaries or caucuses, including Colorado, Maryland and Minnesota. Georgia is expected to join them, providing the first test of Southern strength. March 10: "Super Tuesday." The campaign heads south in a big way, with voters in a dozen states, including Florida, Texas, Louisiana and Mississippi, going to the polls. It is expected to be a good day for Mr. Clinton; if it is not, it could prove ruinous for him. March 17: The campaign moves to the Rust Belt with primaries in Illinois and Michigan. These are important contests for the Democratic Party, whose strategy for winning the Presidency depends heavily on carrying major industrialized states. If the field has not already been pared to two dominant contenders at this point, most analysts expect these races to do it. When the votes are counted, half of the pledged delegates to the Democratic convention will have been chosen. April 7: The New York and Wisconsin primaries. When the polls close, more than 60 percent of the delegates will have been chosen; most politicians expect the likely nominee to have emerged by this point. "Everything," Mr. Morris said, "is going to collapse in April." June 2: The last five primaries, including those of California and New Jersey. If the nominee has not yet been chosen he will be by the end of this day, or the race will remain deadlocked going into the convention. July 13-16: Democratic convention, New York City. Aug. 17-20: Republican convention, Houston.

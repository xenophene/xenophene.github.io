And We Call Ourselves Democrats?

By Larry Agran
Published: January 21, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

Last summer, before I announced my candidacy for the Democratic Presidential nomination, it never occurred to me to worry whether the chairman of the Democratic National Committee in Washington or network executives in New York would like my message. Instead, I worried whether millions of Americans would respond to a campaign calling for immediate measures to cut the military budget in half and use the funds to rebuild our country.

Fortunately, they did. After several of my appearances on national television, positive reactions came in from every part of the country. It seemed that a former mayor -- a liberal Democrat, from conservative Orange County, Calif., no less -- could run for President. This long-shot possibility was confirmed when my name appeared in several early polls, including a Gallup Poll in September.

Then, just as the door seemed to be opening for our campaign, it started to swing in the other direction. That door was locked on Sunday night as I stood outside with 400 pro-democracy picketers in sub-zero weather while the "official" Democratic candidates debated inside a warm New Hampshire television studio.

Last fall, Ron Brown, chairman of the Democratic National Committee, and his operatives began to pass the word to state party chairmen that there were just six "major" candidates and that I wasn't one of them. Taking their cues from the committee, the national news media fell into line.

In New Hampshire, Chris Spirou, the state's party chairman, followed Mr. Brown's script. Calling me a "minor" candidate, he shut off my microphone while I was speaking at a state Democratic convention in November. Then he refused to invite me to a health-care forum for candidates in Nashua last month. When I went to the event anyway, stood up and asked to be included, he and the forum's moderator, Senator Jay Rockefeller, Democrat of West Virginia, would have had me dragged off by security guards had not the audience successfully insisted I be included.

I have little reason to believe that Mr. Spirou, with the acquiescence of the other major candidates, will include me in any further New Hampshire events. Paul Tully, the national committee political director, told me that he could do nothing to improve the situation: The Democratic Party, he said, is a "ramshackle operation."

While this story seems to hold little interest for the national media, New Hampshire newspapers have come to my defense. Noting the seriousness and reach of my campaign they've concluded that by any reasonable standard I should be included in campaign events leading up to the Feb. 18 primary. I'm already on the ballot in 27 states and, in some cases, have slated more delegates than other major candidates.

The self-appointed gatekeepers should listen. Once Presidential candidates have qualified for the ballot in at least 15 states -- something only well-organized candidates can do -- the voters should have a chance to hear from them in nationally televised debates. If this principle had been observed, I would have been on stage at the debate sponsored by NBC last month. Instead, Michael Gartner, the president of NBC news, told me that, based on "news judgment," only the six (now five, with the exit of Gov. L. Douglas Wilder of Virginia) were worthy of participation.

Important nationally televised debates will take place on Jan. 31 and Feb. 16. I hope that in their invitations to the candidates and in their choice of format, the producers -- the MacNeil/Lehrer News-Hour and the League of Women Voters -- will demonstrate their commitment to an open process that can do nothing but enrich our democracy.

As events half a world away remind us, when the forces of democracy are unleashed, surprising, even wondrous, things can happen. Just look at another democrat and former mayor, Boris Yeltsin, who is now President of his country.

Larry Agran, a lawyer, was mayor of Irvine, Calif., for six years.

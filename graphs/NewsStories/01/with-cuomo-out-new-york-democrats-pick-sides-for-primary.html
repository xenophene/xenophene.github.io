With Cuomo Out, New York Democrats Pick Sides for Primary

By SAM ROBERTS
Published: January 11, 1992

Correction Appended

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

The post-Cuomo era of Presidential politics in New York began in earnest yesterday, as hundreds of Democrats lined up behind one or another of the five remaining candidates running in the state's April 7 primary.

Three weeks after Mr. Cuomo's withdrawal and only four days after a favorite son candidacy was ruled out, Democrats freed from any political obligation to the Governor met the 5 P.M. filing deadline to qualify to run to be elected convention delegates in New York's April 7 presidential primary.

Gov. Bill Clinton of Arkansas garnered the most organizational support so far from party and labor leaders -- including endorsements from the Democratic county chairmen of Brooklyn, Queens and Staten Island and a firm expression of support from their counterpart in the Bronx. Top officials of the teachers, public employee and hospital workers unions filed to run as Clinton delegates.

By that measure, Senator Tom Harkin of Iowa was second, also with some significant support from elected officials and labor -- including leaders of the steelworkers and communications workers.

Slates filed for Senator Bob Kerrey of Nebraska included a smattering of public and party officials.

John A. Marino, the Democratic Party state chairman, said yesterday that despair among many party leaders over Mr. Cuomo's decision seemed to be followed by a broad shift to Mr. Clinton. Still, he said, he detected "surprising support" for Mr. Harkin upstate. Wooing of Delegates

Assemblyman Herman D. Farrell Jr., who heads the fractured Manhattan Democratic organization, said that while "lots of folks are running to Clinton," he was leaning toward Mr. Harkin. But he said he would probably await the results of next month's New Hampshire primary before making his final decision.

Some would-be delegates had been urged by Cuomo diehards to run officially unpledged and to express their preference informally. But a spokesman for the Governor yesterday again disavowed any campaign on his behalf.

Prominent Democrats, who had either favored Mr. Cuomo or who viewed his candidacy as a cover to avoid having to choose one of the challengers, have been wooed aggressively since the Governor's declaration Dec. 20 that New York State's budget stalemate would prevent him from running.

Mr. Cuomo and Mayor David N. Dinkins of New York, who played a pivotal role in the Rev. Jesse Jackson's last presidential campaign, do not, at this point, intend to endorse a candidate before the New York primary. Nor, apparently, do most or all of the four Democratic candidates for United States Senate.

Former Gov. Jerry Brown of California and former Senator Paul Tsongas of Massachusetts are running what their staffs call grassroots campaigns with little organization so far in the state, suggested by Mr. Brown's failure and Mr. Tsongas's apparent difficulty in fielding the full complement of 159 delegates to be elected from congressional districts on April 7.

The rest of the 268-member delegation -- second largest after California's -- will be selected in May by the Democratic State Committee. The state's delegation is more than 10 percent of the number needed to win the nomination at the Democratic national convention in New York City next July.

Mr. Brown filed in every district, said his press secretary, Ileana Wachtel, but filed full slates in only about two dozen. Mr. Tsongas hopes to field complete slates in every district, but was still uncertain last night whether that had been accomplished, his spokeswoman, Peggy Connolly, said.

Harold Ickes, manager of Mr. Clinton's New York campaign, said, "We fully expect, absent any glitch, to have full slates in all 34 congressional districts."

Richard Socarides, Mr. Harkin's statewide coordinator, said, "Over 500 Democrats signed up to be Harkin delegates, giving us more than enough by almost three times to fill slates in every district."

And Mr. Kerrey's state campaign director, Jeremy Karpatkin, said, "We will file full slates." Petitions Next Test

Endorsement of a Presidential candidate by local Democratic organizations and by labor unions may not deliver pivotal votes in the primary, but could guarantee that would-be delegates, because they have the support of those organization, can collect sufficient petition signatures to qualify for the primary ballot. The petitioning period begins Wednesday and ends Feb. 13.

"The slating process is one more artificial test," said John Connorton, a New York lawyer and senior advisor to Mr. Kerrey.

Furthermore, the outcome of earlier party caucuses and primaries may recast alliances and even determine which candidates remain in the race by April 7.

"If Kerrey does well in New Hampshire," said Mr. Marino, the New York Democratic State Chairman, "all of this stuff is for play."

In statements submitted by yesterday to the Democratic State Committee, prospective delegates recruited by the campaigns or running individually either pledged to support one of the five remaining presidential candidates or said they intend to run uncommitted. Those who are pledged can still be vetoed by the presidential candidates.

In presidential primaries, New Yorkers and the rest of the nation have typically marched to different drummers. No recent winner of the New York Democratic primary went on to capture the party's nomination and the presidency, although Michael S. Dukakis's success in the state four years ago propelled him toward a first-ballot victory at the national convention.

In contrast to previous years, the belated 1992 campaign has not yet riven New Yorkers along the chasmic philosophical faults that traditionally divided Democrats from each other and, often, from the rest of the nation, too.

Moreover, for the first time since 1980, the primary ballot will not include delegates pledged to Mr. Jackson, who carried New York City four years ago.

"We're seeing a hunger for a candidate who can win," said Representative Thomas J. Manton, the Queens party leader.

New York Republicans, governed by their own political calendar, have already begun gathering petition signatures for President Bush.

"We have discouraged any attempt by anybody else," said William D. Powers, the party's state chairman. Nonetheless, supporters of the columnist Patrick J. Buchanan say they intend to challenge the President.

"We're going to try to get on the ballot in as many districts as we can," said Brooke Beyer, Mr. Buchanan's primary coordinator.

Marc Ellis, operations chief for David Duke, the Louisiana state legislator, said yesterday that legal obstacles had, indeed, discouraged the Duke campaign from fielding any delegates in New York.

"We think we can pick up delegates in a couple of districts, but David is leaning against it," Mr. Ellis said. "You have such Byzantine laws we'd need a battery of lawyers."

Correction: January 14, 1992, Tuesday A chart on Saturday listing politicians running as delegates in the New York Democratic presidential primary misidentified two of them. Joseph Ferris and May Newberger are both former members of the Assembly.

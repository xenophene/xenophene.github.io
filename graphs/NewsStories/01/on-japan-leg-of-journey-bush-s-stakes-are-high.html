On Japan Leg of Journey, Bush's Stakes Are High

By MICHAEL WINES
Published: January 07, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

President Bush arrived in Japan today for four days of politically freighted trade talks, hoping to cap a four-nation sweep of the Western Pacific with concessions from Tokyo that will appear, at least, to prop up America's slumping economy.

Mr. Bush brings his top advisers on the region, his commerce secretary and a platoon of 18 American corporate leaders with him to Osaka and Tokyo.

The Japanese Prime Minister, Kiichi Miyazawa, was said to be eager to give Mr. Bush some of what he seeks. The White House expressed confidence that the public will judge the visit a success.

"I think we feel pretty good that they're going to be responsive," the White House spokesman, Marlin Fitzwater, said of the Japanese. Jobs, Jobs, Politics

But the real stakes in Mr. Bush's visit are not the financial benefits to American companies, which appear comparatively small, but the political and public-relations gains or losses, which are potentially huge.

For weeks before he began his Western Pacific swing, the President cast his journey as a "relentless" personal quest to boost exports, and thus create more jobs for American companies mired in recession. Success would presumably help counter charges by Democratic rivals in the coming election that the White House has no domestic economic policy and no feel for the fiscal problems of the average person.

A good end to the Tokyo visit would also help defuse a challenge to Mr. Bush from the political right, where Patrick Buchanan, a Republican presidential candidate, has advocated an "America first" foreign policy that would retaliate against restrictive Japanese trade policies.

Mr. Bush has singled out Japanese trade barriers as his target, asserting that each $1 billion in American exports creates 20,000 new jobs at home.

Since Mr. Buchanan began his campaign, both he and Commerce Secretary Robert Mosbacher have broadly hinted that Japanese trade barriers are at least partly to blame for the American recession, which has erased nearly 2 million jobs since July 1990.

By the Administration's reckoning, the nation could add more than 800,000 jobs if it wiped out the trade deficit solely by selling more to Japan, rather than by cutting American purchase of Japanese imports.

The President has refused to say how many jobs he hopes to create or to set any explicit goals for the visit to Japan. "I can guarantee you political opponents, no matter what is achieved, will be saying, 'Hey, you didn't jump quite high enough; you need to get over the bar; we just raised it another foot,' " he said at a news conference on Monday. "What is important is that we handle this relationship with a broad global sense, that we make progress on the economic front." Hopes Exceed Expectations

Perhaps sensing that it had raised hopes too high, Mr. Bush's aides have sought to play down public expectations. In interviews, they have stressed that the United States' economic problems cannot be remedied in a single meeting and that the answer to the trade deficit lies not just in persuading Tokyo to buy American goods, but in improving American quality and productivity.

After landing this morning, Mr. Bush flew by helicopter to nearby Kyoto, Japan's former capital, to tour the old Imperial Palace and to lunch with an old friend, former Prime Minister Toshiki Kaifu, who was ousted by his own party in the fall.

Mr. Bush also engaged several men at the palace in a round of kemari, a 1,000-year-old game in which a stuffed ball is kicked from player to player.

The President's major activity today is a visit to a Toys "R" Us store near Osaka. The Administration is highlighting the store's success in entering the Japanese market under an agreement that lifted restrictions that favored "mom and pop" stores and prevented larger American chains from gaining a foothold in Japan.

Mr. Bush plans three meetings with his business delegation and Japanese business leaders during his stay.

Mr. Bush enjoys widespread support in his mission to Tokyo from allies in the Western Pacific, including all three nations he has visited in the last eight days. Leaders of those nations, some of whom recall Japan's sometimes brutal occupation of their homelands during World War II, now are openly fearful that they will soon be dominated not militarily, but by Japanese investment in their economies.

South Korea's president, Roh Tae Woo, was said to have discussed Japan with Mr. Bush at a private dinner on Sunday, a South Korean official said. South Korea runs an annual trade deficit with Japan totaling $8 billion.

That official, who refused to be named, said that Japan's refusal to apologize for its aggressive acts in World War II, as the Germans did, have denied it "moral strength" in the Pacific. Without another significant military and economic presence in the area, Mr. Roh is said to have told Mr. Bush, "it is bad news for all of us."

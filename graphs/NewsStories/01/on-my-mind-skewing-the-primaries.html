On My Mind; Skewing the Primaries

By A. M. ROSENTHAL
Published: January 17, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

What the press giveth we taketh away. Sometimes we do it so rapidly that the recipient hasn't had time to unwrap the gift before, zip, he is staring at empty hands.

Giveth: On the air and in print, the coverage of the Democratic candidates for the Presidency has been full, well planned and detailed. Taketh: Before a vote is cast, everybody "knows" the victor.

Quick, who is the front-runner in the field of five?

Right -- Gov. Bill Clinton of Arkansas. How do you know? Because we told you so, that's how -- columnists, editorial writers, reporters, anchors.

Joe Klein, one of my favorite political reporters outside lovely Fit-To-Print, writes in New York magazine that the Democratic Party "seems to be moving -- quietly, inexorably, tectonically" -- toward Governor Clinton. Journalists use "seems" when we want to deliver a clear message but leave an escape hole in case God or Mario Cuomo has a mind-change. But tectonic is tectonic.

How do we know? Why, somebody told us so -- other journalists, politicians, advertising men dressed up as political consultants for the election season -- and of course, pre-primary polls. But every journalist knows they are the most unreliable of all political polling operations. After almost every primary, reporters and editors tell each other they will never trust those damned things again.

Now, I admire Governor Clinton and what he is saying. But journalistic emphasis on the front-runner at this stage skews the election process. Constant repetition of an unverifiable statement is not news. It is propaganda, intended or not.

The other day Senator Tom Harkin of Iowa and Senator Bob Kerrey of Nebraska came to New York -- to hunt where the ducks are, as Tom Wicker puts it. In a blinding intellectual flash, I saw the real cost to the other candidates and to the voters of naming the winner in advance. We listen to the others with one ear, at best. Why pay real attention since they have already been counted out?

The idea that I would find either of these men politically interesting had not fought its way through my preconceptions of them. Unlike Governor Clinton, both had opposed President Bush's courageous decision to go to war in the Persian Gulf. So if either had been President, Saddam Hussein would have had his nuclear weapons by now, or tomorrow morning.

Mr. Bush's historic error in allowing Saddam Hussein to remain in power does not change that reality. But I officially concede that it should reduce the indignation level about those who were on the other side -- not the nasties of the left like those who still refer to U.S. soldiers as "Panzer" troops but Americans who thought it through as lovers, not haters, of the U.S. and could not go along.

I found Senator Harkin imprecise about some of his answers and I have a feeling that his agricultural-centered populism would not be great for city people. But I found he was far more sophisticated and flexible than I had believed. He failed, miserably, two of the basic litmus tests of the ugly left -- antagonism to Israel and viciousness about all who take a different viewpoint on any subject.

I voted for President Bush and once for President Reagan. But I guess I have always been a bit homesick for that old Democratic razzmatazz -- hearty thwacking of Republicans, calling on Harry Truman and F.D.R. as witnesses and presumably enthusiastic absentee supporters, and all that. In my mind's ear, I could hear "Happy Days Are Here Again" when Senator Harkin spoke at a West Side union hall. I tell you it set my feet tapping. So shoot me.

As for Senator Kerrey, the man has a problem. He suffers from precision, concentration of thought, and courtesy. I forgive him, however, for that and for disturbing my stereotypes.

Right now I do not know whom I would choose if I were to vote in a primary or what lever I will pull on Election Day. Depends. All I am saying is that the Democrats have put forward not dwarfs, not a faceless five, but a good strong selection of men who, as they should, represent different ideas and different traditions in the party.

Nothing on earth will prevent us in the press from keeping on telling you, probably correctly, who the front-runner is. But nothing can prevent a voter from saying: Shove it in your ear; I am going to pay attention to all the candidates, each and every one.

Reporter's Notebook; Uncommon Effort Aids Quayle's Common Touch

By ANDREW ROSENTHAL
Published: January 10, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

He ate milk and cookies by the fire. He dropped his suit coat for a green high school varsity jacket. He ate breakfast at a diner. He shook hands with what seemed like every schoolchild between Littleton and Laconia.

Just in case anyone missed the point after two days of trying to make it, he even dropped into a restaurant called The Common Man this afternoon.

In more than three dozen events in 13 towns, Vice President Dan Quayle has offered what might almost have been a conservative Republican's version of a populist message: He said the poor as well as the rich should be given their choice of schools and railed against both the "stupidity" of banks that made bad loans in the 1980's and what he called the timidity of bankers who now put their money in Treasury bonds instead of business loans and mortgages.

For Mr. Quayle, the common touch often seems as elusive as it is for his boss, President Bush, but Mr. Quayle tried hard for it nevertheless on Wednesday and today as he campaigned through snowy mountain country and covered-bridge small towns in New Hampshire.

Do candidates believe they can convince the voters that politicians are just like them? Probably not, but the need to attain that aura of just-plain-folksiness seems to be an unwritten law of campaigning.

Especially when you are campaigning in a state like New Hampshire, which holds the first Presidential primary every four years and expects its candidates to visit them personally. And especially when you are under fire for being too aloof in the first place from the problems of everyday Americans.

So Mr. Quayle stayed overnight at Doc Hill's house in Littleton, telling audiences the next day how he sipped milk and nibbled chocolate chip cookies by the fire. When he appeared on the NBC program "Today" this morning, there he was in the Hill kitchen, a spotless and seemingly empty cup of coffee at his elbow and the good doctor and his wife behind him at another table, eating breakfast.

Mr. Quayle ate his breakfast at the Littleton Diner and he dropped in to places during the day like the Town Hall-cum-theater-cum-basketball court in Lisbon, where the town's entire school-age population screamed and squealed for him like he was a rock star.

In Woodsville, Mr. Quayle put on a team jacket presented by the high school student body president and waved a varsity letter over his head.

And in Ashland, he posed for cameras outside The Common Man, a restaurant whose fare is more nouvelle than homespun.

The theatricality of these touches does not seem to bother Mr. Quayle or his audiences. For Steven Huntington, an unemployed builder who "out of curiosity" attended a Littleton Elks Club breakfast at which Mr. Quayle spoke this morning, the common touches are all just part of politics.

"I'm not sure it accomplishes a heck of a lot, but people at least feel they have some contact with the powers that be," Mr. Huntington said as he patted his 2-week-old daughter, Eliza, as she slept against his shoulder.

Mr. Quayle spied the child as soon as he walked into the room and walked straight over. He did not kiss her. But he put his finger into her tiny fist and then 15 minutes later mentioned her by name as he spoke of the importance of education.

Mr. Huntington, a lifelong Democrat, laughed good-naturedly. "Oh, yeah," he said. "I'm voting for him now. He's locked up my vote." On Liability and Credit

While Mr. Quayle and Mr. Bush like to say they are aware that the President bears the responsibility when things go wrong (the economy, for instance), they are zealously looking for ways to avoid taking the blame.

For months, the Republican Administration has been blaming the Democratic Congress for not passing the President's legislation. But even Bush campaign advisers have had to admit that is not the best way to approach the re-election debate, and they have been casting about for new methods of framing the "it's not our fault" argument.

Confronted by residents of a state whose construction and real estate industries are, as Mr. Quayle put it today, "in the tank," the Vice President tried blaming the banks. He acknowledged that both private irresponsibility and Government inaction had led to the savings-and-loan crisis, and that some discretion was a good thing in making loans.

But Mr. Quayle said in several campaign appearances today that the time has come for banks to resume lending to home buyers and small-business men, and he insisted there was little the Government could do to compel them in that direction.

"How are you going to regulate stupidity?" he said. "How are you going to regulate mistakes? How are you going to regulate bad judgment?"

Mr. Quayle added: "Now they are going the other way, saying: 'Boy, let's not get risky here. Take the safe way. Take the safe course.' But we're saying, risk is part of the American way. Let's get out and do business." Not All Congress's Fault

Mr. Quayle concentrated in New Hampshire on countering the challenge to Mr. Bush from a Republican conservative, Patrick J. Buchanan. But he did not entirely forget about Democrats, and he did not entirely forget about Congress.

"I'm not going to say it's all Congress's fault," he said in Woodsville. "Most of it is."

Still trying to counter the notion that Mr. Bush is too engaged in foreign policy and not enough in domestic affairs, Mr. Quayle turned on Congress to explain what he said was the gap between the President's diplomatic prowess and his "frustration in domestic policy."

"In foreign affairs, the President decides what is in the best interests of this country, and he does it," Mr. Quayle said. He added: "He decided it was in the best interest of the country to get Saddam Hussein out of Kuwait. He didn't have to ask Congress when to begin the air war. He didn't have to ask Congress when to begin the ground war."

But Mr. Quayle said, "In domestic policy he needs the total cooperation of the Congress." And Mr. Quayle noted, of course, that Congress is controlled by Democrats.

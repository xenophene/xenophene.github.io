THE 1992 CAMPAIGN: Democrats; Democrats Anxiously Monitor Clinton's Strength

By ROBIN TONER
Published: January 31, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

These are jittery days for the Democrats, as they monitor the vital signs of Gov. Bill Clinton's campaign and watch the horizon for signs of a new candidate.

One Democrat on Capitol Hill described it as "CNN emotions," the unending ripples of political anxiety in the party since a supermarket tabloid leveled accusations that Mr. Clinton conducted a 12-year affair with a cabaret singer.

Mr. Clinton's allies say he is through the worst of the "feeding frenzy," and his campaign asserts that the candidate is back on track after denying the singer's accusations.

But Democrats -- those sympathetic to Mr. Clinton and those who hope he stumbles -- are still trying to assess the damage, and almost inevitably some Democrats are wondering whether other candidates will enter the race.

Howard Gelt, the Democratic chairman of Colorado, said, "There's been a little bit of talk out here, just as there's been around the country: Where's Al Gore, where's Sam Nunn, where's Mario -- again. That's just a nervousness that exists."

Mr. Gelt says the Arkansas Governor seems to be bearing up under the furor, but many Democrats argue that the real verdict on his political strength will come in the New Hampshire primary on Feb. 18.

The Clinton campaign is trying to declare the crisis over. George Stephanopolous, deputy campaign manager for Mr. Clinton, said today: "Look at the facts. The fact is, at the height of the scandal, Clinton raised almost half a million dollars in three days and received public endorsements from a host of public officials, while the polls either held steady or rose in New Hampshire."

He added, "The public has indicated it really didn't pay much attention to trash-for-cash journalism, and wanted to get back to the real issues."

Spokesmen for the potential candidates most often speculated about, including Representative Richard A. Gephardt of Missouri, Senator Lloyd Bentsen of Texas, Senator Bill Bradley of New Jersey and Senator Al Gore of Tennessee, dismiss the notion that they are reconsidering a race.

In a typical response, Jack DeVore, a spokesman for Senator Bentsen, said, "He has declined, when asked, to join in any of the speculation." Mr. DeVore added, "He has consistently been getting a lot of calls, and yes, the number of calls has increased."

Gov. Mario M. Cuomo reiterated this week that New York State's budget difficulties continued to preclude him from joining the race.

Mr. Gephardt is a favorite of many party insiders, because of his experience in the 1988 campaign and his message of economic nationalism, considered particularly acute during a recession. But spokesmen say he is planning to run for re-election to the Congress this year, although in an interview on NBC's "Today" program this week he did not seem to close the door completely on a Presidential bid.

The other campaigns are moving to quash the notion that a late-entry strategy could work. The campaign of Senator Bob Kerrey of Nebraska recently prepared a memorandum that outlined how difficult it would be for a candidate to enter the race at this point.

"In light of the Clinton situation, a lot of people would probably want to reconsider whether they'd like to be candidates this year," said Tad Devine, campaign director for Mr. Kerrey. "But I think if they look at it seriously, they will find they're effectively foreclosed because they will not be able to satisfy the filing requirements in a large number of states." Missing the Deadlines

Any new candidate would face formidable hurdles: By the end of January, filing deadlines will have passed in 18 states, which produce 40 percent of the pledged delegates to the convention, according to the Democratic National Committee. By the time the New Hampshire primary is over, filing deadlines will have passed in 27 states, covering nearly 60 percent of the pledged delegates, those who are chosen on the basis of primary and caucus results and are committed to particular candidates.

Still, Thomas E. Donilon, who was a delegate hunter for both Walter F. Mondale in 1984 and President Jimmy Carter in 1980, said a late entry, while unlikely, was "certainly not impossible." And Ronald H. Brown, the chairman of the Democratic National Committee, said, "It's never too late, but I think that speculation is being grossly overstated."

In fact, all the talk may be only a sign of an unsettled race. Mr. Clinton has presented himself as the most "electable" candidate in the field, and many in his party, conservative and liberal, seemed to believe him. "What I hear from Democrats is down deep they hope he can survive this thing because they don't see anybody else who could be the nominee," said Natalie Davis, a member of the Democratic National Committee from Alabama, who described herself as uncommitted.

Just a few weeks ago, many of his supporters saw him on a painless glide path to an early nomination. Now, his rivals sense an opening, although they are careful not to seem to be capitalizing on his misfortune.

"My sense of Clinton's support was that it was powered very much by a perception here that he was the candidate best able to beat Bush," said Mr. Devine, interviewed by telephone in New Hampshire this week. "We'll have to see if that perception is damaged.

Tim Raftis, who heads the campaign of Senator Tom Harkin of Iowa, argues, "I sense people are rethinking his electability argument, and not just on that, but also on his record in Arkansas."

All of this heightens the importance of New Hampshire, which will render the first primary verdict on Mr. Clinton's handling of the furor, including his appearance on the CBS News program "60 Minutes" and his acknowledgement of past difficulties in his marriage. 'I Want to Win'

"This is all inane, stupid and insulting, and I hope the American people jam it down your-all's throat," Bob Slagle, the chairman of the Democratic Party in Texas, told reporters. "The national press corps is acting like a common street gossip in a small town."

Still, other Democrats are holding back. Representative Bob Wise of West Virginia, who said he was uncommitted but "very interested" in Mr. Clinton, said he wanted to wait a bit to see how well the Governor weathered the storm. "I want to win," Mr. Wise said. "I just don't want to have my heart broken one more time by somebody falling down."

Indeed, an important part of the party's psychology is the increasing conviction that Mr. Bush can be beaten -- if the Democrats rise to the moment. "The general attitude is, we can win it," said Mr. Brown, the party chairman. "I see Democrats all around the country, and the first thing they say to me is, we gotta win this thing. And if I say, who are you for, they say, 'I'm for the guy who can beat George Bush.' "

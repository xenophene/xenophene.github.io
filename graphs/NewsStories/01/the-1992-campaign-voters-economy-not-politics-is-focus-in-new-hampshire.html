THE 1992 CAMPAIGN: Voters; Economy, Not Politics, Is Focus in New Hampshire

By ELIZABETH KOLBERT
Published: January 14, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

The bulletin board by the entrance to Daniel's Restaurant and Pub displays the usual assortment of small-town business notices for house painting, massage therapy and firewood. Recently the offerings have been updated with a flier advertising a consumer seminar on "foreclosure, bankruptcy and alternatives."

That such a seminar could be useful is a given among the early evening patrons at Daniel's, much the same way falling real estate prices are now seen as normal. Touched briefly by the boom of the 1980's, Henniker is now settling back, albeit somewhat sullenly, into just getting by.

"It's not in vogue to be rich anymore," said Lorrie Russell, a mother of three. "It's in vogue to survive."

With the New Hampshire primary, the nation's first, just five weeks away, President Bush and his many rivals are trying hard to convince voters in this recession-wracked state that they have what it takes to lead the country through hard times. But such is the pessimism among voters here that they question not only the candidates' abilities, but even whether a solution exists.

"I think it's basically gone beyond the point of anybody solving it," Jim Brown, a supervisor at a local sawmill, said over a beer. "The way the country usually gets out of a recession is government spending, but with the debt so big we can't do that. I just see things getting worse and worse."

Tom Seavey, a self-employed carpenter, offered a similarly gloomy prognosis. "If a poor guy with a sensible brain got in the White House, it would take five generations to heal what has gone wrong," he said.

With his recent overseas trip, President Bush tried to export some of the blame for the country's economic ills to Japan. But the bar crowd in Henniker seemed to think the problem would more reasonably bear the label "Made in America."

Duane Bishop, a supervisor at the Henniker Hardwood and Pallet sawmill, suggested that the auto executives who traveled to Asia with the President ought to do some soul searching before they criticize the Japanese. "The auto industry doesn't have anybody to blame but themselves," he said. "You've got to look in the mirror when you make $4 million."

Mr. Brown agreed. "I don't understand how Bush could expect the Japanese to buy American cars," he said, noting that he himself drove a Volkswagen. Trying to 'Buy American'

Inspired by all the talk of Japan, several Henniker residents said, they had made an effort in recent months to "buy American." It turned out, they said, to be surprisingly difficult.

After her husband was unemployed for a few months last year, Mrs. Russell decided to buy only American-made goods at Christmas. The clothes were too expensive, she said. And the electronic goods simply were not as good.

"The sad truth is their technology is better," she said.

Melanie DeWit, a real estate agent, said that she had gone out to purchase a stereo with the intention of buying one that was made in this country. "I went into several places and I couldn't find any American product to buy," she said. "So I bought Japanese." The Boom Is Gone

Henniker, population 4,151, is typical of the white clapboard towns of New Hampshire that flourished as suburbs spread in the 1980's. To escape higher real estate prices to the south, commuters from Concord, Manchester and Massachusetts built houses in town, supporting a small army of builders.

Now the town is reeling from job losses in distant cities. The commuters are losing their jobs, the building has stopped, and the houses cannot be resold. The only steady work these days comes from New England College, a small liberal-arts college in town.

Looking back on the go-go years of the 80's, Henniker residents express a certain nostalgia for the time before the boom, when the town still counted cows among its residents.

"I think people have forgotten the simple life," said Daniel Carmichael, a telephone repairman, who was having a cup of coffee at the fountain of Henniker Pharmacy. "We used to have 10-party lines, five farms on each line," he said. "Now not only are the 10-party lines gone, so are the farms. The last one sold out last year."

As for the coming election, Mr. Carmichael said that he would probably not focus on it until the summer. When he did, he said, his main concern would be health care.

Mr. Carmichael said he and his wife had recently taken out supplemental health insurance to provide coverage if one of them ever needed long-term care. "It's almost to the point where you have to be working till the day you die," he said.

Although there is only a little more than a month left before the New Hampshire primary, most of the residents of Henniker said they did not know whom they would vote for, or if they would vote at all. There seemed to be a widespread opinion that President Bush had been a disappointment, and that the Democrats had not distinguished themselves.

"They're all stereotypical pols," Mr. Brown said. "None of them really impresses me much."

Lisa Rudolph-Cushman, a resident of nearby New Boston who was visiting Henniker, said she would probably vote for Gov. Bill Clinton of Arkansas in the Democratic primary. "But," she said, "I would have loved to write in Mario Cuomo."

Several people said they applauded Edmund G. Brown Jr., the former Governor of California, and his campaign against the wealthy interests in politics, but they doubted he could get elected.

"I think Jerry's coming from a great place," Mr. Seavey said. "Democrats, Republicans, it's all become a gigantic, business-run thing. A lot of grown men are wasting huge amounts of time."

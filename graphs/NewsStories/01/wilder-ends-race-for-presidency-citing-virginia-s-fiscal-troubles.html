Wilder Ends Race for Presidency, Citing Virginia's Fiscal Troubles

By B. DRUMMOND AYRES Jr
Published: January 09, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

Unable to come up with a message or an organization that promised success in the 1992 Presidential race, Gov. L. Douglas Wilder of Virginia came up tonight with the biggest surprise of the youthful campaign: he pulled out of the contest.

Mr. Wilder, the first black ever elected governor of an American state, said he did not have the time to seek the Democratic nomination for President and also to run the state of Virginia, which faces its worst budget crisis in half a century because of the current recession.

"Long before I announced for President," Mr. Wilder said in Richmond in his annual State of the Commonwealth address to the General Assembly, "I said that if it became too difficult for me to govern the Commonwealth and conduct a Presidential campaign, I would terminate one endeavor. I was left with a choice: either to devote all of my energies to delivering the message or to guiding Virginia through these difficult times. I have chosen the latter."

His explanation for getting out of the race was essentially the same that another Governor, Mario M. Cuomo of New York, recently gave when he announced that he would not enter the race. But unlike Mr. Wilder, Mr. Cuomo enjoyed universally high poll ratings, the promise of ample campaign financing and the prospect that with the help of numerous political allies he could put together a smoothly functioning campaign organization.

Mr. Wilder's announcement caught all but his closest advisers by surprise and injected a new element of suspense into the race for the Democratic nomination.

Mr. Wilder, a grandson of slaves who was born in poverty in Richmond and then struggled to become not only Governor but also a trial lawyer and a millionaire, had been angling for a major share of the nation's black vote. Now that vote, about one of every five Democrats, is more than ever up for grabs by the remaining five major Democratic candidates.

Black voters play an important role both in the March 3 primary in Maryland and the March 7 primary in South Carolina, as well as in the flurry of primaries in Southern and border states on March 10, Super Tuesday. His Democratic rivals had never ceded the black vote to the 60-year-old Governor the way Democrats ceded it to the Rev. Jesse Jackson in 1988, but he was still thought to have considerable strength there. Help for Clinton

At first glance, Mr. Wilder's withdrawal could be a particular boon to Gov. Bill Clinton of Arkansas, the only Southerner among the remaining major candidates. Mr. Clinton is counting on a strong showing in the Super Tuesday primaries.

"I have known and respected Doug Wilder for years and I understand and respect his decision," Mr. Clinton said tonight in a statement released at a campaign stop in Oklahoma. "We haven't heard the last of Doug Wilder, and I look forward to the contributions he will continue to make this year and in the years ahead."

Still, other campaigns were eyeing the Wilder support. "We'll be moving aggressively to let Wilder supporters know that their support is welcome," said Lorraine Voles, press secretary to Senator Tom Harkin of Iowa.

Senator Bob Kerrey of Nebraska said: "No American can look at the personal and political courage of Virginia Gov. Doug Wilder without feeling a sense of pride for all that he has accomplished in his life."

The Wilder campaign was troubled almost from the day the Governor announced his candidacy on Sept. 13, standing on the steps of the Virginia Capitol. He said then that he was "the longest of long shots."

He had fairly high name recognition around the country, a factor that sometimes made him look good in polls. But there was no depth of support. Less Than $1 Million Raised

When questioned at length, prospective voters said they found the Wilder message lacking in substance, heavy on symbolism but short on detail about what the Governor intended to do about the flagging economy, soaring health care costs and other problems.

The latest Federal figures on campaign money raised by candidates showed Mr. Wilder trailing all major Democrats, with less than $1 million taken in, half of that in matching Government money. His campaign staff was little more than skeletal: seven paid workers in New Hampshire, the first primary state, and none in Maryland and South Carolina, where the next major tests will take place.

As late as 6 P.M. today, the Governor's staff was cranking out campaign schedules, and his advisers were laying plans to film television advertisements in New Hampshire. He was due to visit Baltimore on Thursday

"We're in it all the way, from New Hampshire on," Joe Trippi, the Wilder television advertising consultant, said in mid-afternoon when asked whether the Governor would make a major advertising push in New Hampshire.

In a statement released after the Governor's annual address to the General Assembly, in which he waited until the very end to discuss his Presidential plans, Mr. Wilder said he originally got into the 1992 race because "a job had to be done and a message spoken."

"I am concerned about the direction this country is headed and recognize the need for leadership," he continued. "I have the vision, experience and fortitude that is necessary to help reverse this dangerous trend and put this great nation of ours on the right track again."

Some polls, he noted, showed him doing well, particularly in Maryland and South Carolina. What he did not say was that the poll takers attributed much of his high standing in those states to name recognition.

Nor did Mr. Wilder mention in his speech to the General Assembly, some of whose members cheered his withdrawal, that polls in his home state showed his popularity steadily falling, to the point that his political future even there was more and more in doubt. Under state law, he cannot succeed himself as Governor, but he could run for another office, like a United States Senate seat.

In his campaign speeches, Mr. Wilder portrayed himself as "a fiscal conservative with a social conscience." Then he would point out that while other states were raising taxes in an effort to counter the national recession, he forced Virginia to make do by cutting its budget, not by imposing new taxes. Yet when pressed to say whether he was a conservative, a liberal or a moderate, he would insist he was none of them, which many political experts found difficult to comprehend.

In the 1989 Virginia Governor's race, Mr. Wilder had steered clear of race as an issue and had managed the considerable feat of putting together a winning coalition of blacks and moderate whites. He began his 1992 Presidential quest in the same manner but began to hammer upon the racial issue his campaign faltered. By today, the main thrust of his campaign was aimed at black voters.

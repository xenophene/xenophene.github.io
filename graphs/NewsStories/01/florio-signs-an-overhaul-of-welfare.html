Florio Signs An Overhaul Of Welfare

By WAYNE KING
Published: January 22, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

Gov. Jim Florio signed a legislative package today that mandates sweeping revisions in the welfare system, among other things making New Jersey the only state in the nation to deny additional payments to welfare mothers who have more children.

If they are approved by the Federal Government, the changes would remove any possible financial incentive for a welfare mother to have additional children. They would also encourage family stability by allowing welfare mothers to marry without losing their benefits and reward employment by allowing them to earn as much as 50 percent of their total welfare payment without having their benefits cut.

The New Jersey revisions have attracted national attention, even cropping up in the debate among Democratic Presidential candidates on Sunday.

Gov. Bill Clinton of Arkansas, the acknowledged Democratic front-runner, said, "I would not sign that bill." Instead, he said, he would spend more money on education for welfare mothers and "make sure they have child care." Model for Nation, Florio Says

"There is no point in hurting the mothers," he said during the debate. "What you want to do is liberate the kids."

Mr. Florio disagreed with the Arkansas Governor today, saying, "This ought to be a policy nationally."

In fact, the New Jersey package does encourage education and job training. It requires that those receiving welfare whose children are older than 2 and who do not have such other reasons as health problems must get job training or other education to maintain their benefits.

To underscore that aspect of the restructuring package, which has received little notice amid the furor over cutting off benefits for additional children, Mr. Florio signed the legislation at a job training and language education center at Pennsauken, a few blocks from where the Governor once lived in the adjacent city of Camden. System 'Entraps Our Children'

Mr. Florio alluded to his own education, which was cut short when he dropped out of school and joined the Navy. He completed high school by correspondence and obtained a diploma through the General Education Development test. He later graduated magna cum laude from Trenton State College in 1962 and earned a law degree at Rutgers University Law School in 1967.

Rather than liberating poor children, he said, the existing welfare system, which he called "corrupt" and "morally bankrupt," instead "entraps our children in a cradle-to-grave cycle of dependency."

"Why after 40 years," he asked, "do we have welfare dependency continuing unabated?"

The reasons, he said, were that the existing system did not encourage and reward independence, education and family stability, but worked to separate families by decreasing benefits for women who married and by providing more benefits, albeit meager, for bearing more children.

A woman on welfare now receives an additional $64 a month if she has another child.

The State Department of Human Services, which administers the welfare system, says it does not know how many women bear additional children while on welfare.

But Jaqueline Tencza, a department spokeswoman, said that a "snapshot" survey last year indicated that of about 116,000 adults then receiving Aid to Families with Dependent Children, about 20,000 said they had borne additional children while in the program. They were not asked how long they had been receiving aid, so no data were available on the length of the period in which the 20,000 births occurred.

In September 1991, 116,000 adults, most of them mothers, and 247,000 children were in the A.F.D.C. program in New Jersey.

They received a total of $431 million in direct grants: $161 million from the state, $216 million from the Federal Government and $53 million from the state's 21 counties.

The welfare restructuring package was written and shepherded through the State Legislature in the closing days of its lame-duck session this month by Assemblyman Wayne R. Bryant, a Democrat of Camden, then the majority leader.

"I've been working on the welfare system and its negative impact on children for 10 years," he said. "And for those people who say this punishes children, I say, where were you for the past 40 years?"

Mr. Bryant said that Bush Administration officials listened with interest to his proposals when he visited the White House in November.

He said he expected that a request for waivers of Federal regulations to allow his program to be carried out would be sent to Washington within a month and that a response would be in hand by March or April, in time for the revisions to take effect July 1, the effective date of the law signed today.

A White House spokeswoman, Judy Smith, said that while the Administration "has long encouraged state-level reform in welfare," there had been no discussion of waivers and that the White House had no comment on the merits of the changes.

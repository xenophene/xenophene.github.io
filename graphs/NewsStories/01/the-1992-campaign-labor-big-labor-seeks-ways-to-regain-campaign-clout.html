THE 1992 CAMPAIGN: Labor; Big Labor Seeks Ways to Regain Campaign Clout

By RICHARD L. BERKE
Published: January 14, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

The major labor unions, seeking to recapture some of the influence they lost in the Presidential elections of the 1980's, are avoiding endorsements in the primaries and telling their state and local affiliates to pick their own candidates.

The unions, which have historically supported Democrats, are important not only because they influence members' votes, but also because they can deliver organizational help to their favored candidates: telephone banks, voter registration drives and campaign mailings.

In the past, major unions tended to make their choices early, but the strategy backfired in 1984, when they rallied behind former Vice President Walter F. Mondale long before the primaries, then watched helplessly as he was attacked as beholden to labor.

Explaining the decision to allow the union's locals to get behind candidates of their choice, Lane Kirkland, president of the A.F.L.-C.I.O., said, "We cannot afford to sit on the sidelines while others choose the candidates and choose the issues."

Mr. Kirkland added that it would be a mistake for labor to make an endorsement on the national level without a consensus from the rank and file, saying, "Let's try unity for a change."

Keith Geiger, president of the National Education Association, said the organization had shifted its strategy from four years ago, when it endorsed Michael S. Dukakis early in the primary season. This time the union does not plan to endorse until the end of the primaries, allowing the locals to support their favored candidates.

"If the Democrats are going to have a chance," Mr. Geiger said, "a candidate has to show signs of starting fire before we get behind that candidate."

This year all the major Democratic candidates are considered pro-labor, said Geri D. Palast, political director for the Service Employees International Union, and unions are looking at who has the best prospects rather than who has done most for labor.

"People are being extraordinarily pragmatic," she said. "You have to make some judgments about what's going to sell, not only to your membership but to the country at large." Early Dividends for Clinton

The strategy of encouraging locals and affiliates to go their own way could pay early dividends for Gov. Bill Clinton of Arkansas and complicate matters for Senator Tom Harkin of Iowa, who is widely viewed as the closest ally of labor among the five major Democratic candidates.

Governor Clinton, who had never identified himself closely with labor but is now courting major unions, gained some backing last week from two white-collar unions: the American Federation of State, County and Municipal Employees and the American Federation of Teachers.

The signs of support for Mr. Clinton are notable because he is a former chairman of the Democratic Leadership Council, a group of moderate-to-conservative Democrats that has sought to steer the party away from close identification with unions.

Henry Nicholas, president of the Philadelphia local of the National Union of Hospital and Health Care Workers, which is affiliated with the A.F.S.C.M.E., said: "We believe that we need to be about winning in 1992, and our executive board has voted to support the candidacy of Bill Clinton. If we went for Harkin we probably could get 90 percent of our agenda. If we went for Clinton we probably could get 85 percent of our agenda. But it's Clinton who, in my opinion, can get us to the White House."

Rachelle Horowitz, political director of the teachers' federation, said, "As we go around the country in the early states, what I see is a plurality of those in our union who are filing as delegates to Bill Clinton, who has been very strong on education reform."

But Senator Harkin's opponents are not discounting his labor support, particularly from blue-collar unions like the United Mine Workers of America. In its endorsement of Mr. Harkin last week another blue-collar union, the International Brotherhood of Electrical Workers local in New Hampshire, said: "Harkin takes the same tough, pro-working stands that the great Democratic Presidents like Roosevelt, Truman and Kennedy did. And he's the only candidate who does."

In an interview, Mr. Harkin disputed the notion that Mr. Clinton was gaining momentum and emphasized that the big unions had not made endorsements on a national level. "I've never said that every union is going to support me," he said. But he also said he hoped the big unions would endorse him, and added, "I wish they'd hurry up."

Although the locals are operating independently, it is quite possible that the international unions will endorse a candidate if one emerges as a convincing front-runner after the early primaries. At its meeting in Florida next month the A.F.L.-C.I.O. may consider whether to back a candidate, but will do so only if two-thirds of its governing board reaches a consensus.

John Perkins, national director of the federation's Committee on Political Education, says it can be most influential if it gets behind one candidate in the primaries. "If the A.F.L.-C.I.O. endorsed, you could well see a full-court press, with telephone banks and get-out-the-vote efforts," he said.

But Ann F. Lewis, a Democratic political consultant, says the 1984 campaign is a reminder that candidates are not always helped by a united labor front. "The larger labor looks," she said, "the more it's seen as a target."

For now, the labor federation's state and local operations are free to support any candidate. Its official guidelines urge locals to "use their own methods of determining the views and preferences of their members in a manner that leads to a bottom-up rather than a top-down decision."

The more passive posture by unions first became clear in the 1988 race, when the international unions stayed neutral in the primaries and waited until the general election to support the Democratic nominee, Mr. Dukakis.

This year the local unions are expected to wage more significant efforts on their own to help their favored candidates. This is particularly important because the individual Democratic campaigns got off to a much slower start than in 1988 and have far less developed organizations, so they could most benefit from organizational help.

Besides waiting to see whether a consensus candidate emerges, some unions do not want to endorse right away for fear that they will offend those they do not support. In addition to Mr. Harkin, Senator Bob Kerrey of Nebraska is considered a friend of labor in Congress.

In New Hampshire last week, Mr. Kerrey made no secret of his irritation over the fact that some officials in the A.F.S.C.M.E. were moving toward Mr. Clinton. If unions plan to support only the candidate with the best chance of winning, the Senator told reporters, by that logic "they may as well just endorse George Bush."

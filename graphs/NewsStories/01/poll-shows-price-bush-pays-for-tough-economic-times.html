Poll Shows Price Bush Pays For Tough Economic Times

By ROBIN TONER
Published: January 10, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

Eight of 10 Americans say the economy is in bad shape, and President Bush is paying a clear political price for their discontent, according to the latest New York Times/CBS News Poll. But voters know little about the Democrats who would challenge him.

The telephone survey, conducted Monday through Wednesday, shows an unpredictable terrain at the start of this election year: The President's approval rating is down to 48 percent, and voters are evenly divided when offered a choice between re-electing Mr. Bush or replacing him with a unspecified Democrat. But the real Democratic field has barely penetrated their consciousness, according to the telephone survey of 1,376 adults.

Still, the poll reflects the recession's toll on Mr. Bush. Only 18 percent said Mr. Bush cared "a great deal" about the needs and problems of people like themselves, down from 28 percent last March. Thirty-five percent said they thought Mr. Bush did not care much about them, according to the poll, which has a margin of sampling error of plus or minus three percentage points. Skepticism Over Asian Trip

Mindful of the dangers of seeming insensitive in a recession, the White House promoted Mr. Bush's Asian trip as an attempt to produce jobs. But even before Mr. Bush left Japan yesterday with trade pledges denounced by American car makers as woefully inadequate, the public was skeptical:Fewer than a third of those surveyed said they thought Mr. Bush's recent meetings with Asian leaders would increase employment in the United States; 53 percent said they thought the trip was "mainly for show."

About two-thirds, 67 percent, said that Mr. Bush was spending too much time on foreign policy and not enough on domestic affairs. That was up from the 58 percent who said that last October, and the 36 percent who expressed that view the previous year.

Mr. Bush's overall approval rating dropped below 50 percent for the first time in The New York Times/CBS News Poll, a matter of some symbolic note. But at 48 percent it was statistically indistinguishable from the 51 percent registered in November. Moreover, Presidents with similar approval ratings have won re-election.

President Ronald Reagan's approval rating dipped to 41 percent in his first term, and he went on to win 49 states against former Vice President Walter F. Mondale, the Democratic nominee. Still, Mr. Reagan's popularity had stabilized in the mid-50 percent range as he entered his re-election year.

William J. Feltus, deputy campaign manager for communications for the Bush organization, said: "On economic measures, this poll is not appreciably different from others we've seen. And until the Democrat is better known, polls are not going to tell you much about what's going to happen in November."

Geoffrey Garin, a Democratic poll taker, acknowledged that the Democratic candidates were still little known, but added: "A year ago, people were saying it doesn't matter what the Democrats do because Bush is invincible. We're not having that conversation anymore."

The poll indicates that the economy is at the heart of Mr. Bush's problems, and underscores the political challenge facing him as he seeks to allay concerns in his State of the Union Message and beyond. Eighty-one percent now say the economy is either "very" or "fairly" bad, up from 74 percent who took that view in November. Nothing came close to the economy when people were asked to cite the most important problem facing the country.

Moreover, Mr. Bush is clearly being held accountable for the current state of affairs: Only 23 percent said they approved of the way he handled the economy, a level last recorded in the Administration of President Jimmy Carter. Fifty-two percent said the recession was something that a President "can do a lot" about. Attitudes Reflect Anxiety

Economic anxiety is directly linked to current attitudes toward the election: 41 percent of registered voters said that if the election were held today, they would probably vote for Mr. Bush, while 38 percent said they would probably vote for an unspecified Democrat. Those who said the economy was bad were more than twice as likely to prefer the Democratic candidate as were those who said the economy was good.

Still, the election is 11 months off, and these attitudes could change dramatically by then. Moreover, as Republican strategists note, poll takers are now offering people a choice between Mr. Bush and an idealized Democrat. The real Democratic candidates are still struggling to introduce themselves.

Only Gov. Bill Clinton of Arkansas and former Gov. Edmund G. Brown Jr. of California had support in the double-digits among likely Democratic primary voters nationwide. And while Mr. Brown is the best known Democrat in the field, his critics outnumber his admirers by almost two-to-one in his own party. Gains by Clinton

Mr. Clinton has clearly made some strides in the past few months: 14 percent of the self-described Democratic primary voters now say they would prefer him as the Democratic nominee, up from 6 percent in November. Mr. Brown was preferred by 13 percent; Senator Bob Kerrey of Nebraska by 7 percent, Senator Tom Harkin of Iowa by 3 percent and former Senator Paul E. Tsongas by 2 percent. Gov. L. Douglas Wilder of Virginia, who dropped out of the race Wednesday night, was the choice of 6 percent.

When all the respondents who said they were registered to vote -- Democrats, Republicans and independents -- were asked their views of the Democratic candidates, the cloudy image of the current field emerges. In the case of every candidate, the vast majority of the respondents said they were either undecided or did not know enough to have an opinion.

More than two-thirds of the respondents were interviewed before Mr. Bush's collapse from a stomach ailment in Japan, but those polled afterwards expressed views that were statistically indistinguishable from the earlier sample.

Those interviewed Wednesday night were asked whether they worried about whether Mr. Bush was healthy enough to serve effectively for a second term: Only 25 percent said they did feel that anxiety. Worrying About Quayle

Two-thirds of those interviewed Wednesday, however, said they would worry about whether Vice President Dan Quayle would be up to the job of President; this was about the same percentage who voiced those concerns soon after Mr. Bush's thyroid problem last May.

The latest poll indicates Mr. Bush remains the overwhelming favorite of Republicans nationally for renomination: 78 percent said they preferred Mr. Bush, 12 percent favored Patrick J. Buchanan, the conservative commentator, and 3 percent backed David Duke, the former Ku Klux Klan leader and unsuccessful gubernatorial candidate in Louisiana.

The margin of sampling error for smaller subgroups in the poll was larger than the 3 percentage points for the overall poll. For example, it was plus or minus five percentage points for either the Democratic primary voters or the Republican primary voters.

White House Memo; Doubt Is Spoiler in Bid to Be Vice Presidential

By ANDREW ROSENTHAL
Published: January 12, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

Vice President Dan Quayle went to New Hampshire this week to bury Patrick J. Buchanan, but found instead that the conservative challenger to President Bush came to the Vice President's defense after Mr. Bush collapsed in Japan.

"I've engaged in some repartee with Quayle," Mr. Buchanan told a New Hampshire radio audience, "but he's been a pretty good Vice President. He's knowledgeable. I don't think people should be unduly alarmed at the prospect that he would be President."

That Mr. Buchanan felt compelled to ride to Mr. Quayle's rescue was emblematic of the Vice President's roller coaster ride this week and his peculiar political position as he tries to dispel public doubts about his abilities, regenerated by Mr. Bush's illness, build the foundations of a conservative candidacy for the White House in 1996 and at the same time serve a pragmatic, non-ideological President who wants Mr. Quayle mainly to stay out of the way. His Image Is a Priority

At the beginning of the week, Mr. Quayle and his staff were barely able to conceal their glee at a gently written series in The Washington Post that they hoped would prompt at least Washington politicians and reporters to take Mr. Quayle seriously. And they were counting on a two-day campaign swing through New Hampshire to help Mr. Quayle's long-range effort to rehabilitate his image with the rest of the country.

But to the chagrin of Mr. Quayle and his advisers, who have spent three years trying to change the subject, Mr. Bush's upset stomach was enough to rivet attention on the question that Mr. Quayle cannot seem to escape.

Minutes after the Vice President stepped off Air Force Two in New Hampshire last Wednesday, a reporter asked him if he was ready to take over if something happened to Mr. Bush. The Vice President gave the only possible answer: "I'm ready."

But a Republican ally of Mr. Quayle said he still cringed when he heard the exchange. "It's really too bad that Quayle still has to say things like that," he said. "It's certainly not what he came to New Hampshire for."

Then, for two days, virtually every time Mr. Quayle spoke to New Hampshire residents, he began by reassuring them that Mr. Bush was all right after his collapse, that there was no cause for concern and that he was in touch with the Presidential party.

To the vast relief of Mr. Quayle's aides and allies, it was that appropriately Vice Presidential message that was played on the evening news, not his answer to the question that never seems to go away. But aides were vexed that Mr. Bush's illness had provided a distraction from the campaigning in New Hampshire and from the Post series.

By Wednesday night, the staff was turning down opportunities for Mr. Quayle to appear on the networks, an odd policy for a national candidate. But they went ahead with a long-planned interview on NBC's "Today" program and, predictably, much of the questioning was about Mr. Quayle's ability to be President.

The week's sharp contrasts only served to underscore the haunting sense that the Vice President's performance is still somehow judged less by whether he scored some success than by whether he managed to avoid stumbling for another day.

This clearly was not what Mr. Quayle had hoped for in a week that began with the Post series, which presented him as a man who rules his own destiny and whose political acumen had been underestimated. Mr. Quayle's staff was virtually invisible in the articles, a political triumph for a group of aides widely viewed in Washington as veterans of a three-year session of political handling.

Anxious not to appear too happy, Quayle aides took pains to find fault with parts of the series. But as one put it, "Some things may be negative and some things may be positive, but if everyone says it's complimentary, then it is complimentary."

Then there was the trip to New Hampshire, intended to showcase Mr. Quayle's campaigning skills as he prepared for the President's visit next week and to polish his reputation as the Administration's standard-carrier of the Republican right by taking on Mr. Buchanan, who is challenging Mr. Bush in the Feb. 18 primary.

"We get a lot of credit for just showing up, and we can carry a certain message," one Quayle aide said.

But a Republican strategist said: "At the end of the day, all that the Post series can do and all that all the good campaigning in the world can do is to lay the groundwork to have a shot in his acceptance speech at the convention this summer and in the debate with the Democratic Vice Presidential candidate to fully rehabilitate himself. Then maybe he can move on to establish himself as a candidate in his own right."

Mr. Quayle's pell-mell rush through New Hampshire attracted respectable crowds. Some turned out to support the 1992 ticket, but others came because he is not only a celebrity, but also someone who might produce something more piquant than the standard campaign fare.

There were relatively few hecklers along the way. "How does it feel to be the national joke?" shouted one spectator in Plymouth.

But Mr. Quayle's staff likes to point out that one or two nasty shouts is a far cry from his nightmarish campaign for the ticket in 1988.

Aides like to say the "real Dan Quayle" was the one who won Senate races in 1980 and 1986, not the tongue-tied candidate of 1988. That may be the Dan Quayle the public sees 90 percent of the time, but there are still flashes of the other one, who has yet to master the art of public speaking, especially when tired and confronted with an unexpected question.

In Laconia on Thursday, in the last of some three-dozen appearances, he was asked about the space program in the post-Soviet world. Mr. Quayle supervises the space program, but he groped for an answer.

"Think of all the things we rely upon in space today," he said. "Communications from (pause) Japan. Detection of (pause) potential ballistic missile attacks. Ballistic missiles are still there. Other nations do have ballistic missiles. How do you think we were able to detect some of the Scud missiles and things like that?

"Space. Reconnaissance. Weather. Communications. You name it. We (pause) use (pause) space (pause) a lot (pause) today. And we're going to continue."

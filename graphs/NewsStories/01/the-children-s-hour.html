The Children's Hour

Published: January 21, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

In truth, it was not an hour but two, during which five would-be Presidents debated and polished their personas Sunday night on C-SPAN. Under Cokie Roberts's courteous prodding, three of them -- Kerrey, Tsongas and Harkin -- were sharper than on a previous outing with Tom Brokaw. Two of them -- Brown and Clinton -- gave warmth to previously sketchy impressions. But for all five Democrats, it was the children's hour.

Maybe they're powered by a genuine desire to improve the lot of the most vulnerable of all Americans. Maybe they're moved by pragmatism. Either way, it's a hopeful change in politics that they, like President Bush, consider children high on the list of campaign issues.

In 1988 the candidates had to be pushed to pay attention to early childhood development. This time around, they have gotten the message on their own. No one can aspire to be this country's President in 1993 without paying proper attention to children.

Listen, for instance, to Senator Bob Kerrey of Nebraska: "I will fight for a national education policy . . . that focuses on early education."

Listen to former Gov. Edmund G. Brown Jr. of California: "When it comes to people at the bottom, that means let's provide a work opportunity, let's provide training, let's provide health care. Let's give them child care."

Listen to Gov. Bill Clinton of Arkansas talking about New Jersey's punitive new welfare reform law: "I would not sign that bill. . . . I would spend more money on education for these mothers . . . make sure they have child care. . . . There's no point in hurting the mothers. What you want to do is liberate the kids."

Listen to Senator Tom Harkin of Iowa: "We have to get [ to ] the kids early in life. . . . We need adequate funding for maternal and child health care programs, healthy start programs, nutrition programs so these kids are well fed, and, yes, fully funded Head Start."

Listen to former Senator Paul Tsongas: "I can tell you the Democratic President [ who ] takes office in 1993 will be the education President, and the American people can count on that."

And lastly, listen again to Governor Clinton, speaking about the emptiness the prosecutor in the Central Park jogger case found in the eyes of the youths taken in by the police: "We have got to find ways to reconnect these children to the life we want them to live."

Coincidentally, or perhaps not so coincidentally, word comes from the White House that President Bush will propose an increase of more than 23 percent in financing Head Start for preschool children. As yet, however, he has not fulfilled his 1988 promise to expand Head Start to reach all eligible 4-year-olds.

Promises, promises: this is the season. The test for Mr. Bush, or his Democratic successor, will come starting just a year from now. But something is already different, already better. Presidential candidates now feel obliged not merely to kiss babies but to make promises to children.

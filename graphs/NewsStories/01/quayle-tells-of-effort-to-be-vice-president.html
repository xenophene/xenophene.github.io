Quayle Tells of Effort to Be Vice President

AP
Published: January 06, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

Dan Quayle, as a little-known Senator from Indiana, reportedly conducted a quiet campaign in 1988 to persuade George Bush to make him the Republican Vice Presidential candidate.

"You don't run for Vice President," Mr. Quayle is quoted as saying in The Washington Post today. "But let me say that there're ways you can be put on the available chart."

In the first article in a weeklong profile of Mr. Quayle in The Post, the Vice President said that after Mr. Bush won the New Hampshire Republican primary in February 1988, he decided it was time to "see if you can fly." Over the next six months he gave more speeches on the Senate floor, issued more press releases, spoke often with Bush aides and spoke out in Republican caucuses.

The turning point came, The Post said, when Mr. Quayle helped Vice President Bush successfully press President Ronald Reagan to veto a $300 billion military bill that cut money for strategic weapons.

The article said Mr. Quayle's selection was "the happy result of months of subtle, even stealthy planning -- a quality not commonly associated with his name."

The article described Mr. Quayle as "a skillful player of the political game, with a competitive drive that has been underestimated repeatedly."

But it also cited a tendency in Mr. Quayle to skate on the surface of issues, and it said his close associates often question the Vice President's depth and power of concentration.

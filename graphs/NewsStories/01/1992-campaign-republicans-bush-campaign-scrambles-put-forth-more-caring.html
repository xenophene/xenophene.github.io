1992 Campaign: Republicans; Bush Campaign Scrambles to Put Forth a More Caring Candidate

By ROBIN TONER
Published: January 19, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

Racing to overcome a late start and an unexpected primary challenge, the Bush re-election forces have sent the President forth in a burst of appearances intended to show that he cares about the home front and understands the suffering of the recession.

The strategy seems intended to reap maximum political advantage from the State of the Union Message on Jan. 28, when the President will lay out his program for easing the problems he now so openly acknowledges. Indeed, some Republicans are already worrying that the White House has raised expectations for the State of the Union that it will be unable to meet.

In general, the Bush campaign still has a decidedly catch-up feel about it, reflecting a President whose poll ratings dropped with remarkable speed and whose plans presumed a far more leisurely stroll to re-election.

"It's a good team, but they're scrambling now," said Edward Rollins, who was campaign manager for President Ronald Reagan's re-election in 1984. "They've got a primary in five weeks that they didn't expect." The Buchanan Challenge

Bush strategists are essentially trying to retool the President, at a time when a New York Times/CBS News Poll conducted Jan. 6-8 showed that two-thirds of the American people say he spends too much time on foreign affairs, 80 percent say the economy is in bad shape, and fewer than a fifth say Mr. Bush cares "a great deal" about the problems of people like them. The White House hopes to show that the leadership qualities the President demonstrated during the war in the Persian Gulf, when his popularity soared to historic levels, can be brought to bear on domestic problems.

The immediate audience for "Message: I care," as Mr. Bush put it this week, are the voters of New Hampshire. The state's Feb. 18 primary carries a huge risk of embarrassment for the White House because of the aggressive challenge from Patrick J. Buchanan, the conservative commentator. In recent weeks, Mr. Bush and his surrogates on the campaign trail, like Vice President Dan Quayle, have tried to defuse the potential for a protest vote in New Hampshire by persuading its residents that their concerns have already been heard.

"The message for New Hampshire is not necessarily a universal one," said one Republican strategist, "but it is generally applicable: that he cares and understands, that he's going to do something about it, that he does have an economic growth plan and that part of the problem is Congress." Making Up for Lost Time

Last week was an example of the concerned, domestic George Bush. He told farmers in Kansas City that he would "fight for American jobs, everywhere." He told New Hampshire residents that he understood the economy was "in a free fall," although, "maybe I haven't conveyed it as well as I should have." He spoke of his concern with race relations and urban ills in Atlanta.

Even as they recast the Presidential message for an election year, Mr. Bush's campaign advisers are struggling to assemble an organization and make up for months of lost time. Even without a primary challenge, Mr. Bush waited a very long time before beginning to organize for his re-election, many Republicans said. "We were about six months ahead of them in '84," said Mr. Rollins.

Mary Matalin, the campaign political director, said: "The organizational side is clicking right along. We know where to go and what to do." Referring to her Democratic counterparts, she added, "These other guys are still updating their Rolodexes." Teeter-Skinner Team

At the heart of the Bush re-election effort are Robert M. Teeter, the campaign chairman and principal strategist, and Samuel K. Skinner, the White House chief of staff, both of whom have strong roots in Illinois politics and have known each other for years. In contrast to 1984, when James A. Baker 3d, the White House chief of staff, was widely seen as the dominant figure in the Reagan re-election drive, several Republicans close to the Bush campaign predicted that Mr. Skinner and Mr. Teeter would have a much more equal relationship.

Frederick V. Malek, with the title of campaign manager, will be the chief operating officer of the campaign, and Robert A. Mosbacher. holds the title of general campaign chairman. Mr. Mosbacher, who is leaving his position as Secretary of Commerce, is an old friend of Mr. Bush's and will function in a time-honored role in a Presidential campaign: "The guy who knows exactly how the President thinks, knows the President's friends, knows how the President will react in a situation -- the real peer," said one Republican strategist close to the campaign. 'Lacks a Common Touch'

Some Republicans continue to worry about the top command of the Bush campaign for two reasons: as many of them put it, there is no Jim Baker and there is no Lee Atwater. Mr. Baker, now the Secretary of State, was the clear and acknowledged decision-maker in the 1988 campaign, these Republicans say. Mr. Atwater, who died of a malignant brain tumor last year, was the master strategist with a feel for the South, for popular culture in general and for no-holds-barred political combat.

"I think the campaign lacks a common touch," said Craig Shirley, a conservative consultant.

In recent weeks, the campaign has taken on the first of what is expected to be an expanding team of media consultants: Don Sipple, who handled Governor Pete Wilson's 1990 campaign and has a special expertise on California; Mike Murphy, who was the consultant for Gov. John Engler's upset victory in Michigan, and Alex Castellanos, who has handled several races in the South, including Senator Jesse Helms's 1984 and 1990 re-election campaigns in North Carolina.

Mr. Helms's 1990 campaign produced one of the more talked about commercials in recent years, featuring a pair of white hands crumpling a rejection letter while the announcer intoned, "You needed that job and you were the best qualified, but it had to go to a minority because of a racial quota." The commercial proceeded to denounce the civil rights bill then pending in Congress. Veterans of '88 Campaign

The Bush campaign also features such veterans of the 1988 campaign as Ms. Matalin, the political director; Rich Bond, a senior adviser; Charles Black, also a senior adviser, and James P. Pinkerton, whose title is counselor but who is expected to work closely on message. Mr. Pinkerton did much of the research on Michael S. Dukakis, the Democratic nominee, which was used to such devastating effect in the 1988 campaign.

Noting the Presidential experience of most of the top operatives, Mr. Bond said: "We understand a couple of basic things about Presidential politics. First and foremost, we understand it's an elevator operation, and as quickly as you go up, you can just as quickly go down.

"A Democratic nominee is about to burst on the scene and be everything to everybody, and what our operation does is insure a proper picture of this nominee is conveyed to the American people."

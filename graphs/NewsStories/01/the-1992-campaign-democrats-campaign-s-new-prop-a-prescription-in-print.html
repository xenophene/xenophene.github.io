THE 1992 CAMPAIGN: Democrats; Campaign's New Prop: A Prescription in Print

By ELIZABETH KOLBERT
Published: January 25, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

Last Sunday, when the Democratic candidates met for a televised debate, former Senator Paul E. Tsongas brought a prop: a copy of his 86-page prescription for solving the country's problems.

"This is not rhetoric," he said at one point, waving the gray booklet in front of the camera. "This is a real economic plan."

What the gesture lacked in genuine drama it made up with its sober show of specificity. After getting a bad reputation during the last few campaigns, details -- or at least the idea of them -- are making a comeback in the 1992 Presidential election.

Indeed, so high a premium is being put on specifics that one of the hot new issues of the primary campaign is which candidates have published bound volumes of their plans, and which have not. A Three-Volume Set

So far, three of the five major Democratic candidates -- Mr. Tsongas of Massachusetts, Gov. Bill Clinton of Arkansas and Senator Tom Harkin of Iowa -- have published novella-length booklets. Mr. Tsongas's plan, "A Call to Economic Arms," was the first in the series and remains the lengthiest. Mr. Harkin's plan, "A Blueprint to Build a New America," which weighs in at a more modest 35 pages, was released just this week and appears in large part to be an effort to keep up with the competition.

The biggest build-up belongs to Mr. Clinton's booklet: "A Plan for America's Future." In a television commercial that is being broadcast in New Hampshire, Governor Clinton sits in front of an American flag and assures voters that they "deserve more than 30-second ads or vague promises." Then he urges them to visit their libraries to review his plan, or to call his office and receive a free copy.

"Campaigns have always had position papers," said Geoffrey Garin, a Democratic consultant. "But I don't think they've ever been marketed in quite the same way."

At least for the time being, Senator Bob Kerrey of Nebraska has declined to publish a special booklet. This decision is one that he seems increasingly compelled to justify.

"I don't believe this election is going to be won by presenting a flurry of position papers," he said in Sunday's debate.

Despite Senator Kerrey's efforts, however, the "plan gap" has become a point of contention between him and Governor Clinton.

"Our opponents are complaining that we shouldn't be measured by position papers," said Frank Greer, Mr. Clinton's media adviser. "Well, they may not think so, but the voters think so."

The Kerrey campaign, meanwhile, portrays the whole battle of the booklets as vaguely ridiculous. It has tried to recast the issue as one of comprehensive plans versus clear priorities.

"The Clinton people keep repeating like a new mantra that specificity is the new character issue," said Michael McCurry, an adviser to Senator Kerrey. "People do want specifics. But it's not quantity that counts, it's priorities."

Mr. Kerrey has made health care his first priority, and has introduced in the Senate a detailed bill to provide national health insurance. The campaign has no plans to publish a plan, Mr. McCurry said. But if it did, he said, "I guess we should come in higher than Harkin and lower than Tsongas; if we clocked in at 40 to 45 pages, that should be about right."

Political analysts attribute this year's proliferation of plans to the recession, and to the disillusionment it has produced with the glib nostrums of the last few elections. Studiously Vague

But even though the candidates advertise their plans as carefully detailed solutions to the country's ills, truly cynical voters may still find grounds for skepticism. While they are chock-full of statistics, when it comes to paying for their proposals, the plans are studiously vague.

Mr. Clinton, for example, advocates an increased tax exemption for families with children; he says the increase could be paid for by cutting the Federal bureaucracy but never mentions how much the plan will cost, or where he intends to cut. Similarly, Mr. Harkin asserts in his plan that he will provide health care to all Americans. But he never puts a price tag on the plan, and the only savings he mentions, cutting "bureaucratic waste, red tape and padded prices," are not quantified.

In the end, though, what seems to be more important than what is proposed in the plan is the idea that the plan exists. While all of the campaigns report that demand for their plans is brisk, it is unclear whether voters are actually reading the plans, or just collecting them, the way one might collect clippings on important but dull topics, to be perused at some unspecified time in the future.

For example, the reference librarian at the Bedford Public Library, Susan Rotch, had a copy of Governor Clinton's plan on her desk. She said she thought the plan was a "very good idea." But when asked whether she herself had read it, she shook her head regretfully.

"I know I should have," she said.

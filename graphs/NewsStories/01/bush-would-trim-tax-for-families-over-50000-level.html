BUSH WOULD TRIM TAX FOR FAMILIES OVER $50,000 LEVEL

By DAVID E. ROSENBAUM
Published: January 17, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

The income-tax cut President Bush plans to propose this month would make families with incomes of more than about $50,000 the biggest winners, drawing a new line in his election year battle with the Democrats over who can develop the more attractive tax breaks for the middle class.

The Democrats, in Congress and on the Presidential campaign trail, have not found a tax policy they can all support, but most of their proposals aim lower on the income ladder than the President's.

Senator George J. Mitchell of Maine, the Democratic leader, assailed the President's policies in a speech today. But pressed by reporters afterward, he declined to say what specific tax policy he would support. An Economic Stimulation

On the heels of Mr. Bush's campaign trip to New Hampshire on Wednesday, the first of the election year and where he tried to find a firmer footing on domestic and economic issues, new details began to emerge about the budget package he will send to Congress Jan. 29. For example, he plans to propose increasing the personal exemption for a family of four by a total of about $2,000, which would amount to a tax cut of up to $620.

Mr. Bush is expected to argue that a tax cut, which would be immediately reflected in withholding tables, would generate additional spending and stimulate the flagging economy. Democratic politicians tend to accept that view.

But most economists, regardless of their politics, say a tax reduction of $1 or $2 a day would do little to help the economy and would be an especially bad idea if it worsened the budget deficit.

Within the Administration, moderates like Richard G. Darman, the Director of the Office of Management and Budget, and Treasury Secretary Nicholas F. Brady seem to have won out over conservatives like Vice President Dan Quayle and Jack F. Kemp, Secretary of Housing and Urban Development, who want much deeper tax cuts and much more costly investment incentives.

"The pro-growth wing of the Administration is not very happy about the growth package," said an Administration official who considers himself part of the conservative wing. "It's anemic." The official said that bold proposals had been "rejected by the Treasury for green eyeshade reasons."

But another official said that Mr. Bush believed the conservatives had no real choice but to back him and that he was more concerned about losing the middle class to his Democratic challengers.

Budgets are always political documents, and that maxim is even more true in election years. For President Bush, the budget this year is crucial to the image he is trying to sell to the American people.

He clearly hopes to use the budget to make several points to voters: that he is sensitive to how squeezed middle-income Americans feel, that he has a well-formed policy in areas like health and education to which his Administration until now has paid relatively little attention and that he recognizes that the collapse of the Soviet Union will allow the United States to spend less on the military.

Democrats have harped on those issues on the campaign trail and in Washington, and the President feels the need to regain the offensive, his staff members say.

With that in mind, say Administration officials and their allies in Congress, the President will propose these new measures in his budget:

*A total increase of about $2,000 in the personal exemptions that a family of four could claim on their income tax return. That would mean a tax cut of $300 for families in the 15 percent tax bracket with a total income below about $50,000, a $560 reduction for those in the 28 percent bracket with an adjusted gross income between about $50,000 and about $100,000 and a $620 tax cut for families in the 31 percent bracket with incomes between about $100,000 and $158,000. Only families with incomes below about $15,000 and above about $280,000 would get no help at all.

*Refundable tax credits for poor people and deductions for the middle class to help offset the cost of medical insurance.

*A tax break of several thousand dollars for first-time homebuyers who buy any but the most expensive houses.

*An additional tax incentive for savings by middle-income taxpayers that would actually raise revenue for the Government in the first year but would cost money in the years ahead when today's budget rules would not apply.

*A change in depreciation rules to encourage businesses to invest in plants and equipment.

*Rules that would allow people actually in the real estate business to deduct their real estate losses from income earned from other businesses or investments.

*Cuts in military spending of about $30 billion beyond what was previously planned for the next five years.

The Democrats want much bigger reductions in military spending. But otherwise, except for their differences over tax policy, the Democrats differ with the President only in the details.

For example, Senator Mitchell, in his speech at the National Press Club today, advocated a tax credit for business investments, savings incentives and help for first-time home buyers. Mitchell Targets Military

But he called for a $100 billion cut in the military budget over the next five years, and he excoriated what he called the President's "trickle-down" tax policy.

Marlin Fitzwater, the White House press secretary, said today that the President's budget proposals "have essentially been decided upon and completed."

But other officials said three issues still were not settled: how exactly to calculate the increased personal exemption, how much detail to include about medical insurance and how extensively to specify the nature of military spending reductions beyond the next fiscal year that begins Oct. 1.

The officials said the personal exemption, now $2,300 per person, could be raised by about $500 a person, or it could be limited to children and raised by about $1,000 each. As a tax matter, personal exemptions are counted the same way as deductions. That means the higher one's tax bracket, the more the exemption is worth.

So an additional $2,000 worth of exemptions for a family of four would be worth less for a family in the 15 percent bracket (taxable incomes below $35,801, which means a total income before deductions of about $50,000) than it would for those in a 28 percent or 31 percent bracket.

Democrats tend to favor tax credits in the range of $300 to $450 for a family. A credit is subtracted directly from the taxes owed and is worth the same to all taxpayers.

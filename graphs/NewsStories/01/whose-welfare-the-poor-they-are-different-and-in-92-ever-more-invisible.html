Whose Welfare?; The Poor, They Are Different, and in '92, Ever More Invisible

By GWEN IFILL
Published: January 19, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

THE Democratic candidates for President, anxious to woo back the disaffected middle class, have all but abandoned their traditional role as champions of the disenfranchised.

It is almost an oversight, this eerie silence on the campaign trail about issues that affect the poor. But it is a telling omission for the party that had traditionally spoken for the disadvantaged, especially during a battle over whether states should cut entitlement programs to balance strained budgets.

In New Jersey, California, Michigan, Tennessee, Illinois, Massachusetts and Wisconsin, welfare programs have emerged as a popular cash cow for strapped treasuries. In some cases -- as in New Jersey -- the budget cuts have come attached to proposals designed to limit family size. In others, the reductions in general aid have slashed across the board.

But if these remarkable changes of direction have been the subject of heated debate in state capitals -- Jesse Jackson marched in Lansing, Mich., last week -- they have been largely ignored on the campaign trail.

Instead, wooing the middle class has become all the rage. They are angry and they vote. What Presidential candidate could resist?

None, apparently. The major candidates for President are campaigning during a protracted recession that pitched President Bush onto the political defensive as he made his first appearance in New Hampshire last week and has let the Democrats make their case.

But the candidates have built their campaigns around middle-class tax cuts, capital-gains tax cuts and universal health care programs designed to appeal to middle-class workers. Their consultants have searched out the code phrases that might turn off middle-class voters by suggesting that they favor welfare.

When Gov. Bill Clinton of Arkansas commiserates with New Hampshire voters about how the unemployment rolls there have been swollen by middle-class people, he gets approving nods and applause. When the Democratic contender is asked about the New Jersey welfare-cut proposal awaiting the signature of the Democratic Governor, Jim Florio, he equivocates, saying that he lacks specifics on the New Jersey plan. 'Compassion Fatigue'

Even Senator Tom Harkin, the most outspoken defender of traditional Democratic values in the field, has only addressed poverty as a campaign issue in what one of his advisers described as "a thematic sense." Like the other candidates, he talks about child care and child-support enforcement, said Lorraine Voles, a Harkin campaign spokeswoman. But such questions are discussed far more often in the context of what Ms. Voles called the "middle-class squeeze."

In their messages for 1992, the candidates seem to have scrubbed poverty from their agendas. If they talk about the poor at all, it is to promote ideas like earned income-tax credits. Dependency is not in vogue.

"Polls are showing these guys that people are feeling a compassion fatigue," said Robert Borosage, an analyst for the Institute for Policy Studies in Washington who advised Mr. Jackson in 1988. "They're tired of the homeless, tired of poor people. In some sense, it's a great retreat to the conservative agenda."

Take the word "needy." It sometimes seems to have been effectively redefined to mean those who are considered to be otherwise capable of earning $50,000 or more a year. Losing a Voice

"Nobody's speaking for poor people," said Kevin Gray, an adviser to Senator Harkin in South Carolina.

And the candidates' staffs as much as admit it.

Paul Tsongas' "definition of liberalism is expanding the pie," said Peggy Connolly of Mr. Tsongas' staff.

"It doesn't come up a lot," said Steve Jarding, a spokesman for Senator Bob Kerrey of Nebraska. "It is a campaign geared to a middle class pinched very hard in the 1980's. The haves got more and the have-nots got less, but the tax burden fell on the middle class."

It's a message that falls on receptive ears at campaign rallies, events that poor people may not have the motivation or the bus fare to attend. But, if anything, their plight is more desperate than it has been in years.

Last fall, the Census Bureau reported that the poverty rate rose sharply in 1990 and that declining incomes forced 2 million more people below the Federal poverty line -- $10,419 for a family of three. By the end of the year, the Center on Budget and Policy Priorities, a Washington policy research organization that specializes in poverty issues, reported that 40 states cut or froze benefits to families with children.

The five major Democratic contenders will have two opportunities to address such issues in Washington this week before the United States Conference of Mayors and Mr. Jackson's Rainbow Coalition.

It is an interesting dilemma for the Democrats, who are working assiduously to shed any Republican effort to tag them as tax-and-spend liberals. Michael S. Dukakis was never able to fully escape such a characterization.

The other problem is that, for ill or good, the problems of the poor have become inextricably bound up in the thorny question of race. David Duke, the former Ku Klux Klan Grand Wizard, garnered the majority of the white vote in the Louisiana governor's race by alluding frequently to undeserving welfare recipients.

During the last two Presidential elections, the Democrats were able to conveniently sidestep the whole matter by ceding the issue of the disadvantaged and the disenfranchised to Jesse Jackson.

"The unmentionable in all of this is race," said Mr. Borosage. "What this represents for Democrats is a kind of assumption that people think of poor people as African-American and Hispanic."

But, in a politically twisted way, there could be good news in the offing for the disenfranchised. Absent Mr. Jackson's overwhelmingly popular presence, white candidates will probably be more likely to lobby for black votes. And they will remain ever mindful that many blacks, too, are middle class.

And in the final analysis, candidates seem to realize that even many of the poor don't like to call themselves poor. "We all want to think we're middle class, even if we're not," said Andres Gonzalez, a former executive director of the Texas Mexican-American Democrats, which recently endorsed Governor Clinton.

It is not as if the candidates never come in contact with the needy. Last weekend, Bill Clinton toured a soup kitchen in Keene, N.H., while last Friday, Mr. Tsongas toured a Boston homeless shelter. But these often have the air of side trips.

Some advocates hope that poverty will return as a campaign issue when the campaigns leave the largely rural early primary states and travel to industrial ones.

"Frankly, it has more to do with the fact that the poor are not a powerful constituency," said Jennifer Vasiloff, the executive director of the Coalition on Human Needs, an antipoverty group based in Washington.

The candidates deny this. Helping the economy will help all income groups, they say. And the poor will reap their share of the benefit.

Mr. Borosage noted that organized labor and some other advocacy groups have already accepted this approach as pragmatic and the best way to win the general election. "A lot of the organized activists are prepared in fact to make that deal," he said. "But poor people aren't prepared to make that deal."

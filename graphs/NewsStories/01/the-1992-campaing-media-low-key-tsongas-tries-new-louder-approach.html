THE 1992 CAMPAING: Media; Low-Key Tsongas Tries New, Louder Approach

By KAREN DE WITT
Published: January 29, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

At first, Paul E. Tsongas hoped his message alone would carry him through in his drive for the Democratic Presidential nomination. Mr. Tsongas, the diffident former Senator from Massachusetts, refused to be packaged by consultants like some other candidates.

But after 10 months of campaigning and little evidence that his candidacy was taking off, he has discovered that some media savvy and aggressive television advertisements might sweeten the appeal of his tough prescription for economic recovery.

While it is not possible to show a cause and effect, Mr. Tsongas in recent weeks has strengthened his second- place standing behind Bill Clinton, the Governor of Arkansas, in several surveys of New Hampshire voters. In the aftermath of assertions about Mr. Clinton's personal life, one poll this week even showed him ahead.

But before he agreed to be coached in public speaking and to use message-oriented advertising, Mr. Tsongas had to overcome his own dislike of consultants and what he saw as President Reagan's reliance on them. "It's never been a matter of my confidence," Mr. Tsongas said. "But perception is a function of television personality. I had such a strong negative reaction to Reagan that I didn't want to be a made-over person. I had to accept the fact that this is important."

Mr. Tsongas said that in the first televised debate, held in December, he thought that he let his supporters down.

"My instinct was to defer to someone and to look over at them and away from the camera," said Mr. Tsongas. But before the last debate Jan. 19, Mr. Tsongas took voice lessons and got coaching tips about television from his cousin Joyce Tsongas, who is a speech coach.

Mr. Tsongas also said he was getting better at controlling his urge in speeching to grab paper and pencil and draw charts and graphs that no one in the back rows can see. And for the most part, he has stopped letting his voice drop to inaudibility at the end of sentences. He has also bought two new suits. Attacking an Image Problem

The make-over appeared to be working during a day's campaigning here.

Mr. Tsongas attacked his image problem head on, joking with a insurance workers in Concord about how central casting would never pick "another Greek from Massachusetts" like Michael S. Dukakis as a Democratic Presidential candidate. He mixed humor and his message of economic responsibility when he added, "Look at the Japanese: Who was the last charismatic prime minister?"

And in a meeting with a group of environmentalists in Concord, when one person asked Mr. Tsongas which of the competitors he would be willing to serve with as a running mate, he replied, "At some point, I will interview them all as my running mate." The audience laughed.

Tonight, as he watched President Bush's State of the Union Message at the home of Ken and Sandra Lord in Salem, Mr. Tsongas passed around a bowl of popcorn and said, "This will taste better than that," in a reference to Mr. Bush's speech.

Afterward, Mr. Tsongas termedthe President's message, which included several proposals meant to invigorate the economy, "voodoo economics continued." Seeing a Breakthrough

Mr. Tsongas said that his two main goals in the final weeks before the Feb. 18 primary here were improving his television presence and emphasizing the differences between his economic message and those of the other candidates.

Mr. Tsongas said that he felt the second debate, which was broadcast by a local television station and C-Span and seen by 90,000 New Hampshire adults, was a breakthrough for him and that voters were taking a second look at his candidacy.

Delmar (Doc) Anderson, the Democratic committeeman for Raymond who introduced Mr. Tsongas at a recent gathering, underscored that point when he told the audience they should focus on whether "you like what he says rather than thinking about whether he can be elected."

Mr. Anderson, a former Boston University professor who now raises goats, said of Mr. Tsongas: "He's got a dry sense of humor and I don't think he's going to be talked into riding a tank." Mr. Dukakis was derided in the last Presidential campaign as trying to show he was tough by riding a tank. Wishing for Two Votes

At the town meeting in Raymond, Laura Caron stood up and told Mr. Tsongas that she agreed with everything he had to say.

"But who is on the cover of the news magazines?" she said. "That's my concern for you."

Ms. Caron's question crystallizes Mr. Tsongas' political problem in this first primary state and beyond. Some Democrats here give Mr. Tsongas high marks for his tough economic message, but they just as often doubt whether the dour, low-key Massachusetts native with the plaintive voice and deferential stance can succeed in New Hampshire or beyond.

"People feel a really big responsibility in this primary," Mr. Anderson said. "A lot of people wish they had two votes, one for the person they think could be elected, the other for Tsongas as president."

After the meeting, Ms. Caron said, "What he's got to say isn't easy to swallow, but it makes sense." Mr. Tsongas offers an uncompromisingly tough message to voters. In his latest advertisement he stands outside a closed factory, and says: "All the Japan bashing in the world won't open this factory."

Mr. Tsongas differentiates himself from the other Democratic candidates who vow to get tough on what they describe as unfair trade practices. "Let's not kid ourselves," says Mr. Tsongas in the advertisement. "America must take control of its own fate."Mr. Tsongas promises to "shake things up" from the board room to the factory floor and beat the Japanese the "American way," by making "quality come first again."

Mr. Tsongas said upon his inauguration as President, he would overhaul what he says is an antiquated economic system. He would freeze Federal spending, and order regulators to halt banks from calling the loans of viable businesses and home owners.

Mr. Tsongas said he must finish first, second or a strong third in the New Hampshire primary, Feb. 18, if he is to remain in the race.

Mr. Tsongas's campaign manager, Dennis Kanin, said the campaign organization was set to take that same message to the nation, but that Mr. Tsongas would not be redesigning his message for other primary and caucus states.

"There is no reason to," said Mr. Kanin. "We think people are looking for straight answers and that is what Paul Tsongas is about."

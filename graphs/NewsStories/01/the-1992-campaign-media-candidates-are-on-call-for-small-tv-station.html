THE 1992 CAMPAIGN: Media; Candidates Are on Call for Small TV Station

By JOHN TIERNEY
Published: January 16, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

Jack Heath, the 31-year-old news director of a small television station here, did not have to hang up on anyone at the White House this week or threaten to cancel an interview with the President. He had used that tactic -- with great success -- before his interview in December, but this time the problems were resolved more smoothly.

The only real difficulty came on the eve of President Bush's arrival in New Hampshire, when the White House called to say that Mr. Bush would find time during today's visit to tape an interview with the station, WMUR-TV. "They said we'd be the only media to get an interview," Mr. Heath said Tuesday, but he was still not satisfied. He wanted the President on live at the start of the 6 o'clock news.

"They told me it was impossible because the President was scheduled to speak to a Rotary Club dinner starting at 5:55," Mr. Heath said. "I told them, 'Frankly, I don't care much about scheduling. That's your problem. All I know is we've got a newscast at 6 with an audience that's a lot bigger than the one at that dinner, and I'd like the President of the United States on live. We'll put him on at the top and give him 10 minutes.' They said they'd get back to me."

Later they did get back to him, which is why the Rotarians had to wait a few extra minutes to hear from Mr. Bush today. The President first stopped in a nearby room for a live interview during WMUR's newscast. He joked about his flu, talked about his concern for New Hampshire and demonstrated just how important a little station has become to any candidate in the Presidental primary.

It now rivals, and possibly surpasses, The Union Leader newspaper as the chief source of political news in the state. Its influence derives partly from its extensive political coverage but mainly from a rapid growth in its audience, which is now larger in New Hampshire than that of any evening newcast from the networks or the nearby stations in Boston.

"When it comes to television, this is it," Senator Bob Kerrey, the Democratic Presidential candidate from Nebraska, said on Tuesday night as he sat in the WMUR studio waiting for his interview on the evening news. At least one Presidential candidate usually appears live each evening, and the station has studios and cameras set up to handle three separately on the same newscast.

"The impact of the station is pretty stunning," said Mitchell Schwartz, the state director of the Democratic campaign of Gov. Bill Clinton of Arkansas and a veteran of two previous Presidential campaigns. "I don't know of any single station anywhere in the country that's so important politically."

WMUR, an ABC station, has long been the only network affiliate in New Hampshire, but for most of its history the newscasts attracted little attention from politicians -- or from the station management, for that matter. The news operation consisted of six people, Thomas J. Bonnar recalls, and it was not exactly in a position to compete with the Boston stations. Early One-Man Operation

"Back in the 1970's," said Mr. Bonnar, who is now WMUR's operations manager, "on a typical day I would go out with a camera, film two or three stories, come back and process the film, edit it, write the script and then anchor the show."

The station was taken over a decade ago by new management, and the news staff has grown to 33 people. The ratings have gone up dramatically, too. The station is quick to remind politicians -- and their advertising buyers -- of the latest Arbitron report, from November, showing that WMUR's 6 o'clock newscast reaches a third of the television audience in New Hampshire at that hour.

According to the report, the newscast is seen by slightly more than 100,000 adults in 65,000 households -- about one of every six homes in the state. Within New Hampshire that is larger than the audience for any network evening newscast, a little more than the combined audiences of all three local newscasts from the Boston stations, and roughly comparable to The Union Leader's circulation of 70,000 daily and 100,000 on Sundays.

The station's correspondents are relatively young and inexperienced -- some make less than $20,000 a year, not even half the going rate in a major market like Boston -- and they have to work without the field producers and audio technicians that are standard for the networks. But what the political coverage lacks in polish it makes up for in sheer volume. Often half the news time is taken up with campaign stories and interviews.

Unlike the fiercely partisan Union Leader, the station does not endorse any candidates in the primary, and the candidates seem generally satisfied with its even-handedness. What they especialy like are its audience and its ample supply of free broadcast time, and they have learned to pick out the station's microphone flag in the swarm of the national news media's cameras at a campaign event.

"We call it the mike magnet," said Susan Masley, a correspondent. "When I'm in a crowd and we put up our mike flag with Channel 9 on it, sometimes the candidate will come right over when he sees it. Or one of his staff will rush over and say, 'What do you need?' I'll say, 'I'd like a one-on-one,' and pretty soon the candidate is coming our way." Hanging Up on White House

The magnetism is going to cease on Feb. 18, the day of the primary, and its journalists insist that they are not letting their current popularity go to their heads. Mr. Heath says that he never intended to be arrogant with White House, not even when he hung up on someone there in December.

The phone call came just before a visit that Mr. Heath made to the White House in December. Although he had been promised free access to the President -- a personal interview as well as several chances to film the President at work during the day -- Mr. Heath said that he got frustrated when different officials at the White House kept calling him to confirm the arrangements.

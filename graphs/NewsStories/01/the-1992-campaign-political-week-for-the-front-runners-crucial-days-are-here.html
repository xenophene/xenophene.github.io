The 1992 CAMPAIGN: Political Week; For the Front-Runners, Crucial Days Are Here

By R. W. APPLE Jr
Published: January 28, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

Pace-setters often fade in the stretch. It happens to horses, and it happens to politicians. So this week, which should show something about the staying power of the presumed front-runners in the New Hampshire primary, could turn out to be highly significant.

Neither President Bush nor Gov. Bill Clinton of Arkansas is likely to find the going easy. Mr. Bush must somehow manage, in his State of the Union Message on Tuesday night, to convince the intensely provincial people here that he has a concrete program to pull their state out of recession. Mr. Clinton must somehow recover his balance after being staggered by allegations of marital infidelity last week.

For the President, the task is making hard-hit voters believe that he, a man born to privilege, rich most of his life, understands their plight.

Bush supporters in the state are fretful, and they do not hide it. "I'm very, very nervous about him putting all his eggs in one basket," said Tom Rath, a former State Attorney General who has been active in the Bush campaign. "Suppose he gives the greatest speech of his life. The next day, the guy in Laconia or Nashua still doesn't have a job. He's still hurting."

Challenged here by Patrick J. Buchanan, the conservative commentator, limited in what he can propose by lack of resources and obliged to take his stand weeks before the vote here, Mr. Bush has his work cut out.

On Wednesday, only a few hours after his speech, the President will announce his budget, which is expected to exceed $1.5 trillion, and the Government is expected to make public a new set of statistics showing that the economy, after starting to recover last spring and summer, stalled again last fall. The figures will give fresh ammunition to those who argue that Mr. Bush mishandled the economy.

And all week long, Mr. Buchanan will be on the streets of the state, while Mr. Bush is absent. The President will not be here again for two weeks, campaigning solely through radio and television advertisements and surrogate speakers.

For Mr. Clinton, the stakes are even higher. As he said in his interview on the CBS News program "60 Minutes" Sunday evening, this week should demonstrate whether he will be able to resume discussion of the economic and social issues from which he was diverted by accusations that he had carried on a 12-year affair with Gennifer Flowers, a former nightclub singer.

That will depend on the voters and on the reporters covering him, whom the Governor accused of playing "a game of gotcha" at his expense. When accusations of sexual impropriety surfaced four years ago during former Senator Gary Hart's Presidential campaign, reporters hounded Mr. Hart with questions on that subject alone, contributing to his decision to withdraw from the race because he could no longer get his message across.

Mr. Clinton forcefully denied he had an affair with Ms. Flowers, but refused to deny having an affair with anyone, saying he had had rocky passages in his marriage and allowing the public to draw its own conclusions.

A telephone survey last week, after the accusations had been publicized by Star, a supermarket tabloid, showed Mr. Clinton sagging badly in New Hampshire. It said he had fallen into a tie with former Senator Paul E. Tsongas of Massachusetts, who had been trailing by 25 percentage points.

Several polls have shown that about 15 percent of American voters say they would not back someone who had had an extramarital affair. The figure is even higher in the South, where the primaries on March 3 and 10, in which Mr. Clinton has been favored, are concentrated.

Much will depend on whether Mr. Clinton can avoid additional damage. Mr. Tsongas, dismissed until recently as a Bruce Babbitt -- straight-talking but not salable -- has been winning plaudits in the last few days and might then be a beneficiary, or Senator Bob Kerrey of Nebraska.

It is still possible, politicians here say, that people will believe Mr. Clinton and his wife, Hillary. Some may vote for him because they resent the role of news organizations in spreading the accusations, which have not yet been substantiated. If he wins -- and with five candidates in the Democratic primary, he might need only 35 percent or 40 percent of the vote -- he might be strengthened enough to put his drive back on course.

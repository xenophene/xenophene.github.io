Observer; It's Only A Story, Bill

By RUSSELL BAKER
Published: January 25, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

Not a single vote cast, and Governor Clinton has already won the press. He is now the Front-Runner! Evidence is everywhere. The cover of Time. The New Republic dazzled. New York magazine fascinated. Grocery tabloids leering with hints of scandal. What could be firmer evidence of front-runnerhood than hints that the man is worth a grocery leer?

The Washington Post coins "Clintonmania" and gives ultimate proof of front-runnership: a practically endless portrait of his campaign strategist. This indeed is front-running. And not a vote cast anywhere.

How did Clinton became front-runner faster than a speeding bullet? To understand you must know that a front-runner is indispensable to the press. For the press a campaign without a front-runner is like Washington without a President. Without one you can't build a good story. Imagine not having Ron and Nancy or George and Barbara to look in on every day.

That's why we keep a fortune in story-telling know-how constantly massed at the White House -- to bring you every tiny episode in the adventures of Man of Destiny, his Lovely Spouse and their Charming Dog Buster. You need story, so we need story.

O.K., back to front-runner-ism: Imagine another Presidential campaign coming up. Woe and misery! For months nothing but politicians doing dim, dreary political things at a time 250 million Americans don't give a hog's snuffle about politics. We simply have to have a story.

The solution: Persuade the public there's a race going on. In fact, we've sold the idea so well that you're probably saying, "But it is a race, stupid." To which I can only reply that if you're under the impression the Kentucky Derby has something in common with deciding who gets a free pass to the U.S. Treasury you need to repeat Political Science 101.

Yet to keep you interested we pretend this isn't about taxes and boodle but about a race. Races are fun -- somebody ahead, somebody gaining: that's entertainment. Which is why we need a front-runner soon after the show starts. Normally the press would have anointed one months ago and it would have been Governor Cuomo.

This would have given us rich, slowly swelling story values -- the long buildup for Cuomo, then alarms about Cuomo losing steam, then the delicious possibility that he might be "knocked off" in New Hampshire.

New Hampshire's almost ritualistic "knocking-off" of the front-runner is an overworked plot twist of which press and public never tire. After the front-runner's long buildup, his knocking-off not only creates an exciting event to keep race fans attentive but also gives the press a refreshing twist to get us through the story's next furlong. ("Now who will be the new front-runner, race fans?")

By refusing to campaign, Cuomo upset the usual schedule and made trouble for those who have to make the nutty New Hampshire primary seem like one of the vital events of American political history. Since nobody but their wives had heard of the other Democratic candidates, how was an audience to be built?

Obviously an instant front-runner was needed. But why Clinton? He won the elimination process. Paul Tsongas's Greek and Massachusetts credentials reminded the press unhappily of Michael Dukakis. Senator Harkin wore the label "liberal" and the press has decided "liberal" won't do. The label for Jerry Brown was even deadlier: "flaky."

Senator Kerrey didn't do quite as well as Clinton in New Hampshire polls. Polls have biblical weight among political reporters, even in exercises as weird as a Democratic primary in New Hampshire. An intensely conservative state, tiny New Hampshire invariably votes Republican and bears no demographic resemblance to the mass of the American electorate.

Logically, a good showing in conservative, Republican, tax-hating New Hampshire should disqualify a Democrat, but politics hates logic. Thanks to the press ballyhoo accorded a New Hampshire winner, big contributors pour wealth into his campaign fund while starving the rest.

Clinton fitted the press idea this month of what a Democratic candidate should be in 1992. The accent was Southern, the style telegenic, the smile good, the history unliberal, the program not exciting. Ideally of course, unpredictable, outrageous, meaningless little New Hampshire might once again delight all humanity by knocking off poor Clinton. What a good story for us, and for you.

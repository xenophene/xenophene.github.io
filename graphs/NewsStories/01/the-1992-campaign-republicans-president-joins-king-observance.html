THE 1992 CAMPAIGN: Republicans; PRESIDENT JOINS KING OBSERVANCE

By ROBIN TONER
Published: January 18, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

President Bush came to the resting place of the Rev. Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. today, locked arms with his widow and sang "We Shall Overcome" in a trip clearly aimed at smoothing the edges of the Presidential image on race.

But before Mr. Bush left the stage of the Martin Luther King Jr. Center for Nonviolent Social Change, he heard an angry benediction from one of Dr. King's daughters, the Rev. Bernice King. In her prayer, she said the problems of racism, poverty and violence that her father had fought against remained a blight on the land, although she did not link their persistence directly to Mr. Bush.

"How dare we celebrate when more than 40 million Americans are without health care?" she demanded in the closing prayer. "How dare we celebrate in the midst of a recession, when nobody is sure whether their jobs are secure?"

It was a tough note of implicit criticism in an otherwise gently planned day in which Mr. Bush surrounded himself with the symbols of racial tolerance and biracial democracy. He laid a wreath at Dr. King's tomb, signed a proclamation declaring Monday a national holiday to honor Dr. King and kept Coretta Scott King close to his side.

The President was accompanied by his only black Cabinet member, Dr. Louis W. Sullivan, Secretary of Health and Human Services, who seldom travels with him. Before joining the Cabinet, Dr. Sullivan was president of the Morehouse School of Medicine in Atlanta.

In Mr. Bush's speech at the King Center today, he appealed for "compassion" and "service" and the values that Dr. King "beseeched us to embrace -- of tolerance and decency and mutual respect." Mr. Bush talked of his committment to "root out bigotry wherever we find it." He listened to the glee club from Dr. King's alma mater, Morehouse College, sing spirituals like "I've Gotta Mind to Do Right Everyday." 'Cannot Walk the Walk'

But there was blunt criticism of the President's trip from Democrats. John Lewis, the Democratic Congressman from Georgia who served with Dr. King in some of his bloodiest campaigns, said in an interview of Mr. Bush, "He's good at talking the talk, but he cannot walk the walk." Mr. Lewis added, "Dr. King didn't go in much for symbols."

And Ronald H. Brown, the chairman of the Democratic National Committee, declared in a statement: "Today, at the gravesite of Martin Luther King Jr., President Bush's hypocritical rheotric was at its worst. How can a President who allowed racially divisive ads to become the cornerstone of his Presidential campaign, who signed the Civil Rights Act only after insurmountable pressure from civil rights organizations, be taken at his word when he claims he's committed to ridding our country of bigotry and racism?"

Mr. Brown was referring to the Bush campaign's use in 1988 of the case of Willie Horton, a black convicted murderer who escaped from the Massachusetts prison system while on a weekend furlough and later raped a Maryland woman and stabbed her fiance. The campaign used the case to attack Michael S. Dukakis, the Democratic nominee, as soft on crime. Where Sensitivity Counts

As he enters an election year, Mr. Bush has two political reasons to send signals of racial sensitivity. Republicans have long hoped to nibble away at the Democrats' support among blacks, which is overwhelming in Presidential elections and many statewide races. At the same time, Mr. Bush has to pay attention to his party's image on racial issues among centrist white voters.

Some analysts argue that these voters can be turned off by the rougher racial politics of recent years, including Mr. Bush's repeated attacks on Congress for advocating what he asserted was a "quota bill" but what its advocates, Republican and Democratic, asserted was simply legislation to combat job discrimination. Moreover, such voters are likely to be alarmed by the rise of David Duke, the former Klansman who ran for Governor of Louisiana last year and is now seeking the Republican Presidential nomination.

Mr. Bush told his audience at the King center today that he was proud that two major civil rights bills had been passed during his Administration: the Americans With Disabilities Act and the Civil Rights Bill of '91. The latter measure generated a two-year struggle between the White House and Congress that ultimately ended in compromise. 'Kindler, Gentler' Oratory

Mr. Bush's day was infused with the kind of "kinder, gentler" oratory that he occasionally used in the otherwise hard-edged 1988 campaign. He talked of the importance of family -- "Barb and I feel it in our own lives" -- and the importance of community service. He noted that he had met this week with Magic Johnson and said he wanted to help the basketball star in fighting the spread of AIDS.

"I want to use the bully pulpit of the White House -- continue to use it for that same purpose, to speak out for strong research so to help people better understand the disease and to speak out for a change of behavior," said Mr. Bush, who has often been criticized by advocates for people with AIDS for not doing enough.

Mr. Bush also took the opportunity today to announce a reorganization of Federal job training programs that he said would make certain that such programs suited "the needs of the workplace and the marketplace." Administration officials said the move was intended to streamline and consolidate such programs, currently spread over several Federal agencies, and create "one stop shopping centers" for such services.

An Administration official said it would not involve any additional money.

Mr. Bush was greeted here today by a poll in The Atlanta-Journal Constitution that showed that even in the South, which is considered his stronghold, the President is suffering political harm. In the poll, conducted by telephone in 12 Southern states, Mr. Bush's approval rating had dropped to 55 percent, from 73 percent in September.

The survey, conducted by telephone Jan. 9-13, was based on interviews with 802 people considerered likely to vote and had a margin of sampling error of plus or minus 3.5 percentage points.

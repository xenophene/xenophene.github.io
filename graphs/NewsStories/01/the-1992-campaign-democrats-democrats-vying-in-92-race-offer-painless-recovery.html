THE 1992 CAMPAIGN: Democrats; DEMOCRATS VYING IN '92 RACE OFFER PAINLESS RECOVERY

By DAVID E. ROSENBAUM
Published: January 18, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

Taking their cue from the successful scripts of Ronald Reagan and George Bush, the Democratic candidates for President this year offer voters mostly painless solutions to the nation's economic problems.

Day after day in their campaigns, the candidates say the economy is the pivotal issue, and they repeat a common theme about ending a recession caused by the Bush Administration and launching a period of sustained economic growth.

But except for former Senator Paul E. Tsongas of Massachusetts, the Democrats make no suggestion, at least in their public pronouncements, that some bitter medicine might be necessary now to guarantee a healthy economy in the long run. Economists Express Distress

Their omissions distress many economists who are usually aligned with the Democratic Party, like Ted Van Dyk, who was an associate of Hubert H. Humphrey, and Charles L. Schultze, who was budget director in the Johnson Administration and chairman of President Jimmy Carter's Council of Economic Advisers.

The Presidential candidates rarely mention such unpleasant topics as the budget deficit, poor people, energy wastefulness or urban decay. Instead, they concentrate on tax cuts, universal medical insurance and new public works projects.

Economists generally agree that there is no way to finance such popular measures without increasing the deficit, raising taxes or cutting Social Security and Medicare, but a voter would never know that from listening to the Democratic candidates. A Reagan 'Legacy'

"This is significantly a legacy of Ronald Reagan," said Robert J. Shapiro, an economist here who is advising Gov. Bill Clinton of Arkansas. "Reagan taught politicians and their handlers to focus on short-term benefits and that there was never any downside to the promise of a benefit."

Of the five major Democratic candidates, Mr. Tsongas and Mr. Clinton have published detailed statements of economic principles and proposals. They differ on many specifics but basically agree on the philosophy of using tax policy and Government resources to make workers and business more productive.

Senators Tom Harkin of Iowa and Bob Kerrey of Nebraska talk much more in generalities. But they have staked out distinct positions along the ideological spectrum: Mr. Harkin as the apostle of the liberal and labor wing of the party who advocates spending Government money on projects, Mr. Kerrey as a moderate who wants to overhaul the medical care system.

Former Gov. Edmund G. Brown Jr. of California says he is still working on the details of his platform, and he has avoided many of the economic issues that the other candidates discuss.

The other candidates deplore the growing gap between the incomes of the rich and the poor and blame the policies of the Reagan and Bush administrations. All the Democrats say the Government should be more actively engaged than it has been under Mr. Bush in using tax and spending policy to meet social and economic goals. And to one degree or another, all the Democrats advocate some form of economic nationalism, from Mr. Harkin's fist-pounding vow to "get tough on our trade competitors," to Mr. Tsongas's gentle urging of consumers to buy American-made products.

In his literature, Mr. Clinton maintains he is a free trader, but in campaign speeches his views are hardly distinguishable from those who openly favor a more protectionist trade policy. In the candidates' debate on Dec. 15, Mr. Clinton said he would "insist on fair trade," the euphemism used by those who favor trade restrictions, and suggested he would "retaliate" if Japan did not change its trade policies.

Many economists with strong Democratic connections said they were upset by the emphasis on trade restrictions and on the candidates' reluctance to discuss what these economists see as the country's economic difficulty, the enormous budget deficit.

"I'm terribly dismayed," said Mr. Van Dyk, who is now an economic and political consultant. He holds that any responsible Democratic platform would include a reduction in the deficit through a higher gasoline tax and a limit on what rich people could receive under Medicare and other Government programs.

Mr. Schultze, now an economist at the Brookings Institution, scolded the Democratic candidates for not coming to grips with the budget deficit. "You're not going to get out with nice, sugar-coated medicine," he said.

But the politicians remember what happened when Walter F. Mondale proposed a tax increase in 1984 and Michael S. Dukakis talked grimly about the deficit in 1988, while Mr. Reagan and Mr. Bush talked only of keeping taxes low.

Mr. Clinton, Mr. Kerrey and Mr. Brown support a tax reduction for middle class taxpayers, although most economists say that such a cut would do little for the economy.

Mr. Clinton and Mr. Kerrey say they would finance tax relief for the middle class primarily through higher taxes on those with incomes of more than about $200,000 a year. Mr. Brown has not said how he would pay for a tax cut.

From different perspectives, Mr. Harkin and Mr. Tsongas oppose an income-tax cut and say the money could be better used elsewhere.

Mr. Harkin calls the few hundred dollars a year that would be yielded by a tax cut "a joke, a symbol," and would prefer to spend the money, as he said in last month's debate, on "roads and bridges and highways, mass transit, communications, energy systems."

Mr. Tsongas has a more downbeat tone and stresses the importance of the deficit and the strain an income-tax cut would place on the budget. But he favors other tax cuts that he says would increase saving and investment, and some of his proposals differ little from Mr. Bush's. For example, Mr. Tsongas would cut the capital gains tax, expand individual retirement accounts with favorable tax treatment and allow a new tax credit for business investments in plants and equipment.

Mr. Tsongas is not the only Democrat who sometimes sounds like Mr. Bush on economic matters. He is joined by Mr. Clinton and Mr. Kerrey in calling for a cut in the capital gains tax, long favored by the Republicans. Unlike Mr. Bush, who would apply the cut to profits from almost all investments, the three Democrats would restrict it to specific investments.

Mr. Harkin, in contrast, says a capital gains tax cut would "just dig the hole deeper" by subsidizing the rich. Using the Government

But however much some of their policies may sound like the Republicans', the Democratic candidates are distinguishable from Mr. Bush by an important feature. The President basically wants the Government to keep hands off and let the marketplace run the economy. To one degree or another, the Democrats want to use the Government's influence to guide the economy.

Mr. Kerrey and Mr. Tsongas use the phrase "industrial policy" in their speeches. This is a term, anathema to most Republicans, for throwing Government resources behind specific industries and even particular companies to give them a leg up on their international competitors. Mr. Clinton avoids the term but in many ways embraces the doctrine.

Mr. Kerrey and Mr. Clinton espouse a new agency in the Government to channel money into civilian research, modeled after the existing Pentagon research agency that some conservatives have criticized for violating free-market principles.

Mr. Harkin also talks about investment, but it is primarily Government investment he wants. He proposes public works projects like the New Deal efforts of the 1930's that he says would put millions of Americans to work.

All the candidates would finance at least some of their proposals with military budget reductions. But none have toted up the cost of their programs or said exactly where the Pentagon would be cut.

Mr. Clinton has an additional idea. He would divide the budget into investment spending and consumption spending. Each year, the consumption budget could rise no faster than the rate of increase in personal incomes.

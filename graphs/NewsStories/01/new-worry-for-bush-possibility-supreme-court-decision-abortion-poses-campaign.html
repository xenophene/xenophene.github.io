New Worry for Bush; Possibility of Supreme Court Decision On Abortion Poses Campaign Risk

By ROBIN TONER
Published: January 22, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

The Supreme Court's action today on abortion activates an extraordinarily divisive issue in the 1992 Presidential election and becomes one more worrisome variable for President Bush's re-election campaign.

Since the Supreme Court began restricting abortion rights, most notably in 1989, Democrats have grown increasingly convinced that their support for the constitutional right to choose abortion is a major political plus.

Many Republican strategists had hoped to cast their party in 1992 as a "big tent" where all sides could feel at home. But today's action suggests that the conservative Court created by Presidents Reagan and Bush will send a very different message this year.

Most of the major candidates for the Democratic Presidential nomination were already beginning to attack Mr. Bush for his staunch opposition to legal abortion. Today's Court decision, accepting a Pennsylvania abortion case for review and raising the possibility of overturning or further weakening Roe v. Wade, the 1973 decision legalizing abortion, before the election, suggests that the issue could haunt Mr. Bush throughout the general election campaign. Partisan Lines

The partisan lines could hardly be clearer: All five of the major Democratic candidates will help mark the 19th anniversary of Roe v. Wade Wednesday night at a Washington gathering of the National Abortion Rights Action League. Mr. Bush, who once held a more flexible stand on abortion earlier in his political career, has remained fiercely anti-abortion as President and Vice President.

The political impact of abortion, like every other aspect of the issue, is a matter of dispute. Both sides come armed with voting data that show they have the greater political power and can produce more votes at the polls. What is clear is that Mr. Bush is headed for a squeeze from both sides.

Patrick J. Buchanan, who is challenging Mr. Bush from the right in the Republican primary here, declared today: "I'm pro-life and I think my party should be pro-life. If that loses my party votes, so be it."

At the same time, Frank Greer, an adviser to Governor Bill Clinton of Arkansas, gave a taste of the Democratic attack to come. "It's going to be a voting issue," Mr. Greer said. "I believe George Bush has packed the Court and he's going to pay a political price for it. This will push the issue front and center."

Torie Clark, press secretary to the Bush campaign, declined to comment on the potential political fallout. "No comment on hypotheticals," she said.

Both Mr. Bush and his party leaders seemed to recognize that public attitudes were changing on the issue as the Court moved closer to overturning Roe v. Wade. As a result, Mr. Bush has tried to signal that those who disagree with his stance on abortion were nevertheless welcome in the party. But even before today's Court action, Mr. Bush was having trouble managing the issue in his own party and seemed headed for a battle over the Republican platform, which now calls for a constitutional amendment outlawing abortion.

The worst case scenario for Mr. Bush, in the view of many Democrats and some of his Republican friends, would involve the High Court overturning Roe at the height of the campaign. That, coupled with the weak economy, could have a significant impact on the younger, suburban voters who are a critical swing vote in many Presidential elections.

The abortion rights issue had seemed to cool since the handful of elections held in 1989, right after the Court handed down Webster v. Reproductive Health Services, which gave states sweeping new powers to restrict abortion. The Virginia and the New Jersey elections for governor that year seemed to turn on the abortion issue, giving major advantages to the Democrats that year. But in 1990, when the economy started to lag and war was looming in the Persian Gulf, the issue no longer seemed as dominant.

There are constant arguments over which side has the greater political clout. Abortion opponents assert that their forces vote, consistently and single-mindedly, on the issue -- in far greater numbers than those who support abortion rights.

Both sides are dug in. And the Supreme Court today may have guaranteed that a new and bruising round of the struggle will be fought in 1992.

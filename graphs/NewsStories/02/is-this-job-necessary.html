Is This Job Necessary?

By Michael Kazin
Published: February 23, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

CRAPSHOOT Rolling the Dice on the Vice Presidency. By Jules Witcover. 450 pp. New York: Crown Publishers. $25.

SINCE World War II, as Jules Witcover documents in "Crapshoot," some Vice Presidents have occasionally managed to transcend the passivity that comes with the job. Richard Nixon publicly debated the merits of consumer capitalism with Nikita S. Khrushchev; Spiro T. Agnew waged an alliterative and popular assault on the liberal news media; and Walter Mondale struggled, without much success, to teach President Jimmy Carter how to get his program through Congress. Still, the jokes that have dogged Dan Quayle since his nomination in the summer of 1988 reflect the enduring superfluity of the office, as well as the mediocrity of its current holder. A special variety of gallows humor has always attended the men -- among them such indisputable talents as John Adams, Thomas Jefferson, Theodore Roosevelt and Lyndon B. Johnson -- whose only essential task has been to wait for their boss to quit or die.

Mr. Witcover, a syndicated columnist, quotes the one-liners, but his objective is more ambitious. He tries to persuade us to take the choice of candidate for the second spot more seriously. Eight Presidents have died in office, and another resigned. That fact, along with the current widespread assumption that the Vice President is the natural heir to the top job, should, Mr. Witcover argues, dissuade us from accepting a person picked primarily to help win the general election or to satisfy the whim of a party's standard-bearer. With Dan Quayle clearly in mind, he writes: "A new Vice President must be ready to assume the Presidency from a standing start. It is legitimately said that the Presidency is no place for on-the-job training, nor should the Vice Presidency be."

Mr. Witcover mulls over, without embracing, a number of alternative proposals that might satisfy his high-minded objective. Should a Presidential candidate choose his or her running mate before the nominating convention? No, for that would eliminate well-qualified rivals who, once defeated, would gladly accept the second spot. Should the choice of a Vice President be deferred until after the election, allowing the President-elect to nominate someone of enough independent stature to win a majority vote from Congress? No, for that would be undemocratic, and would potentially make the office hostage to a Congress controlled by the opposition party. Mr. Witcover seems most sympathetic to the idea, once argued strongly by Arthur M. Schlesinger Jr., that the office should be eliminated altogether. If the Presidency fell vacant, a quick "intermediate election" could be scheduled while a Cabinet member temporarily filled the office.

But this discussion, stuffed into a concluding chapter, is overwhelmed by the outpouring of journalistic history that comes before it. "Crapshoot" is a book written for election addicts. In brisk, colloquial prose, Mr. Witcover races through the political careers of every Vice President from John Adams to Henry A. Wallace, Franklin D. Roosevelt's running mate in 1940. Then he slows down to recount some familiar but absorbing tales: the Democrats' 1944 dumping of Wallace for Harry S. Truman; Richard Nixon's salvation of his place on the 1952 Republican ticket with his famous Checkers speech; the humiliation Lyndon B. Johnson suffered from the Kennedy clan in order to get the Vice-Presidential nod in 1960; Spiro T. Agnew's speedy transformation from Executive of Baltimore County to renowned scourge of "nattering nabobs of negativism" to plea-copper on a charge of income tax evasion; and, of course, the saga of Dan Quayle, whom Mr. Witcover dubs President Bush's "Damoclean sword." (He reminds us of the press's "gaffe watch" on Mr. Quayle, observing that "the most quoted Quayleism" of a recent season was "his twist on the United Negro College Fund slogan that 'A mind is a terrible thing to waste.' Speaking for the fund, Quayle mangled it into 'What a waste it is to lose one's mind, or not to have a mind, is being very wasteful. How true that is.' ")

It is good to have all these stories collected in one volume. But the zest Mr. Witcover brings to his main task undermines the sobriety of his advice on choosing future Vice Presidents. Besides, in an age when Presidential nominees are identified weeks or even months before party conventions, speculating about who would help or damage the ticket is one of the few joys left for Americans who care passionately about campaigns and elections. For this reason alone, I doubt the present selection process will soon be altered.

Michael Kazin teaches history at American University and is writing a book on populism in the 20th-century United States.

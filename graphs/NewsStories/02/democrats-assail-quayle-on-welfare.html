Democrats Assail Quayle On Welfare

By LINDSEY GRUSON
Published: February 29, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

In a swelling chorus of outrage that was colored by partisanship, New Yorkers -- at least Democratic politicians -- today lambasted Vice President Dan Quayle and called his criticism of the state and its welfare system little more than a thinly disguised appeal to racism.

Gov. Mario M. Cuomo said Mr. Quayle's stinging rebuke on Thursday of New York's "liberal economics" and its welfare system should have made the audience ill and was part of a calculated political effort to blame the recession on the unemployed.

"There's no doubt that the Republicans have done their political homework," Governor Cuomo said in a statement released by his office. "They've decided from their polls that a significant number of voters are susceptible to divisive and irrational arguments that welfare is largely to blame for the nation's economic ills."

The counterstrike by the Governor, whose verbal feuds with the Vice President have often degenerated into slashing personal exchanges, came less than 24 hours after Mr. Quayle traveled to New York City and in a biting, broad-ranging attack, called it a paradigm of everything that is wrong with liberalism.

"The liberal vision of a happy, productive and content welfare state hasn't even worked on 22 square miles of the most valuable real estate in the world," the Vice President said in a speech to the Economic Club of New York. He went on to recite a litany of New York's problems and said they were a product of liberal social policies.

"Liberal economics may prevail here, but it sure doesn't work here," the Vice President said in the speech to club members during a dinner at the New York Hilton in midtown Manhattan. "Let us look at this great city. It attests to what becomes of those who put their faith in the benevolence of the state."

The speech was part of an effort by the Bush Administration to make welfare into a major election-year issue on the assumption that middle-income voters will be hostile to welfare in a recession. The President, whose re-election drive has been plagued by much greater hurdles than expected just six months ago, made welfare an important point in his State of the Union Message last month.

Mr. Cuomo has generally urged the Administration not to exploit welfare as a political issue and has warned that the tactic risks fanning racial intolerance. But today's response rekindled the personal feud between the Governor and the Vice President that erupted last fall when Mr. Quayle repeatedly referred to the Governor as "Mario" and Mr. Cuomo responded by calling the Vice President "Danny the Cabin Boy."

"If they were dumb enough to listen to the Vice President and not get sick to their stomach, this country is in worse shape than I thought," the Governor said, referring to members of the Economic Club. "If they were as smart as I think they are in New York, what they said is: 'This is a joke. They sent this kid here to play cynical, political games.' " Mr. Cuomo made that remark in an interview on the CNBC program "Inside Opinion."

The Governor's view that Mr. Quayle was appealing to racism by bringing up welfare was supported by Senator Daniel Patrick Moynihan, the New York Democrat. A student of the welfare system for more than three decades, Senator Moynihan has written several studies on the subject and is considered one of the country's leading authorities.

"We're not going to let welfare become a corrosive, divisive, damaging, hurtful issue to our nation. We're better than that, better than this speech," Senator Moynihan said today, as he introduced a bill intended to break welfare's hold on generations of many families by increasing Federal financing for job training to $5 billion, from $1 billion.

Though Senator Moynihan criticized welfare bashing in general, he also called on Mr. Bush to "put up, or please to shut up." He criticized, as an appeal to race, a Bush campaign commercial in which the President promises to "change welfare and make the able-bodied work."

Senator Moynihan's Republican counterpart, Senator Alfonse M. D'Amato, praised Mr. Quayle's comments. As part of his own stump speech in his bid for re-election, the state's junior Senator regularly denounces "welfare cheats" and claims that the state's exceedingly generous welfare provisions make New York a haven for the poor. 'Basically Correct'

"Quayle's observations were basically correct," Senator D'Amato said today in a telephone interview while traveling between events in New York.

Mr. Moynihan said the criticism of welfare raised the race card.

Asked whether the recent statements on welfare had raised a race-based appeal, a Bush campaign spokeswoman said, Torie Clark, said: "Nothing could be farther from the truth. It's just the opposite in fact -- the President wants to help all people of all colors to get work, to get education and to help them keep their families together."

Many of Mr. D'Amato's potential Democratic opponents found the Administration's approach wanting.

Geraldine A. Ferraro, the former Queens Congresswoman, said Mr. Quayle's remarks were part of a cynical attempt by the Republican administration to shift the national debate away from the cuts in Federal aid that have caused the urban social problems that the Vice President points to as proof that the welfare system does not work.

"I think it's a tactic that is attempting to move the discussion away from what the truth is," she said. "The truth is that the Federal Government has abrogated its responsibility. Everything tends to look distorted when you're looking at reality through a country club window."

Another Senate candidate, City Comptroller Elizabeth Holtzman, joined the chorus denouncing the use of welfare as a political tool. "This is a diversionary tactic by two birds of a feather," she said, referring to the Senator and the Vice President. "New York is in trouble because of the disastrous budgets of Bush, Quayle and D'Amato."

THE 1992 CAMPAIGN: Florida; Where the Immediate Forecast Is Sunny for Bush but Cloudy for Democrats

By DAVID E. ROSENBAUM
Published: February 29, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

A week and a half before the primary voting, George Bush seems poised to win all the Republican delegates from Florida, the fourth largest block in the country, but the Democratic picture is much more clouded.

Bill Clinton has far and away the best organization and the most money to spend among the Democrats, and the campaigns of his competitors have not taken root here. But this large, diverse and rapidly growing state has a history of election surprises. Few experts rule out the possibility that Mr. Clinton could be overtaken on March 10, "Super Tuesday."

Despite Florida's economic miseries and President Bush's disappointing showing in New Hampshire and South Dakota, he appears unbeatable here. Bush Campaign Optimistic

The President's son Jeb, a former Florida Secretary of Commerce and now his father's state campaign chairman, said he saw "no visible evidence" that the campaign was in any trouble.

Thomas Hines, a Tampa businessman who is running the Florida campaign for Mr. Bush's challenger, Patrick J. Buchanan, said Mr. Buchanan had not conceded Florida, but he added, "I'd be satisfied with just a portion of what we did in New Hampshire here in Florida."

Under Republican rules in Florida, the candidate who gets the most votes in a Congressional district takes all the delegates from that district, and the one who wins statewide also wins a bonus group of delegates.

So even if Mr. Buchanan receives 37 percent of the vote as he did in New Hampshire -- much better than most politicians imagine he will do -- he will have no Florida delegates to the Republican convention in August unless he wins a Congressional district.

Democrats are looking at two examples from the past and are uncertain which is the most applicable to 1992.

Four years ago, Michael S. Dukakis used organizational and financial strength to win a substantial victory here. The alternative example is that of Gary Hart, who came to Florida in 1984 after a surprising victory in New Hampshire and defeated Walter F. Mondale, who had superior organization and money.

This year, Mr. Clinton, the Governor of Arkansas, has campaign organizations in all the state's 67 counties and endorsements from many of its most prominent Democrats. He handily won a straw poll at the state Democratic convention in December, and he has enough money for blanket television advertising.

Richard Scher, a political scientist at the University of Florida, said he was of two minds about which of the past examples would prevail this year.

On one hand, Mr. Scher said, many Democrats seem disturbed by questions about Mr. Clinton's personal life and are "waiting to see if another shoe drops." Where's the Excitement?

But in Mr. Clinton's favor, Mr. Scher said, is the fact that none of the other Democrats seems to have generated the kind of excitement here that Mr. Hart did in 1984.

Paul E. Tsongas may have the best chance to emulate Mr. Hart's come-out-of-nowhere success in Florida. Mr. Tsongas, the former Senator from Massachusetts, has run a few television advertisements and plans to step up his commercials as election day nears.

Dennis Kanin, Mr. Tsongas's national campaign manager, conceded that Mr. Clinton would be difficult to overtake. But he said, "We have enough money to be competitive." Mr. Kanin called Flordia "delegate rich," and observed that under the Democratic rules, Mr. Tsongas could win delegates without finishing first statewide or in any Congressional districts.

The other Democrats do not seem to. Paul Pezzella, an experienced organizer of Florida campaigns who is running Senator Bob Kerrey's, suggested that he did not have sufficient money now for television commercials

Senator Tom Harkin's campaign also seems to be struggling, even though he finished second in the straw poll in December.

Former Gov. Edmund G. Brown Jr. of California is not mounting a competitive campaign here.

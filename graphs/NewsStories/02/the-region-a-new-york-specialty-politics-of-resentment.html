THE REGION; A New York Specialty: Politics of Resentment

By MARTIN GOTTLIEB
Published: February 09, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

It's tough to sell mosaics in New York City, even ones that purport to be gorgeous.

As Mayor David N. Dinkins himself might attest, a steadfast appeal to people's better instincts makes for slow going at best.

But that is not the only approach to, and not the most direct and visceral means of, reaching a common ground among people of varied and often hostile backgrounds, particularly in the fractious mix of New York.

Running like a deep river through all these groups -- defined by ethnicity, religion, race, gender, sexual preference, physical impairment, economics and so on -- are deep hurts and resentments formed by historic oppression, discrimination, stereotyping and exclusion from power and from society at large.

Each group experience varies, of course, and it is the particulars -- from events as broad as slavery and the Holocaust to those as specific as trying to maneuver a wheelchair onto a sidewalk without a curb cut -- that make the pain authentic and heartfelt.

But while the particulars are different, the feelings and resentments they stir have much in common. They are the Krazy Glue behind New York's jumbled mosaic.

"Every person I know of in a minority group who has lived with a consciousness of his or her minority status has a feeling, something in the gut, that makes them distrust others' attitudes toward them -- particularly those of people in power," said Irving M. Levine, director emeritus of the Institute for American Pluralism at the American Jewish Committee. 'Acts Like One'

Gov. Mario M. Cuomo, who, like Mayor Dinkins, normally strikes a less embittered note, took hold of these sentiments in his recent bashing of Gov. Bill Clinton for remarks made in a taped conversation with Gennifer Flowers, who claimed to have been his lover.

In an exchange, Ms. Flowers said of Mr. Cuomo, "I wouldn't be surprised if he didn't have some Mafioso major connections."

Mr. Clinton replied with a laugh, "Well, he acts like one."

Mr. Cuomo, who has never been a friend of Mr. Clinton's, brought the roof down on him. As he said in one variation on his theme of the next couple of days: "If you say it this casually about Italian-Americans, what do you say about blacks, what do you say about Jews, what do you say about women, what do you say about the poor people, what do you say about all the other groups who traditionally . . . become the scapegoat?"

More than one national political commentator dismissed this with a groan, concluding that Mr. Cuomo had turned a private verbal gaffe into a call to arms, thereby thrusting himself again into the center of national politics without laying a Presidential candidacy on the line.

But locally, his outrage, however charged with national political freight, has long precedent and wide appeal, particularly because Mr. Cuomo used aggrieved feelings to link disparate groups rather than divide them.

Indeed, the city's most beloved political figure, Mayor Fiorello H. La Guardia, was perhaps the consummate practitioner of what his biographer, Thomas Kessner, called a politics of "unrelieved resentment."

An Italian who grew up on Western Army posts, whose mother was Jewish, whose family was Protestant and whose stature was short and comical, he seemed perpetually on the fringe -- out West where many of his early friends were Indians, in the Italian-American community where his pedigree was decidedly odd and in politics, where he would pick verbal fights that often led to near physical ones when he heard the slur "wop."

"He had a true sense of marginality tied to a deep sense that he had a right to be an American," said Mr. Kessner, who teaches history at the City University of New York. "He was a parading personal drama of vulnerability." Championing the Outsider

There was the time, for example, when Mayor La Guardia's commissioner of water supply and electricity rejected a short man as an inspector in a tough neighborhood. "He won't do," the commissioner said. "He's too small." La Guardia leapt from his chair, Mr. Kessner recounted, and, jumping up and down, blurted, "WHAT'S THE MATTER WITH THE LITTLE GUY? WHAT'S THE MATTER WITH THE LITTLE GUY? WHAT'S THE MATTER WITH THE LITTLE GUY?"

Politically, this translated into fabled forays into New York's ethnic neighborhoods and to addresses from City Hall and in Congress in which he championed the interests of the city's minority groups less by touting their virtues than by attacking their enemies. In 1917, as a freshman in Congress, for example, he made his less than appreciated mark on the heavily Protestant institution by arguing that the literacy tests it instituted for immigrants discriminated against Catholics. The tests, he explained, used selections from the King James Bible.

For Jewish groups he targeted, among others, Henry Ford, whose anti-Semitism was only one of his prejudices. "Based on his ignorance of history, literature and religion," said Congressman La Guardia in an address to the House, "Henry Ford has done more, I will say, owing to bigoted hatred, to create strife and hatred in this country among the races than any man in the United States. And I will say that the wealth and ignorance of Henry Ford combined has made it possible for vicious men to carry on a nefarious warfare against the Jews, not only of America, but of the whole world."

There is, of course, a danger of demagoguery in the stirring of resentments -- something La Guardia was accused of coming close to and something that the rise of David Duke, for one, makes an all the more cautionary concern. The sense of exclusion "can unite people because it's part of the psychological aspect of being in a minority -- as a group member you can easily understand it," said Joseph Giordano, a consultant to the National Italian-American Foundation and a therapist who specializes in intergroup relations. "But it has the power to be destructive. It's powerful stuff."

Mr. Giordano believes that even if Mr. Cuomo should have dropped the issue more quickly than he did, he handled it positively by broadening it into a matter of concern for more than Italians.

Whether a political agenda lay behind Mr. Cuomo's comments is something Mr. Giordano wonders about as much as anyone else. After all, Mr. Cuomo is not the first Italian-American from New York to test the Presidential waters. Roughly 50 years ago, Fiorello La Guardia embarked on a national tour filled with rousing speeches that brought audiences to their feet. After one, according to Mr. Kessner, he turned to a handsome WASP aide and remarked, "If I had a face and a name like yours, they might think of me as a Presidential candidate."

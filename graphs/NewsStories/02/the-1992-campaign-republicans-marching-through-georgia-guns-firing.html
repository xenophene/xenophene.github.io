THE 1992 CAMPAIGN: Republicans; Marching Through Georgia, Guns Firing

By ROBIN TONER
Published: February 26, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

The Republican primary battle between President Bush and Patrick J. Buchanan has rolled into Georgia with an angry tone. Each camp is trying to cast itself as the authentic heir to the Southern conservative tradition, and each is attacking the other in a rough politics of values.

In a dizzying succession of television interviews, Mr. Buchanan sat in an Atlanta hotel suite this week and hit almost every flashpoint in Southern politics. He railed against "blasphemous art" financed by the Federal government, fumed about "racial quotas" and hammered at a President who he said had "cut deals" with Senator Edward M. Kennedy, the Massachusetts Democrat who is one of the enduring targets of the Southern right.

Meanwhile, the Bush campaign, after largely holding its fire in New Hampshire, was on the air today with a new television commercial featuring Gen. P. X. Kelley, the retired Marine Corps Commandant, who criticized Mr. Buchanan for opposing the war in the Persian Gulf. "It was a disappointment to all military people," the general says, "a disappointment to all Americans who supported the Gulf War, and I took it personally."

In a region noted for its tradition of military service, the commercial asks, "If he doesn't think America should lead the world, how can we trust him to lead America?" It ends with the line: "Pat Buchanan: wrong on Desert Storm, wrong for America."

Bill Thorne, executive director of the Bush campaign in Georgia, said: "We came out with a hard-hitting ad because we found most people didn't know he opposed the President on the Persian Gulf war. It's an educational effort."

Jerry Woodruff, director of communications for the Buchanan campaign, said in response to the new commercial: "I don't think Pat Buchanan is vulnerable on the Gulf War issue. After all, Saddam Hussein still has a job while a lot of Americans do not."

Asked by reporters about the Kelley commercial, Mr. Buchanan acknowledged that he initially had opposed United States involvement in the war. But he said that "from the very first shot" he had stood behind the American military and he argued that his initial reluctance for this country to become involved had been no different from positions taken by Gen. Colin L. Powell, the chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff, and Gen. H. Norman Schwarzkopf, the commander of allied forces in the gulf.

In short, both sides are running hard for next Tuesday's primary in Georgia, whose results could be an omen for the following week, when voters in eight Southern or border states go to the polls. After the embarrassment of Mr. Buchanan's 37 percent showing in New Hampshire, to Mr. Bush's 53 percent, the Bush campaign is taking a far more aggressive stance here.

Mr. Bush will encamp in Georgia this weekend, state campaign officials said, and Vice President Dan Quayle is scheduled to arrive on Wednesday. The Bush campaign has also mobilized an army of surrogates, including Representative Newt Gingrich, the Georgia conservative; Senator Phil Gramm of Texas, another stalwart of the right, and Housing Secretary Jack F. Kemp.

Charles Black, a senior adviser to the Bush campaign, said of Mr. Buchanan, "We're going to respond to his charges, and highlight some of his more controversial views." Playing Hardball

Mr. Buchanan, addressing remarks to the Bush campaign as he was campaigning today in Tennessee and Mississippi, said: "If you want to come ahead and play hardball, come right ahead. We Buchanans are used to it."

He accused Mr. Bush and his surrogates of attacking him personally, going beyond the bounds of good political taste.

"The personal insult is the resort of an exhausted mind," he said in a speech to students at Vanderbilt University in Nashville.

The President's forces are trying to pull off a complicated task: to simultaneously defeat Mr. Buchanan, mollify the party's right and lay the groundwork for unifying the party for the general election. In a sign of that balancing act, Fred Cooper, the chairman of the Bush campaign in Georgia argues that good Republicans should not indulge in protest votes. "Our real enemy is the liberal Democrat who the President will be running against," he said.

In a sense, some Bush allies acknowlege, the Republican predicament recalls the task of past Democratic nominees in trying defeat the Rev. Jesse Jackson without antagonizing his core of support. "In some ways, he is sort of the Jesse Jackson of the right," said Mr. Gingrich of Mr. Buchanan in an interview. "But he has much less of a base in the real world."

So far, the Bush campaign is having the toughest attacks on Mr. Buchanan come from voices other than the President's. The other new television commercial begun by the Bush forces today is a positive message featuring the President himself. The advertisement talks about the "agenda to strengthen our economy" and ends: "President Bush: the future of America. In the hands of experience." No Help from the Challenger

Mr. Buchanan is not making it easy for Mr. Bush. At a rally in Atlanta Monday night, where he noted that two of his great-grandfathers "happened to be troublemakers and rabid secessionists," Mr. Buchanan told his cheering audience that, "There's only one candidate in this race who's a conservative and a traditionalist across the board, who believes in lower taxes, in less spending, in traditional values, in standing up for the right to life."

The commentator and former Presidential speech writer grouped Mr. Bush with former Senator Paul E. Tsongas of Massachusetts and Gov. Bill Clinton of Arkansas, the leading candidates for the Democratic Presidential nominations, describing them as "big government people, big spenders."

"They believe in quotas, they believe in Congressional pay raises, they believe in higher taxes," he said.

As his audience urged him on, Mr. Buchanan added, "And if I get elected I will go to Washington and from day one I will fight every one of them up there."

He also continued to emphasize his theme that it was time to "put America first," and redirect American resources to the homefront.

Mr. Gingrich said in an interview, "He's trying to find the Panama Canal: that visceral, emotional, powerful issue that galvanizes a constituency." The Georgia Republican predicts that Mr. Buchanan will get 25 to 30 percent of the vote here.

The Buchanan forces are clearly hoping to attract conservative Democrats, who can vote in either primary because of Georgia's election law. This remains one of the important variables here. In the meantime, the Bush-Buchanan struggle carries on, with a decidedly Southern inflection.

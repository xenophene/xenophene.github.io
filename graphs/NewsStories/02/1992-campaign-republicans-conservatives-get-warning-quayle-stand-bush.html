THE 1992 CAMPAIGN: Republicans; CONSERVATIVES GET WARNING BY QUAYLE TO STAND BY BUSH

By ANDREW ROSENTHAL
Published: February 22, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

In one of its most direct efforts to undermine Patrick J. Buchanan's insurgency, the White House today dispatched Vice President Dan Quayle to warn Republican conservatives that if they work against President Bush they "risk being out of power for a generation."

Mr. Quayle argued that while the President was not "100 percent" conservative, to repudiate him would risk turning the Cabinet over to liberal jurists and advocates of abortion. The Vice President, who has long suffered ridicule, used ridicule to paint a Republican nightmare of a "national condom-distribution campaign for grades 7 and up."

As the 1992 campaign shapes up not only as a test of Mr. Bush, but also of who will lead the conservatives in 1996, Mr. Quayle put in a pitch for himself. In a speech to the Conservative Political Action Conference tonight, he said that re-electing the President would be preparation for another conservative victory in 1996, presumably with Mr. Quayle at the head of the ticket. A Pragmatic Appeal

The Vice President, the White House's primary link to the Republican right since the dismissal of John H. Sununu as White House chief of staff in December, sought to shelter Mr. Bush with the legacy of conservatives like Ronald Reagan and Barry Goldwater. But Mr. Quayle did not promise any changes in what conservatives believe are Mr. Bush's moderate policies and instead offered this pragmatic appeal: Mr. Buchanan cannot win, and backing him would hurt the party.

"The only real question facing us today is whether our President will enter the general election campaign from a position of strength or from a position of weakness," Mr. Quayle said.

He added: "Anyone has the right to wage a symbolic campaign. But it would be irresponsible to endanger all that we have achieved."

The audience gave the Mr. Quayle a warm reception, but it seemed more enthusiastic when he talked about the Persian Gulf war and the collapse of communism than when he talked about domestic issues. The Vice President was introduced by the Ambassador from Kuwait, Saud Nasir al-Sabah, who gave Mr. Bush what amounted to a political endorsement. Only One 'Pat' Mentioned

The Vice President did not mention Mr. Buchanan by name; the only "Pat" cited in the speech was Representative Patricia Schroeder, the Colorado Democrat.

The speech clearly represented a move by the White House and the Bush campaign to stanch the bleeding in the Republican Party as they try to find a way to put the President back on the offensive politically. Its tone of alarm ran counter to the official White House line that Mr. Buchanan is a minor annoyance, that Mr. Bush does not have to cater to the right to win re-election and that his candidacy was untouched by a weak showing in the New Hampshire primary.

"One of our supporters suggested to me that the New Hampshire vote might actually be a blessing in disguise," Mr. Quayle said. "And I could only think of Churchill: 'At the moment, it seems quite effectively disguised.' "

Republican conservatives said that Mr. Bush needed to court conservatives, but that sending Mr. Quayle to do it was not enough. "I think ultimately the argument won't carry the day," said Gary Bauer, president of the Family Research Council, an anti-abortion group.

He added: "They have it within their power to derail the Buchanan candidacy by doing a half-dozen or so conservative things. I'd make the budget director head of the campaign in American Samoa, for starters."

The budget director, Richard G. Darman, is widely disliked by conservatives for having kept a $500 increase in the income tax exemption for children off Mr. Bush's list of the seven most urgent economic measures. Mr. Bauer said that had hurt the President badly.

Mr. Quayle belittled conservatives who argue that a Democratic victory this year "would give the conservative movement a chance to renew and revitalize itself." Conservatives, he said, face "a choice: of continuing what we started or going back to the old bickering over who is 90 percent or who is 100 percent pure." 'The Liberals Are Coming'

He added: "Meanwhile, the liberals are coming together, hell-bent on capturing the White House by whatever means possible. If we lose to them now we will risk being out of power for a generation."

The Vice President jokingly asked his audience to imagine a victorious Democrat naming former President Jimmy Carter as Secretary of State and Ralph Nader as Attorney General. He also offered these images:

*Kate Michelman, head of the National Abortion Rights Action League, is made Secretary of Health and Human Services with a program of "cradle-to-grave socialized medicine," anti-welfare reform programs, condom distribution and "a Federal right to abortion on demand."

*Mrs. Schroeder becomes Secretary of Defense, cuts $150 billion from the Pentagon budget and calls the "Star Wars" antimissile program "a nasty, nutty nightmare."

*In mid-March, "after a 30-minute hearing, a party-line vote confirms Laurence Tribe as the 107th Justice of the Supreme Court of the United States." Mr. Tribe, a Harvard law professor, is a bete noire of the conservatives.

Those mentioned by Mr. Quayle responded in kind.

"I don't believe this," Ms. Michelman said. "H.H.S.? I'm holding out for Vice President. Dan Quayle's proof anyone can do that job."

She added, "I think it is a sign of how far out of touch these guys are with Americans when they think that the need for comprehensive health care and support for the right to choose is a joke."

Calling Mr. Quayle the "attack bird of the Bush campaign," Mrs. Schroeder riposted, "Imagine Neil Bush as Secretary of the Treasury for the next four years." Neil Bush, the President's son, has been embroiled in the savings-and-loan scandal.

Mr. Tribe said: "I certainly didn't expect to be part of Dan Quayle's utopia. And if I'm to be part of anyone's nightmare, Dan Quayle would be a reasonable choice."

Apart from supporting Mr. Bush, the Vice President's appearance was a chance to push himself in the struggle for leadership of conservatives.

In this election, Mr. Quayle declared, "we will lay the groundwork for our next great triumph: a fifth straight conservative victory in the year 1996."

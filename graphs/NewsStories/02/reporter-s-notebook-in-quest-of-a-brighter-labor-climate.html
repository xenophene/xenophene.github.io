Reporter's Notebook; In Quest of a Brighter Labor Climate

By PETER T. KILBORN
Published: February 21, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

Even when Presidents are Republicans, trade unions usually find grounds for getting along with secretaries of labor on some issues, even if agreeing to disagree on others. But union leaders meeting here this week to plan policies find the current incumbent, Lynn Martin, as hostile as they come.

Like previous labor secretaries, Mrs. Martin, who has been in office a year, appeared at a closed session of the 35-member executive council of the American Federation of Labor and Congress of Industrial Organizations. Participants at the annual gathering said she noted at least twice that a mere 16.1 percent of American workers belonged to unions. Unions attribute the decline in part to Republican policies that discourage people from joining unions, and they interpreted her remarks as a way of saying that unions do not matter.

Asked later about Mrs. Martin's appearance, Lane Kirkland, president of the A.F.L.-C.I.O., said, "The areas of disagreement and disparity of views that surfaced were nicely coated with expressions of concern." He mocked her "blithe optimism" that the trade agreement with Mexico would not result in the loss of American jobs.

Ready, Willing and Sidelined

Ronald Carey, elected president of the International Brotherhood of Teamsters on a promise to purge the union of corruption, showed up at the door to the conference's first executive council meeting on Monday seemingly raring to go to work.

As head of the richest and biggest union, with 1.4 million members, Mr. Carey can be expected to wield some influence at these weeklong conferences, where leaders of the nation's major unions work out positions on legislative and other issues. But Mr. Carey discovered that he would have to wait for another day to get in.

As Mr. Carey approached the conference room, William J. McCarthy, who retired as teamsters president with Mr. Carey's inauguration on Feb. 1, invited Mr. Carey into an anteroom for a chat. Twenty minutes later, it was the retired president who emerged to take a seat at the conference. Without comment or evident emotion, the new teamsters leader headed back to his room.

The seat on the executive council, it turns out, was still Mr. McCarthy's because he was elected to it for another two-year term by delegates to an A.F.L.-C.I.O. convention in November. Normally, a member who loses his union job immediately relinquishes his council seat to his successor, although nothing says he must. Mr. McCarthy intended to give up his seat but in his own way.

"Billy indicated he wanted to spend the day listening to the presentations," Mr. Carey said of their chat. Inside, near the end of the meeting, John Sturdivant, president of the American Federation of Government Employees, said Mr. McCarthy made a gracious and brief speech.

"He said his health was bothering him, that he was having problems with his voice and hearing," Mr. Sturdivant said. "He submitted his resignation and proposed Carey," whom the council promptly elected.

Blaming the Unions

Unions tend to attribute their troubles to everyone but themselves. But on the fringes of the gathering here, some of the several hundred guests are blaming the unions for not getting their message to the public, and in particular they blame Mr. Kirkland's A.F.L.-C.I.O.

Greg Tarpinian, head of a labor advocacy group in New York, the Labor Research Association, distributed the results of a survey by Fingerhut/Granados Opinion Research showing that only 3 percent of American voters even knew who Mr. Kirkland is.

"Nineteen ninety-two should be a watershed year for American labor," Mr. Tarpinian said, adding that a weak economy could be used to drive the Republicans out of the White House. "But labor needs an overarching strategic outlook, and it doesn't have one," he said.

A state labor leader who refused to be named for fear of losing his job added: "There is nobody giving a vision of the labor movement to the American public. If you asked who is the spokesman for workers, it's Jesse Jackson, and he's not a part of the labor movement."

Search for a Rescuer

For an institution that fears it is sinking toward irrelevance as long as Republicans remain in the White House, organized labor seems apathetic about the men who are vying to dislodge President Bush. At meetings here two years ago, half the participants seemed to be wearing lapel buttons assailing Frank Lorenzo, head of the now defunct Eastern Airlines, for union-busting.

But of several hundred people attending a reception here this week, only one guest had a Presidential campaign button. David Schlein, a vice president of the American Federation of Government Employees, wore one reading, "Labor for Tom Harkin." One other man, Gerald McEntee, a council member who is president of the American Federation of State, County and Municipal Employees wore a Clinton sticker, but in deference to the council's agreement to stay neutral until later in the week, when a decision is made on whom to support, he kept it on the back of his lapel.

Political figures who arouse strong support here, like Gov. Mario M. Cuomo of New York and Representative Richard A. Gephardt of Missouri, are not running, though hopes were high for a while that they would.

Rumors circulated at the start of the conference that the many New Yorkers who run unions were plotting to ally behind Mr. Cuomo.

"I think there's a real opportunity to throw Bush out," said Moe Biller, president of the American Federation of Postal Workers, a New Yorker who at 76 years old is the dean of the council. "I think Cuomo can do the number best."

But then came the write-in vote in New Hampshire that left Mr. Cuomo in the rear of the pack.

Upon agreeing to end their neutrality in the campaign, 14 unions said they would be supporting Senator Harkin, three said they would back Mr. Clinton, and the rest said they were still sounding out their members.

"We don't have anybody out there that people like," said Barry Feinstein, the top teamsters' leader in New York. "So people are settling."

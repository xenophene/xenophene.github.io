THE 1992 CAMPAIGN: White House Memo; Trick for Bush Is to Run Against the Bush of 1980

By ANDREW ROSENTHAL
Published: February 14, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

In a new commercial that his campaign began showing on television today, President Bush gazes into a camera in the White House. He reminds New Hampshire Republicans that they "will once again lead the nation" in choosing a President and asks them to "stand united to defeat the liberal Democrats in the fall."

Campaigning in New Hampshire last month, Vice President Dan Quayle repeatedly told voters: "The Republican revolution began here 12 years ago in the snows of New Hampshire. They've been trying to tell you made a mistake. You did not make a mistake."

There is only one catch: Mr. Bush's 1980 candidacy, built on moderate Republican themes, died in the snows of New Hampshire, where he lost badly to Ronald Reagan in the primary and later withdrew from the race.

But now it is an important part of Mr. Bush's strategy for the New Hampshire primary to harken back to the Reagan legacy and present himself as a true conservative in hopes of countering Patrick J. Buchanan's insurgency from the right -- even if it ignores Mr. Bush's 1980 defeat. Trappings of Presidency

Another part of the strategy is to drive home the theme of Presidential leadership, using the trappings of his office. Today's commercial is the second shot in the White House. An earlier one this week emphasized Mr. Bush's leadership in the war against Iraq last year.

Mr. Buchanan, the conservative columnist who is Mr. Bush's only Republican opponent, admits that the President has an enormous advantage from his incumbency, which enables him to command headlines and television time and draw big crowds.

But the first part of the strategy may prove more challenging, in part because conservative Republicans count Mr. Bush as a moderate apostate and in part because he has, in effect, begun a campaign against the George Bush of 1980.

After withdrawing from the Presidential stakes then, Mr. Bush swung his policies sharply to the right to become Mr. Reagan's running mate. In 1988, Mr. Bush won the New Hampshire primary by presenting himself as a more moderate, but still conservative, alternative to candidates like Jack F. Kemp Jr., Bob Dole and Pat Robertson, the television evangelist.

Now Mr. Bush is sounding more conservative than ever, campaigning against government that is too big and expensive and for welfare reform, deregulation and family values.

Burton Yale Pines, vice president of the Heritage Foundation, a conservative research organization, said that in recent days, Mr. Bush had also been telling conservative Republicans that his Administration was going to start enforcing the Supreme Court's 1988 "Beck decision," which prohibits unions from collecting dues from nonmembers for anything other than collective bargaining.

"There's a whole range of conservative things that Bush could have done two or three years ago and is only doing just now," Mr. Pines said. "I'm glad he's deciding to cut regulations, but the taxes he is cutting are the taxes that he raised. The regulations he wants to reduce are the regulations he increased. The deficit he wants to cut is the deficit he raised."

Charles R. Black Jr., senior adviser to the Bush campaign, insisted that Mr. Bush ran on a conservative platform in 1988 and delivered on as much of it as he could."Pat Buchanan, who has undertaken an insurgent conservative candidacy, has only been able to identify two issues that he harps on: the tax increase and the civil rights bill," Mr. Black said.

He argued that Mr. Bush accepted a tax increase in 1989 only to get a compromise on spending cuts with Congressional Democrats and that Mr. Bush delayed signing a job discrimination bill until he believed it would not establish quotas. Avoiding Ideological War

Clearly, the Bush campaign does not want the Republican primary in New Hampshire to be an ideological battleground. "There aren't many people who are for Pat because they don't think Bush is conservative enough," Mr. Black said. "They are dissatisfied with the economy and want to cast a protest vote."

But Mr. Bush's appeal to the right is evident. And conservatives like Mr. Pines, who probably would not like Mr. Bush no matter what he said, are not buying it. "We're all full of love in our heart for the repentant sinner," he said, "but there is reason for us to be skeptical of this."

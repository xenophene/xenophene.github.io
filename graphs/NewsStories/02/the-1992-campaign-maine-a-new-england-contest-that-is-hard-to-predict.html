THE 1992 CAMPAIGN: Maine; A New England Contest That Is Hard to Predict

By B. DRUMMOND AYRES Jr
Published: February 22, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

When it comes to the spring Presidential caucuses and primaries, a case can be made that as New Hampshire goes, so goes Maine.

That at least has been the political way of things in recent election years, particularly on the Democratic side. But when Democrats and Republicans caucus all across Maine on Sunday to express their Presidential nominating preferences for 1992, the results seem at this point to be anything but inevitable, especially on the Democratic side.

In other words, there is no guarantee for former Senator Paul E. Tsongas of Massachusetts, who won the New Hampshire primary on Tuesday, that Maine will also make their fellow New Englander a winner.

Not only do Maine voters leave the impression that they are in an unpredictable and irascible mood, like voters everywhere, but the caucus process itself also adds to the uncertainty. Candidates can pack the caucuses with their supporters, bringing results that may not reflect the true feelings of the general electorate. Organization's the Thing

Although the candidates are spending less time here than in other, more delegate-rich states, their aides are aggressively lining up backers. The outcome may well depend more on organization than message, money or media, which was not the case in New Hampshire.

"I wouldn't bet a penny on anything; not this year," said Jo Karr, the chairwoman of Maine's Democratic Party. "You can't control it. You can't buy it with television. You can't predict it. You can't rely on what's happened in the past, on what they did over in New Hampshire -- certainly not this year, not the way people are feeling."

Every campaign organization is mostly working phones and sending out letters. Very few campaign advertisements are showing up on television or in newspapers. And because the candidates themselves are mostly elsewhere, there is little campaign news on the air or on the front page.

"A caucus state is a little like a duck on water," Ms. Karr said. "It's hard to tell how much paddling is going on." Bush Victory Is Expected

Political professionals say President Bush, who has a summer home down the coast in Kennebunkport and is regarded by many people in Maine as one of them, will win the Republican contest easily.

But given the weakness he showed in New Hampshire, the experts wonder whether a significant number of Maine Republicans might opt to send a message of discontent by voting for "uncommitted" in their caucuses.

And the political professionals wonder whether Mr. Bush's Republican challenger, Patrick J. Buchanan, who has no visible campaign organization in the state, might nevertheless embarrass the President anew by attracting some insurgent support.

"Nobody expects the President to show badly," said Edward O'Meara, the chairman of Maine's Republican Party. "There's no real evidence of any Buchanan organization out there, not even a phone number. But you never know for sure."

On the Democratic side, the outcome of the caucus is anybody's guess.

At first glance, Mr. Tsongas seems almost certain to get a big boost from his victory in neighboring New Hampshire and from the fact that he comes from Massachusetts. If he does not he will be seriously weakened.

"Give us that second booster shot," Mr. Tsongas told voters on Thursday night when he showed up briefly in the state for a rally in Bangor.

While Mr. Tsongas would seem to have a regional advantage in Maine, Gov. Bill Clinton of Arkansas is believed to have the best organization in the state, with four offices and eight paid campaign workers. And Mr. Clinton has decided to break away from campaigning in the South on Saturday for a pre-caucus supper in Lewiston.

"This is retail politics," said David Leavy, Mr. Clinton's field director. "You can't do it from afar. You have to get out and meet the people." Brown Is Running Hard

Adding to the uncertainty of the Democratic contest, former Gov. Edmund G. Brown Jr. of California has been working the state hard, committing himself to five consecutive days of campaigning here this week.

He did better in New Hampshire than most political experts had expected, winning 8 percent of the vote after polls showed him with about half that much. He and a number of experts here believe that his anti-establishment message may resonate well in Maine.

As for the other Democrats in the race, Senator Tom Harkin of Iowa has visited Maine several times, though not this week. There is little expectation that he will finish near the top in the caucuses, but no one is writing him off; his unabashed liberalism has captured the support of much of the state's labor establishment.

Senator Bob Kerrey of Nebraska paid a quick visit to Maine on Wednesday, saying he had come "to let you know I'm by no means out of this race." But his organizers say his expectations here are low.

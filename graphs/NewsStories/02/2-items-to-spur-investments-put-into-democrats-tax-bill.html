2 Items to Spur Investments Put Into Democrats' Tax Bill

By ADAM CLYMER
Published: February 20, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

Responding to protests from their colleagues that their tax plan offered little to stimulate the economy, Democrats on the House Ways and Means Committee modified the measure today to add two provisions to spur investment.

In a two-hour party caucus this morning, complaints rose across a broad spectrum of ideology that the original recommendation of a permanent one percentage point cut in the corporate income tax would not only do little good but would also look bad when compared with the measure's two-year cut in taxes for the middle class.

So the committee Democrats agreed this afternoon to scale back that corporate tax reduction to two years, which put their measure in surplus by $7.1 billion over six years. The tax increases in the measure, primarily increases in the top personal income tax rates, total $93.3 million.

They agreed on two other two-year measures. One was President Bush's proposal for an Investment Tax Allowance permitting increased first-year depreciation for new equipment. The second was a plan to allow small businesses to deduct directly -- instead of depreciating -- up to $25,000 of new investment instead of the current $10,000.

Democrats will discuss the revised plan at another party caucus on Thursday. If the changes are sufficient to satisfy today's critics, they plan to bring the measure to the House floor for a vote next week.

While the Democrats were tinkering, House Republicans were plotting and chortling. Representative Newt Gingrich of Georgia, the No. 2 Republican in the House, said his troops planned to disrupt the House next week if the Democrats stacked procedural rules against them on the tax bill.

Democrats usually arrange House procedures to the disadvantage of the Republicans, who usually just complain. But Mr. Gingrich said his party would do more next week, using tactics he likened to those employed by outraged House members in the last century and by the Rev. Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. in the 20th.

The Republicans, at a caucus of their own, did not discuss adding any provisions to Mr. Bush's seven-point plan that he says is urgently needed to bolster the economy. Mr. Gingrich said that while some members might have suggested adding some form of middle-income tax break of their own, they did not because they enjoyed being in the position of citing the statement of the Democratic winner in the New Hampshire primary, former Senator Paul Tsongas, that he would veto the Democratic bill if he were President. Tsongas Stand Invoked

Representative Jerry Lewis, chairman of the House Republican Conference, called on Democrats to respond to their leader in New Hampshire. Mr. Gingrich said Democrats "refuse to pass a signable growth proposal and will not even write one that their own candidate, Paul Tsongas, would sign."

Mr. Tsongas has been especially critical of the heart of the Democratic measure, two years of tax credits of up to $400 a year for a couple earning $26,000 or more, and up to $200 for individuals earning $13,000 or more.

President Bush said today in Knoxville, Tenn.: "It's a little ironic that the Democratic front-runner, who could well be the party's standard-bearer, opposes what the Democrats in the House of Representatives are doing. I mean they are out of step with their own leader at this point."

House Speaker Thomas S. Foley, asked his view of Mr. Tsongas's stand, said, "I don't agree with it, and the public will decide whether Senator Dukakis is going to be able to veto anything." Mr. Foley later he had not meant to confuse Mr. Tsongas, a former Senator from Massachusetts, with that state's former Governor, Michael S. Dukakis, the Democrats' losing Presidential candidate in 1988. He said: "That was a slip. I'm not snide."

The latest version of the Democratic bill makes no changes in either its tax credit provision, questioned at this morning's caucus because it was temporary, or its plan to index capital gains to inflation, which went largely without criticism today.

The caucus began, several lawmakers reported, with a strong plea for party unity from Mr. Foley, and was punctuated by reminders that the Democrats, having made a fairness argument against the Republicans for months, had to keep on making it.

One of the sharpest criticisms came from Representative David R. Obey of Wisconsin. Later, speaking more mildly, he told reporters that not many people had asked for the corporate rate cut, and that there were better uses for the money. He said the cut had been defended by members of the Ways and Means Committee today as a way to offer something to business in a way that would "fend off worse things that would have made the package vulnerable to a bigger bidding war."

Representative Barney Frank of Massachusetts called the corporate rate issue the major bone of contention at the caucus. He said it "would not be particularly stimulative" and was expensive.

But even the provision's critics said passing the bill quickly was important. "The worst thing is to flounder," said Representative Leon Panetta of California.

"The faster we move the better," said Representative Timothy J. Penny of Minnesota.

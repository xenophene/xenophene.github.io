Iowa, Its Economy Healthy, Scarcely Misses Caucus Blitz

By ISABEL WILKERSON
Published: February 08, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

Just days before the Iowa caucuses, there are no Presidential candidates kissing pigs or courting farmers, no telephone banks digging for supporters and no crowds at the bars. For months, all has been quiet during what is normally a quadrennial bonanza that brings worldwide attention and tens of millions of dollars to the Iowa economy.

But this year, one of the state's Senators, Tom Harkin, is running for the Democratic nomination, so his party's caucuses on Monday are likely to be no contest. The Republican meetings will not even express Presidential preferences. Still, while the state may be feeling a psychological loss, the change has not been the economic blow that many leaders feared.

This year, more than in any recent election year, Iowa can do without the extra money. While much of the rest of the country is mired in recession, Iowa, chastened and wiser after the hard times of the 1980's, has managed to escape it.

The state's unemployment rate, at 4.7 percent, is well below the national average. Farm land values have risen for the fifth straight year. Housing starts are at near record levels. Business bankruptcy filings are half what they were in 1987. And last year more than a dozen companies announced plans to move to Iowa and hire thousands of the state's already hard-to-get workers.

That has put Iowa in stark contrast to the economic misery in New Hampshire, the center of Presidential politics now, and much of the rest of the country, where long lines for a scant supply of jobs have become an enduring symbol of tough times.

And so instead of bemoaning the absence of candidates and reporters on expense accounts, Iowa is counting its good fortune. "We haven't lost anything with the caucus," said Sandi Stamp, coordinator of Iowa Caucus Project, a visitor information office that the Des Moines Chamber of Commerce sets up at caucus time. "That was gravy."

Iowa, like much of the Middle West, took its blows during the early and mid-1980's when high debt and interest rates, repeated droughts and low crop prices forced farm foreclosures and bank failures. Businesses left or laid off workers. Those that survived retooled, automated and began the slow road to recovery, whittling the jobless rate down from a high of 8 percent and investing in equipment and new construction only cautiously.

"All the companies in Iowa that didn't have staying power are gone," said Harvey Siegelman, an economist at the Iowa Department of Economic Development.

What has emerged is an economy with stable, incremental growth rather than wide swings. "Iowa is more a story of flatness than of extremes," said David Allardice, vice president and assistant director of research at the Federal Reserve Bank in Chicago. "We didn't have the speculative excess in the 80's."

Mr. Siegelman added, "The recession of the 80's in Iowa saved us from the national recession of the 90's."

Now much of Iowa is experiencing a sense of understated prosperity. Service sector jobs, fueled by a strong insurance and financial industry in Des Moines, grew 10 percent in the last five years. The rate of growth in the same time period was 7 percent nationwide, Mr. Allardice said. Some Worries Remain

And while the manufacturing sector continued to contract as companies with big Iowa operations like John Deere, the farm equipment manufacturer, and Winnebago, maker of motor homes, found that people in the rest of the country could not afford big-ticket items, manufacturers managed to hold the decline in jobs to less than half a percent last year. Nationwide, manufacturing jobs declined by nearly 5 percent.

Economists do worry about declining pork and beef prices. If the downward trend is prolonged, they say, it could land farmers in deeper debt. But these experts also point out that this is a different Iowa from the one that seemed like one big farm foreclosure only a few years ago, and while the state got a $25 million windfall from the 1988 caucuses, it will do fine without them this year.

Politically, though, it is not such a different Iowa. In the caucus system, people attending meetings in their voting precincts express Presidential preferences by selecting delegates to county, district and state party conventions. Since the 1970's, when the caucuses began attracting national attention as the first measure of strength in Presidential elections, many people have become savvy participants or spectators, and for them, there is a sense of loss.

Guido Fenu, owner of a Des Moines restaurant that has been a gathering place for political and press entourages, went to France for the Olympics this week rather than watch nothing happen in Iowa.

Lowell Junkins, the Democratic candidate for governer in 1986, said he did not even know what time his caucus would start. "Whatever we do, it doesn't count," he said.

And Mike Lavalle, another Des Moines restaurant owner who remembers desperate camera crews videotaping meat cooking on his grill, is experiencing caucus withdrawal, characterized by a burning desire to see satellite transmitting trucks on Locust Street.

"Before, I could look at a candidate and say, 'I know what he looks like at breakfast and what he looks like at the end of the day,' " Mr. Lavalle said. "You feel disenfranchised. You feel like a regular citizen."

Though the caucuses are not bringing in visitors, the better times are bringing back many of the Iowans who defected to Texas, California and elsewhere in the 1980's.

"Things have turned around," said G. David Hurd, chairman of The Principal Financial Group. The insurance and financial services company has added 300 to 500 new positions each year since 1988 and has changed the Des Moines skyline with an ambitious building program that included a 44-story office building. "People are coming back because they can find work," Mr. Hurd said.

By the end of last year, the unemployment rate in Des Moines had dropped to 3.8 percent, and now there is an air of quiet efficiency and assuredness in the office buildings and in the downtown "skywalk" network that connects them by way of raised, enclosed walkways.

When employers have a job opening, instead of finding long lines of applicants as is the case in hard-hit New Hampshire and other regions, employers in Iowa worry if they can find enough people to fill their openings. "Hardly anybody's coming in for applications," said Chip Human, general manager of the Fairfield Inn in West Des Moines. Employers Sharing Workers

In some cases, employers are finding they must share their employees with other companies because many applicants are merely looking for supplementary income, not a job to put food on the table.

The Residence Inn, down the block from the Fairfield, just hired a man who is an auditor at an insurance company during the day to be its night desk clerk. Most of the other applicants for the position also had other jobs.

"People in Des Moines aren't really looking for work," said Lissa Helling, the hotel's general manager. "It's just not a reality for us. I guess we're very fortunate."

Indeed, Des Moines is making money, but it's just not the same without the caucuses, people say. This week, instead of opening its doors to the men who would be leader of the free world, Des Moines is the site of the Iowa Pork Convention and 37th annual Farm Machinery Show.

"The element of celebrity status, having the whole world watching, having Diane Sawyer in our skywalk system, you miss that," said Debra Steilen, spokeswoman for the Greater Des Moines Convention and Visitors Bureau. "But we didn't hold our breath waiting for that to happen again."

How Bush Can Save Himself

By Edwin Meese 3d
Published: February 20, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

The New Hampshire results are the wake-up call the Bush Administration needs. The President's political survival now demands that he return to the conservative principles on which he campaigned in 1988.

The message could not have been clearer: Washington caused the recession, and voters expect leadership that will undo the damage and put people back to work. The President must first announce a detailed economic game plan. Then he must persuade voters he intends to stick to it.

Getting tough with Patrick Buchanan is not the answer. Mr. Buchanan wouldn't even be in the race if the Administration hadn't abandoned policies that won the election, continuing the Reagan legacy. Instead of swatting at his challenger, the President needs to get tough with his own team.

The best way he can do that is to finish making changes in his starting lineup. He has already changed his catcher, bringing in Sam Skinner as chief of staff, and increased his batting strength, with Clayton Yeutter heading his domestic policy staff. Now it's time to replace his starting pitcher, Richard Darman, the budget director.

Mr. Darman's fingerprints on the recession are unmistakable. He persuaded Mr. Bush to abandon his campaign pledge and go along with a record tax increase in the 1990 budget deal. Today, the economy is floundering, tax revenues have declined and the deficit has mushroomed. Mr. Darman has been wrong about virtually every major economic indicator for the last two years.

Mr. Bush must also replace jumbled economic policies with a conservative philosophy that emphasizes the health of the American family and economy. Washington's unrelenting reliance on tax increases and spending reveals little sympathy for the pressures on the family. In his State of the Union Message, the President proposed modest tax relief for middle-income families, but even that promise has been shelved. He must first end the growing tax bias against families. In 1948, the average family of four paid virtually no income tax, because of the personal exemption. Today it pays an average of 24 percent of its income, as taxes and inflation have nearly annihilated the exemption.

The most direct way to provide tax relief for families is through a tax credit, a dollar for dollar reduction in taxes. Mr. Bush proposed a $1,000 tax credit for pre-school children in 1988. After three years of declining fortunes, here's a better idea: a $1,000 credit for each child between 6 and 18, and $1,500 for children under 6. Phased in over five years, this would reduce the typical family's tax burden to what it was a generation ago.

He must also press Congress to cut the capital gains tax to 15 percent. Liberals say this is a sop to the rich. Let them. This is the time for confrontation with Congress, not accommodation. The current tax of 28 percent discourages investment and job creation, as Paul Tsongas, the Democratic front-runner, readily affirms.

Other business taxes should likewise be cut. When businesses invest in new equipment, they must write off the cost over a period of years. But even modest inflation erodes the deduction's value; this discourages investment. Indexing the deductions for inflation would encourage investment.

Also helpful would be a reduction in the Social Security payroll tax -- this could be done without hurting the long-term integrity of Social Security -- as well as expanded eligibility rules for individual retirement accounts. Allowing all Americans to own I.R.A.'s would step up the flow of capital needed to generate growth.

Opponents of tax cuts say they could fuel the deficit. But not if Washington learns to control spending. Instead of allowing domestic spending to rise at 10 percent a year, as it did in fiscal 1991 and 1992, Washington should hold non-defense increases to 4 to 5 percent, slightly above inflation.

If the White House needs an economic vision, this is it. With a serious effort to curb excessive regulation and a promise to steer clear of anything that even looks like a budget "summit," the President would have a program to present to the voters. Without one, New Hampshire could be the first of many wake-up calls.

Edwin Meese 3d, Ronald Reagan fellow in public policy at the Heritage Foundation, was Attorney General in the Reagan Administration.

THE 1992 CAMPAIGN: Voters; The Final Phase: Getting the Faithful to the Polls

By RICHARD L. BERKE
Published: February 18, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

The New Hampshire primary campaign today entered its final and most critical phase: the battle over turning a preference for a candidate into an actual vote.

After all the commercials, all the rallies, all the house parties, it is all but too late for the Presidential candidates to win significant new support. The object now is for the candidates to persuade their faithful, and more importantly, those who may be wavering, to vote Tuesday.

"The question now is, 'Can they get their voters out?' " said Robert E. Craig, chairman of the political science department at the University of New Hampshire. "The thing to do is to keep bothering people until they vote. A candidate's personality itself just isn't going to pull anyone to the polls."

To that end, Senator Tom Harkin of Iowa devoted as much time today rallying volunteers as talking to voters. "Don't leave anyone behind," he implored his troops at his campaign office here this morning. "Don't leave anyone at home." Busing in Volunteers

The candidates were busing in hundreds of volunteers from their home states to help them pull, and in some cases drag, their supporters to the polls. Senator Bob Kerrey of Nebraska even offered baby-sitting services for people so they could vote.

Since New Hampshire is a relatively small state, with 511,307 registered voters, even a few thousand votes can make a difference.

"There aren't that many voters here," said Richard F. Winters, a professor of government at Dartmouth College. "The larger the number of candidates, the easier the case can be made that organizing to get out the vote is important."

Volunteers waving their candidates' placards popped up in the most remote parts of the state. While there is no evidence that posters translate into votes, Paul Johnson, Mr. Kerrey's campaign director here, said that at the very least "the old visibility trick" boosts morale among workers. 'You Bring Them Out'

Even more important are the phone calls the campaigns are making behind the scenes. After hundreds of thousands of phone calls, most of the campaigns have identified people who say they will vote for their candidate. They are now concentrating on calling those people again today, and as many as three times on Tuesday, to make sure they vote.

Robert (Skinner) Donahue, a veteran of get-out-the-vote drives since the Presidential campaign of George S. McGovern in 1972, said the process had not changed much. "You call them," he said. "You identify them. You bring them out. It's not different from what people have been doing 20 years in New Hampshire."

Mr. Donahue, who is helping the campaign of former Senator Paul E. Tsongas, said the emphasis through Tuesday was for the phone callers to "talk to the faithful, to the church people who are already in."

The telephone script followed today by Kerrey volunteers was typical. People who are undecided or leaning to Mr. Kerrey were told, "I'm urging you to vote for Bob on Tuesday because Democrats have to nominate someone who can win for a change." Victims of a Barrage

The badgering does not always help.

"Most people are tired of being asked who they are voting for," said Kathleen Ley, a senior at Providence College in Rhode Island after several hours of phone calls on behalf of Mr. Harkin. "They don't want to hear it any more. They've been barraged."

The degree to which a campaign can get out the vote depends on the depth and strength of its organization, which can be almost impossible to gauge. Political strategists around the state generally credit the Tsongas campaign with being the most visible. That is in large part because Mr. Tsongas is from neighboring Massachusetts and could easily import volunteers.

Mr. Kerrey is considered to have one of the strongest voter identification efforts. He inherited the foundation of the network set up for former Senator Gary Hart of Colorado, who won the primary in an upset in 1984.

Gov. Bill Clinton of Arkansas does not have as strong ties to New Hampshire, but his campaign benefits from having more money than the others. That pays for such enticements as distributing videotapes of Mr. Clinton discussing the issues to 20,000 households where voters were thought to be leaning toward him.

Michael Whouley, a Clinton campaign official, said he thought of the video idea as a way to make the voter drive "intimate and personal." A Lift From Labor

Senator Harkin has got a lift from some labor groups that have dispatched volunteers to encourage fellow union members to support him.

Former Gov. Edmund G. Brown Jr. of California is running a bare-bones campaign with no visible voter mobilization efforts.

While President Bush focused on the primary relatively late, he benefits from a longtime connection to New Hampshire, where he has run in past campaigns for President and Vice President.

Mr. Bush's challenger, the columnist Patrick J. Buchanan, has a much less developed effort but is counting on a strong showing from conversatives.

Republicans, who can vote only in their own party's primary, make up the largest voter pool, with 210,106 registered voters. In the second largest pool are 150,841 "undeclared voters," or independents, who are not bound to a party and can vote in either the Democratic or the Republican primary. There are nearly as many registered Democrats, 150,360, and they can vote only in their party's primary.

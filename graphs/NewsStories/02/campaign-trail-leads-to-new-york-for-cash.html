Campaign Trail Leads To New York for Cash

By MICHAEL SPECTER
Published: February 16, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

In the last desperate days before the New Hampshire primary, as each candidate spins wildly from one quaint village to the next, often it is cocktails in Manhattan that is really on their minds.

"It's cash," said Stanley Greenberg, a poll taker for Gov. Bill Clinton of Arkansas, who traveled with the candidate to a New York fund-raiser on Monday. "Everyone needs a lot of it, and a lot of it is here. After New Hampshire, the campaign goes on. But if you can't buy a plane ticket it's hard to get your message out."

So even in the busiest week of the campaign year so far, when every New Hampshire minute counts for every candidate, Governor Clinton and his wife found time to spend an evening in New York. So did Senator Bob Kerrey of Nebraska, who showed up on Tuesday at the Regency Hotel, also with hat in hand. Senator Tom Harkin of Iowa was here the week before. And the new leader in the polls, Paul Tsongas, found time to attend six events in New York over the last two weeks. Hoping for a shower of celebrity if he wins in New Hampshire, Mr. Tsongas will return to the city the minute the primary ends. The Money Crowd

"I hate to sound like that song," said Jim Armenakis, the New York State chairman for the Tsongas campaign. "But if you can make it here, you can make it anywhere." That may or may not be true. But if you cannot make it in New York -- at least with the money crowd that campaigns need to prosper -- you certainly cannot make it in other places for long. New York and Los Angeles are the mother lodes for campaign fund-raising, but when you are unable to stray far from New Hampshire the money primary takes place mostly in New York.

And although a candidate has to win votes to become a nominee, the money primary is at least as important as any other aspect of the race. With more than $5 million in the bank, Governor Clinton has emerged as the only candidate with enough money to cruise -- at least financially -- out of New Hampshire. His opponents have reported surges in contributions over the last two weeks as polls showed Governor Clinton's campaign faltering, and they hope that a good showing this week, will turn the faucets on to full force.

Even the amount raised by the Clinton campaign, a treasure-trove by the standards of his competitors, could evaporate in a few weeks of bitter campaigning in expensive states like Texas or Florida. The steady need for cash has turned New York, and its wealthy investors, into hallowed ground.

The figures for January have not yet been reported to the Federal Election Commission, but by the end of December Governor Clinton had opened a huge financial lead on his competitors, having raised more than $3.2 million. Campaign officials said that by the end of January the figure had grown to more than $5 million -- almost twice as much as the $2.2 million reported in December by the next most successful fund-raiser, Senator Harkin.

Senator Kerrey had raised slightly less than $2 million, according to the F.E.C., and Mr. Tsongas barely more than $1 million. By comparison, Michael Dukakis had raised well over $10 million by this point in 1988, and President Bush has already reported contributions of more than $9 million for his re-election bid.

Nearly a third of campaign contributions comes from dinners, breakfasts, drinks and "house parties" in New York, political consultants say. The F.E.C. has not yet released detailed reports on where each candidate's contributions have come from, so it is difficult to assess trends, but the Clinton event at the Sheraton Center Hotel last week, raised nearly $700,000 for the struggling candidate, and Kerrey officials said they raised more than $200,000 on Tuesday at their smaller, more intimate affair.

"New York is the best possible place to do these things," said Norman Adler, a political consultant. "People are concentrated in a relatively small place. There are a lot of big players who can fork out $1,000 whether or not they have committed themselves to one candidate or not. And if you can raise a bunch of money in a single night, you look good on television. It's a little bit of substance and a lot of shimmering."

The recession has made money scarce this year. Wealthy real-estate developers are disappearing fast, and many seem uninterested in supporting Presidential candidates -- reserving their money instead for local leaders who may be better able to scratch their backs in return. The Cuomo Factor

As with every other factor in the race for the White House, the long, dark shadow of Gov. Mario M. Cuomo has also had its effect.

"Of course he's been a factor," said Richard Socarides, the state chairman for the Harkin campaign. "If you are a New York contributor and you think he may run, it can cause you to think twice about giving to the others."

New York's role as electoral money depot does not please everybody. Many politicians view the constant round of fund-raisers as distractions from more traditional political commitments.

"This process stinks," said John Marino, the state Democratic chairman. "They are running all over New Hampshire all day long, and they get on a plane and race to New York for dinner to grovel for money. They hardly see a normal voter. They don't go to subway stops, they go to Wall Street."

New York's Ballot Disgrace

Published: February 23, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

Once again New York stands embarrassed before the nation because of its petty-minded election law. Paul Tsongas, fresh from victory in New Hampshire, now faces the possibility of getting knocked off New York's April 7 Democratic primary ballot because of a legal challenge to his petitions. Patrick Buchanan, meanwhile, is definitely off the G.O.P. ballot, and the state's Republican Presidential primary has been canceled.

Unlike many states, which sensibly reserve a spot on the primary ballot for all serious White House contenders, New York forces candidates to collect petition signatures and then defend them against endless legal challenges invited by the state's arcane petition rules.

Mr. Tsongas is vulnerable because he filed the fewest signatures of any of the five major Democratic candidates, just 3,952 more than the 10,000 required. That's far short of the cushion typically needed for protection from ballot warfare. Bill Clinton, to his great credit, has decided not to pursue a challenge to the Tsongas petitions. But the fringe New Alliance Party vows to press the case.

Mr. Buchanan's problem is even more basic. Thwarted by a state party united behind Mr. Bush and by threatened legal challenges, Mr. Buchanan failed to file slates in any of the state's districts. That handed Mr. Bush the state's 100 Republican delegates without an election.

The State Legislature, which has stalled on reform, now has a duty to restore democracy so that New Yorkers can vote for the same candidates as the rest of the nation. If it acts swiftly, it can pass a law putting all major Presidential candidates on this year's primary ballots.

Ralph Marino, the majority leader of the Republican-controlled Senate, is a supporter of President Bush. He says changing the rules now would be unfair to candidates who ran a successful petition drive. But fairness to candidates is not the main yardstick. The existing rules are unfair to voters who can't register their opinion of leading candidates and to the state itself, which is inevitably harmed when top candidates don't have to address its issues and curry its favor. New York's election law is a destructive national laughingstock.

On My Mind; The California Story

By A. M. ROSENTHAL
Published: February 07, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

Somewhere in this state, somewhere between the Mexican and Canadian borders, there must be Californians so interested in how the New Hampshire primary will go that it will influence their own thinking or voting.

There must be. The press, TV and politicians keep hammering it into our heads that New Hampshire voting is hugely important and will affect the nation, so pay paralyzed attention.

The only thing is, in a week of poking around I have not found a single Californian who is waiting for a cue from New Hampshire -- or anybody who knows such a person.

I found only people who feel that the voters of the most powerful state are fully qualified to choose among Democrats and between Democrat Whoever and President Bush without guidance from one of the smallest states.

Long-time, very long-time, Republicans told me that whatever happens in New Hampshire or any place else, election day will be tight for Mr. Bush in California. They know things are worse in California than in 1988, when he managed to win the state with a 3 percent majority. And they have a strong hunch that given the quizzical way the country looks at Mr. Bush right now, he could not win without this state's electoral votes.

You do not have to be a psychiatrist, just a listener, to discover that some Republicans are not yet putting their full muscle into the campaign. They feel Mr. Bush has been neither ideologically loyal nor polite enough to Ronald Reagan, their hero now and forever.

So Mr. Bush obviously will have to hustle to win in California -- hustle not just for the votes of that middle class everybody loves, but for the money, energy and shove of some of the upper class Californians who may not be as huggable politically anymore, but helped put the President where he is.

As for Democrats, including some leaning to Gov. Bill Clinton, they look at the list of candidates and can't quite believe that's all there is and that is all there is going to be. At every dinner, Democrats make lists of possible added starters. There are no new ones -- just the familiar few Senators (Bentsen, Gore, Bradley) and the one Cuomo. Talk goes on through the evening -- will one of them jump in and deliver the election victory Democrats can almost smell cooking in the oven but are not yet sure Governor Clinton can put on the plate.

Anyway, I am glad I am not a foreign correspondent in the U.S., say from Japan. Imagine trying to explain to readers in Hokkaido what New Hampshire is all about. For instance, why are 125,000 people, the number of Democrats who will vote in the primary and who would fit into Universal Studios tour buses on a good weekend, so important that they have to be interviewed by the press one by one?

How do you explain to Japanese why it should matter in the Napa Valley exactly how many votes are won in New Hampshire by a right-wing columnist with a thing against Zulus?

Statistically it seems strange that anybody thinks that Californians, who after all are smart enough to live in California, would be stupid enough to accept this New Hampshire hype.

In the arithmetic of politics, the opinions of California are more than 13 times as important as those of New Hampshire. California has 54 out of the country's 538 votes in the Electoral College compared to New Hampshire's 4. On election day that is the one, the only bottom line.

Talk about voting power as it ought to be counted, and California is 30 times as important. It has that many times the number of New Hampshire's million-odd.

If troubles are how attention should be judged, California has a variety as full as any state -- unemployment, smog, water feuds, migrant labor, welfare, crime, drugs. And it has an American reality not to be found in 98-percent-white New Hampshire -- burgeoning minorities demanding center stage.

And with all due homage to New Hampshire, California not only has more problems, it has a lot more artists, writers, scientists, engineers and other creators. If California does not move ahead, the chances are the rest of us, including New Hampshire, won't either.

So California is not waiting for New Hampshire to show the way, nor is any other state. That's the lead of the story, in Japanese or in English.

COMPANY NEWS; Prudential Securities Sued by Ex-Worker

By LESLIE WAYNE
Published: February 20, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

A former top executive at Prudential Securities Inc. has contended in a lawsuit that the firm coerced executives to contribute up to 5 percent of their salaries to state and local political campaigns. The charges are also being investigated by the United States Attorney in St. Louis and are the subject of an arbitration hearing by the National Association of Securities Dealers.

In a lawsuit, filed in Federal District Court in Philadelphia, Mark D. Schwartz, a former first vice president and regional manager of Prudential's Philadelphia office, said he had been dismissed for complaining about the forced contributions. He also contended that top Prudential executives had threatened employees with dismissals or the withholding of raises and bonuses if they failed to make contributions to candidates of the firm's choice.

Mr. Schwartz's lawsuit, which was filed in October 1990, was disclosed yesterday in New York Newsday, which reported that former Prudential employees said the contributions were one of several ways the firm sought municipal financing business from state and local governments. Insight on Ties

The suit also sheds light on the tight ties between Wall Street and the world of political fund raising. Wall Street has a long history of contributing to political campaigns to help gain access to future office-holders, as well as to the multimillion-dollar fees earned from municipal-securities underwriting assignments.

While corporate and municipal finance departments have historically donated to candidates of all political persuasions, the most active fund raising is by municipal financiers for state and Federal candidates that can send business their way.

For instance, among the current Presidential candidates, Gov. Bill Clinton of Arkansas turned to the head of the Stephens Inc., a brokerage based in Little Rock, Ark., for aid in raising about $50,000 during his bitterly contested 1990 election campaign for governor.

Senator Bob Kerrey worked for more than a year for a New Jersey municipal securities firm, Printon, Kane Group Inc., after leaving the Nebraska Governor's office, receiving nearly $290,000 in compensation and another $35,000 in campaign contributions for his 1988 Senate race.

But in the case of Prudential, Mr. Schwartz, who set up the firm's municipal finance political action committee, contends in his suit that the firm crossed the line between voluntary and coerced contributions. He said top Prudential executives told members of the firm's public finance departments in Chicago and other cities that they would have neither a "bonus or a job to worry about" if they refused to make contributions to candidates of the firm's choosing, New York Newsday reported.

The article said the threats took place under the tenure of John C. Glidden, head of the public finance department, who recently resigned.

A spokesman for Prudential Securities, James Daniels, said yesterday that the firm disagreed with Mr. Schwartz's accusations and added that unfounded complaints by "disgruntled" former employees were "endemic" in the industry.

Mr. Daniels, however, confirmed that the United States Attorney in St. Louis had asked a grand jury to investigate the matter and that the National Association of Securities Dealers, an industry self-regulatory body, was holding arbitration hearings over Mr. Schwartz's dismissal.

Federal civil rights laws prohibits employers from threatening their workers with termination or other punitive acts to extract political contributions from them.

Stephen B. Higgins, the United States Attorney in St. Louis, would not discuss the matter yesterday. A spokesman for the securities dealers' association said the situation was still an "open case," declining to comment further.

Mr. Schwartz could not be reached for comment yesterday, and his lawyer, David A. Gradwohl of the Philadelphia firm of Pelino & Lentz, would not comment.

THE 1992 CAMPAIGN: Maine; Brown and Tsongas Divide Democratic Spoils in Maine

By B. DRUMMOND AYRES Jr
Published: February 24, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

Former Gov. Edmund G. Brown Jr. of California won, at the least, a symbolic victory today in Maine's Presidential caucuses, vying unexpectedly for first place with former Senator Paul E. Tsongas of Massachusetts as the final votes were counted.

Gov. Bill Clinton of Arkansas, originally thought to be Mr. Tsongas's main opposition, finished well back, struggling with "uncommitted" to eke out a distant third place.

With 97 percent of the state's 500 or so caucuses reporting by 10 P.M., Mr. Brown and Mr. Tsongas each had about 30 percent of the vote. Mr. Clinton and "uncommitted" each had about 15 percent.

State Democratic officials said late tonight that the vote count would take several more days to complete because of the remoteness of some of the caucus sites and because the results had run so consistently close.

The winner in the end will almost certainly have only a razor-thin edge, so the final results are likely to do little to clear up where the 1992 Presidential contest is headed. Still, it seemed clear that Mr. Clinton and Mr. Tsongas had stumbled a bit while Mr. Brown, previously regarded as a fringe candidate, had at last found a sounding board for his "outsider" message that money is the root of all political evil.

At stake in Maine are 23 delegates to the national nominating convention. The delegates will be chosen at a May state convention by some 3,000 delegates elected in today's caucuses, which attracted about 15,000 voters or 5 percent of the state's registered Democrats.

The former California Governor campaigned heavily here after doing better than expected in the New Hampshire primary on Tuesday. Besides his outsider message, he stressed environmental issues, especially his opposition to nuclear power. That struck a sympathetic chord in Maine, which has many new residents attracted to the state by its outdoors.

"Jerry Brown had the right message in the right state and he was the right messenger," Ken Dean, the co-director of the Brown campaign here, said as the final votes trickled in.

Mr. Tsongas made a major effort in the caucuses here over the past month or so and had called upon Maine voters this week to give him a "booster shot" after his first-place finish in New Hampshire.

"We're satisfied even if Brown did run well," said Bin McCarthy, one of Mr. Tsongas's top advisers in New England. "We thought Clinton was going to take it." Easy Victory for Bush

Mr. Clinton also made a major Maine effort. One of his leading supporters, Mayor Tom Allen of Portland, tried to put the best face on today's outcome. "We think we did well," he said. "He got additional delegates. He's running a national campaign and couldn't spend but so much time here. Jerry Brown spent all of his time but at the expense of other states."

Until today's caucuses, many Democratic leaders around the country had considered Mr. Brown, at best, a political nuisance, a candidate wih impractical ideas. Some called him "Moonbeam.

"Now it's Jerry Brown who will get a nice bump out of all this," said Jo Karr, the chairman of Maine's Democratic Party. "A lot of people had written him off but now they're going to have to take another look."

Maine Republicans also caucused today, giving President Bush an easy victory.

Actually, the Republicans' nominating effort in the state, in which 1,200 delegates are sent to a May state convention that will then send 22 delegates to the national convention, is spread over weeks. But most town and county meetings were held today and party officials said Mr. Bush won about 9 of every 10 delegates at stake. Patrick J. Buchanan, the columnist and television commentator who embarrassed the President by winning nearly 40 percent of the vote in New Hampshire, made no effort here and picked up less than 5 percent of the support. 2 Embarrassed Candidates

Mr. Tsongas had hoped that his victory on Tuesday in the New Hampshire primary, combined with his New England roots, would lift him to an easy victory here and propel him toward still other victories in the contests soon to come in Maryland and farther south.

Mr. Clinton had hoped to embarrass Mr. Tsongas in his backyard and soften him for a speedy political kill in Maryland and elsewhere. Instead, Mr. Brown embarrassed both men.

Whatever the final reading on who won and who lost in Maine, the outcome of the contest served notice on both Mr. Tsongas and Mr. Clinton that they have unexpected political problems.

First, there was the matter of message.

Though the Maine caucuses are not necessarily a gauge of nationwide sentiment -- Jesse Jackson finished a strong second here in 1988 -- Mr. Brown's message obviously was well received here, leaving Mr. Tsongas and Mr. Clinton to wonder where their messages had fallen short.

Next, there was organizing.

Both Mr. Tsongas and Mr. Clinton knew from the outset, when they committed themselves to major efforts in Maine, that organization can be as much a key to caucus victories as message, media and money. And yet in the end, Mr. Brown, a candidate thought to have a limited organization, put together an effective one.

An aggressive campaign organization can often pack caucuses with a candidate's supporters, thereby demonstrating more strength than the candidate actually has in the electorate at large.

Mr. Clinton had opened four offices around the state and had moved in eight paid workers. He made a number of visits, including an appearance Saturday night that he scheduled at the last minute.

Mr. Tsongas also paid a number of visits to the state, one of them this morning just as the caucuses were beginning. His volunteers poured into Maine from Massachusetts in the final days.

Mr. Brown relied heavily on volunteers, too. But the heart of his campaing was a weeklong, dawn-to-dusk tour of the state, making personal appearances wherever he could gather a handful of people or an auditorium full.

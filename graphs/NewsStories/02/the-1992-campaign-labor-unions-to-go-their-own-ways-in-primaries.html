THE 1992 CAMPAIGN: Labor; Unions to Go Their Own Ways in Primaries

By PETER T. KILBORN
Published: February 20, 1992

Correction Appended

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

Divided over Gov. Bill Clinton of Arkansas and Senator Tom Harkin of Iowa, the nation's leading labor unions decided today to go their own ways in choosing whom to back in the Democratic Presidential primaries, at least until a political consensus forms.

Leaders of 32 unions that make up the executive council of the American Federation of Labor and Congress of Industrial Organizations are meeting here this week to set their agenda.

Until now, the individual unions had agreed to stay neutral. Today, their leaders said the unions could begin throwing volunteers and dollars behind whatever candidate they choose.

Richard L. Trumka, president of the United Mine Workers, said he backed Mr. Harkin but was concerned that in finishing fourth in New Hampshire his campaign had been badly managed.

The most enthusiastic of the union presidents here are Governor Clinton's backers, like Gerald McEntee of the American Federation of State, County and Municipal Employees, with 1.2 million members the A.F.L.-C.I.O.'s second-biggest union after the Teamsters. Around the Table

The union presidents met privately today, and people who attended said the A.F.L.-C.I.O.'s president, Lane Kirkland, went around the table sounding out each on their preferences and on abandoning the federation's neutrality.

Mr. Kirkland said later that 5 unions said they were still polling their members, 14 said they supported Senator Harkin and 3 were for Governor Clinton.

Jack Sheinkman, president of the Amalgamated Clothing and Textile Workers Union, said that he, like most, spoke up for Senator Harkin, whose positions on issues most coincide with those of the unions.

But three important unions -- Mr. McEntee's public employees union, the American Federation of Teachers and the Hotel and Restaurant Employees -- named Governor Clinton.

Mr. Harkin was thus left short of the two-thirds approval of the rank and file that he needed to win endorsement by the federation.

Former Senator Paul E. Tsongas of Massachusetts received support only as a last-resort alternative to President Bush. At an A.F.L.-C.I.O. convention in November, Mr. Tsongas said he opposed one of labor's top priorities, a law prohibiting employers from dismissing strikers.

Union leaders showed little interest in the two other Democratic contenders, Senator Bob Kerrey of Nebraska and former Gov. Edmund G. Brown Jr. of California.

Correction: February 21, 1992, Friday An article yesterday about an A.F.L.-C.I.O. meeting misidentified one of three unions there that said they would support Gov. Bill Clinton for President. It is the Retail, Wholesale and Department Store Union, not the Hotel and Restaurant Employees Union, which has remained neutral. The article also misstated the number of unions not yet committed to a Presidential candidate. Fifteen of the 32 at the meeting remain uncommitted.

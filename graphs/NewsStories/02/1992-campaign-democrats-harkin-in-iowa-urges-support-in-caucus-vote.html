1992 CAMPAIGN: Democrats; Harkin, in Iowa, Urges Support in Caucus Vote

By RICHARD L. BERKE
Published: February 09, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

Declaring that "Iowa still has the first shot that's going to be heard across the country," Senator Tom Harkin crisscrossed this state today, imploring Democrats to vote on Monday in the Iowa caucuses, the first electoral contest in the 1992 Presidential campaign.

Senator Harkin has been repeating a similar line to audiences in New Hampshire, which will hold the first primary a week later. "The people of New Hampshire," he said the other day in Portsmouth, "are going to fire the shot heard around the world."

The truth is, the more important shot for Mr. Harkin's survival as a Presidential candidate is the one that will be heard in New Hampshire. Because he is the favorite-son candidate here, no other Democrats are seriously competing in Iowa, and there is little question that the Senator will win.

His priority is apparent from his schedule: Senator Harkin's aides have calculated that since announcing his candidacy on Sept. 15 he has campaigned 9 days in Iowa and 44 days in New Hampshire. While an overwhelming victory here would affirm loyalty to Mr. Harkin among Iowa Democrats, it would probably not give his national campaign a lift. Decisive Victory Sought

Even so, his advisers worry that a less-than-decisive victory in Iowa could be devastating for his chances in New Hampshire, where polls show Mr. Harkin is in third or fourth place.

So the Harkin campaign is running phone banks, sending out mailings and staging rallies with the Senator all weekend in an effort to convince supporters that the caucuses are important after all. The campaign estimates that its organizational effort here has cost $200,000 to $250,000.

"People say, 'Why are you here, Harkin? Why aren't you in New Hampshire?' " Mr. Harkin told supporters in the central Iowa town of Fort Dodge this morning. "I say: 'Look, you've got to understand something. There's a bond between me and my brothers and sisters in the state of Iowa.' I'm here to tell you I don't take you for granted. What happens here Monday is much larger than just your home-state boy running."

This afternoon, at the Siouxland Senior Center in this city in western Iowa, Mr. Harkin received a standing ovation after telling the residents, "Think how proud you're going to be one year from now when a son of Iowa, someone you know, someone who has your values, takes the oath of office as the President of the United States."

In a letter mailed last week to 60,000 Iowans who have been identified as frequent caucus participants, Mr. Harkin, who was elected to a second term in 1990 with 54 percent of the vote, was even more urgent on pressing Democrats not to brush off the caucuses.

"Forget conventional wisdom," he wrote. "It will not be easy for our campaign to 'win' the Iowa caucus. The media have placed enormous expectations on our performance. These 'experts' say we must win by a tremendous margin to be the 'real winner.' "

Harkin advisers said that to meet the media's expectations the Senator needed to break the record of support for President Jimmy Carter, who beat Senator Edward M. Kennedy of Massachusetts in the 1980 Iowa caucuses with 59 percent of the vote.

But a poll published today by The Des Moines Register shows that Senator Harkin was not doing as well here as his backers had hoped. While he still is the clear winner among Democrats likely to attend the caucuses, his level of support was 54 percent. Gov. Bill Clinton of Arkansas had 16 percent; Senator Bob Kerrey of Nebraska, 8 percent; former Gov. Edmund G. Brown Jr. of California, 5 percent, and former Senator Paul E. Tsongas of Massachusetts, 5 percent. Twelve percent were undecided.

In a similiar poll last December, Mr. Harkin won the backing of 68 percent, followed by Mr. Kerrey with 10 percent and Mr. Clinton, with 5 percent.

Mr. Harkin's solitary campaign here is a far cry from this day four years ago, when 11 candidates, Democrats and Republicans, barnstormed the state to win over uncommitted voters.

Among Republicans, there is even less activity this year than among Democrats. Republicans have decided not to hold a straw poll at the start of their caucuses, apparently fearing that such a tally could diminish President Bush's showing. Neither of the two Republican insurgents, Patrick J. Buchanan and David Duke, have mounted challenges here. Low Turnout Predicted

The absence of a contest is likely to be apparent when the caucuses are held Monday evening. John Roehrick, chairman of the Iowa Democratic Party, predicted that his party's turnout would range from 30,000 to 70,000, compared to the record attendance of 120,000 in 1988. The lower the turnout, he said, the more Mr. Harkin will benefit because most of the hard-core caucus loyalists support the Senator.

Though Mr. Harkin is the favority son, some politically active Democrats in Iowa say they are bitter that his candidacy has usurped the limelight here, particularly when his campaign has yet to take off in New Hampshire, and are fearful that the state party will suffer in November as a result of low turnout at the caucuses.

"I think he's on a wild goose chase and Iowa Democrats are going to suffer because of it," said Ned Chiodo, a Des Moines lobbyist who has backed Mr. Harkin for the Senate but not for the White House. Without meaningful caucuses here, he said, the state party has lost its best vehicle for organizational and fund-raising drives, as well as its ability to attract candidates for local offices.

Mr. Roehrick said it was short-sighted for people to complain about the lack of competitive caucuses here. "If Mike Dukakis had won the White House, we'd be in the same position," the party chairman said. "No one would be looking at us. We're going to be back in '96."

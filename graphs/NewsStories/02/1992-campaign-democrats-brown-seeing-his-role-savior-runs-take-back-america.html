THE 1992 CAMPAIGN: Democrats; Brown, Seeing His Role as Savior, Runs to 'Take Back America'

By KAREN DE WITT
Published: February 17, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

Edmund G. Brown Jr. sees himself as a savior, his cause summed up in the title of the half-hour documentary-style political commercial that he is presenting to New Hampshire voters on cable television: "Take Back America."

So despite what naysayers argue or what public opinion polls show, Mr. Brown, the former Governor of California, is out on the hustings, pushing his anti-money, anti-big politics messages with the fervor of a plaid-shirted evangelist.

He does not want to speculate about the polls, who is ahead, who is behind, and whether he has any chance of becoming the Democratic nominee. This is all "a game set up by the corrupt political system and the media" that he continually inveighs against, he said in a recent interview. 'I'm in It Until the End'

"I'm in it until the end," he said. "I'm going to Maine, Maryland. I have a full slate of Maryland delegates. My strategy is to present as clearly as I can the truth of our condition in the world."

Mr. Brown is outraged and offended, ready and willing to wrench democracy away from the forces of corruption that he contends have captured it, though those same forces are the ones that once bankrolled the campaigns that made him a two-term Governor of California, from 1974 to 1983.

Mr. Brown is consumed with a desire to serve whether anyone in the political establishment wants him to or not, because, he said, "People are coming up to me and beseeching me to do this."

Mr. Brown, who once wrote that he was both attracted and repelled by politics, says he has long been keenly interested in changing the political process. When he first ran for governor in 1974, he campaigned on an anti-lobbyist, anti-big-contribution platform. His latest crusade was born, he said, of his experience as chairman of the California Democratic Party from 1989 to 1991, when he said he discovered that "greed for money" ran the political system at the expense of ordinary citizens.

And he has tried to tap into the general disgruntlement with politics-as-usual with his insurgent campaign, his "800" number for contributions and limitation on donations at $100. Although Mr. Brown trails in public opinion polls, his campaign stops in the civic centers, living rooms and school auditoriums of dark, snowy towns and cities in New Hampshire, come with an enthusiastic, foot-stomping ring.

Mr. Brown spoke earlier this month in Laconia, N.H., at a town meeting in an old factory that is now the state meeting house. Standing on the rough wooden floors, Mr. Brown managed to conjure the air of an old-fashioned New England revival, haranguing his audience about the responsibility to be Thomas Paine's "winter patriots" while painting horrific verbal pictures of corrupt politicians, bloated lobbyists and a tax system that is "a banquet feast of corruption."

"I've always been a believer in the progressive tax, but the tax keeps changing," said Mr. Brown, noting that President Bush is proposing another change in the system. His comments were greeted with applause.

Mr. Brown's own tax proposal is a 13 percent flat tax with only three deductions: for rent, home mortgage and charity. He said the change would keep everyone honest and not give any benefits to wealthy interests. Any loss from the present system, he said, would be made up with a value-added tax, a type of national sales tax.

His talk was strewn with ideas, figures and can-do affirmations. "I believe that we can create jobs," he said. "And we can create jobs now. The country does have the money."

And sprinkled throughout, there were always the assertion that things were not happening because of corruption in politics. "Lobbyists, money, deception, fraud, all of it, O.K., that happened," Mr. Brown said. "I'm here to break up that cozy arrangement."

He disparaged his rivals, hinting at the character questions hanging over Gov. Bill Clinton of Arkansas, and deriding the plan by Senator Tom Harkin of Iowa to invigorate the economy with a highway building program. "We'll still be running Japanese cars down them," he said.

Mr. Brown deals with critics who say he is shrill and impractical by reminding his audience that as governor and an atypical politician, he was considered "a weird, alien being" for reducing his salary and driving a Plymouth instead of a chauffeured limousine.

"I paid my own rent out of my own salary," he said. "Pretty weird, huh?" Fervor Seems Like a Calling

During a question and answer period, a member of the audience asked why it had taken him nearly 20 years to see the faults in the political system and his culpability in it. Mr. Brown compared his position to that of St. Augustine, the North African bishop and saint who led a sinful life before recognizing the error of of his ways.

"I don't intend to become a bishop or a saint," Mr. Brown said. "I just didn't have the will or the insight to declare war on this corrupt incumbent establishment."

Wendy Wilson, a nurse from Gilford who came to the meeting with her two children, liked what she heard. "There's a need for a major overhaul," Ms. Wilson said. Is Mr. Brown the candidate to do it? "I wonder if anyone can do it," Ms. Wilson said.

At times, Mr. Brown's fervor seems almost like a calling -- perhaps a substitute for the priestly vocation abandoned by this ex-Jesuit seminarian?

"I wouldn't call it a call," he said. "That's something to think about. But it's a very powerful feeling."

And if he does not prove successful at the polls? Mr. Brown said he would continue his attacks on government and politics because "enhancing and preserving democracy won't disappear on Nov. 3."

NASA CHIEF QUITS IN POLICY CONFLICT

By WILLIAM J. BROAD
Published: February 13, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

The head of the National Aeronautics and Space Administration, Richard H. Truly, announced his resignation yesterday after a long and often losing battle with the White House over the direction of the nation's space program. Adminstration officials, who spoke on condition of anonymity, said Mr. Truly had been forced to step down.

Mr. Truly, a space pioneer and former astronaut, was chosen to head NASA after the Challenger disaster in January 1986 to try to get the space agency back on its feet. His resignation comes as the agency again faces an uncertain future.

The Administration officials said his departure was prompted by mounting friction with the White House over basic policy issues. The White House opposed building more shuttles, wanted to entertain radical ideas for space exploration and sought to scale back plans for huge science projects meant to expand the frontiers of knowledge. These and other initiatives were general resisted by Mr. Truly and the space agency. He Says He Didn't Have Choice

"Frankly it wasn't what I had planned," Mr. Truly said in a telephone interview with The Associated Press. "It's a situation where the President decided to make a change."

A high NASA official said of the resignation, "This has been in the mill for months."

President Bush said yesterday that the search for a new space agency administrator had begun and that the resignation of Mr. Truly had been accepted regretfully.

NASA said Mr. Truly would remain on the job until April 1. But the agency now finds itself somewhat adrift and with a leadership vacuum. The deputy administrator's slot has been empty since late last year when J. R. Thompson resigned for what he said were personal reasons.

The space agency is at a turning point, most space experts agree. The end of the cold war has diminished its original mission of trying to get a technological upper hand on the Soviets. And a bruising battle in Congress last year to secure financing for the agency's planned $30 billion space station is expected to flare again this year. Some lawmakers who supported the financing last year recently voiced doubts about continuing money for the project in light of the recession and continuing Federal budget deficits.

Most important, the resignation comes just as the agency is set to go before Congress to justify a request for an increase in its budget of about 4 percent for the next fiscal year. That would put the agency's annual budget up to $15 billion.

"It's a bad year to bail out and a bad time of year to bail out because it's right at the beginning of the budget cycle," said John E. Pike, head of space policy for the Federation of American Scientists, a private group in Washington.

A NASA official conceded that it's not a good time for the resignation but added that "Dick will do everything he can to help us get things squared away" before he leaves.

The tenure of Mr. Truly, 55 years old, a retired Navy admiral and the first former astronaut to head the space agency, was marked by two key successes. The agency recovered from the Challenger disaster, having now flown 20 successful missions with the nation's fleet of winged spaceships since then. And it began getting large budget increases for the space station, the centerpiece of the agency's plans.

But the agency also suffered severe setbacks in Mr. Truly's tenure, including bad publicity over repeated launching delays, shuttles that leaked fuel, the discovery of a flawed mirror on the Hubble Space Telescope and, most recently, a stuck antenna that threatens to cut back dramatically on the scientific payoff from the $1.4 billion Galileo spacecraft now on the way to Jupiter.

"Admiral Truly is a good man who did a good job under difficult circumstances," said Senator Al Gore, head of a Senate subcommittee on space. "He leaves with the respect of everyone who worked with him at NASA and in Congress."

A space pioneer, Mr. Truly flew on two early space shuttle missions, including the second flight, in 1981, and became head of the shuttle program shortly after the Challenger explosion, in which seven astronauts died. He is widely credited with getting the space program back on its feet. Under his direction, the shuttles underwent a $2 billion repair program and began flying again in 1988, so far with no in-flight emergencies. Conflict Occurred Early

Mr. Truly became Administrator in July 1989 and almost immediately fell into conflict with the White House and its National Space Council, headed by Vice President Dan Quayle. The council openly and repeatedly criticized NASA programs for taking too long and costing too much and called on the space agency to get new ideas and new technology from universities, Federal laboratories and private business.

The space agency often resisted, with Mr. Truly occasionally taking his opposition public.

Dr. John M. Logsdon, a space policy analyst at George Washington University, said yesterday that Mr. Truly was "a man of high integrity who fought hard for his vision of what was good for the space program."

But Dr. Logsdon added, "That view was not shared by the White House and was not, in my view, an appropriate approach to putting together a space program for the 1990's and beyond."

Basically, he said, Mr. Truly placed central emphasis on the winged spaceships, which he had piloted, at a time when the nation needed to move toward new alternatives. Outside Vision Sought

Mr. Truly's conflicts with the White House included whether outside agencies and experts were needed to help set the nation's space agenda. For instance, the White House became interested in ideas for inflatable space stations space ships, which would cost far less than the rigid metallic structures sought by the space agency.

In 1990, the White House began actively reaching beyond NASA in a search for new ways to advance the nation's manned space program, challenging a near monopoly the agency had held for more than three decades.

In another conflict, the space agency fought bitterly and hard for a huge a series of orbiting platforms, the Earth Observing System, that would carry instruments to study environmental changes in the atmosphere and on earth. But the White House wanted the proposed $30 billion system in smaller pieces to reduce the risk of failure, allow flexibility to change the program in midcourse and possibly send some instruments aloft earlier than first planned.

After many battles, often waged with panels of outside experts, the White House won.

Neither the space agency nor the White House gave a reason for Mr. Truly's resignation. But agency officials said the White House asked Mr. Truly to subject a letter of resignation, which he did on Monday, with Mr. Bush accepting it yesterday,

In a statement, Mr. Bush praised Mr. Truly as a strong leader in during a troublesome time.

"It was under Dick Truly's able leadership and steady hand that NASA was able to rebuild the space shuttle program and return it to safe operation in 1988," he said. "The nation owes Admiral Truly a great debt of gratitude."

Mr. Truly, born in Fayette, Miss., on Nov. 12, 1937, is the eighth administrator of the space agency.

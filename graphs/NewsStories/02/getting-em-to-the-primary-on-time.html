Getting 'em to the Primary on Time

By GLENN RIFKIN
Published: February 13, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

Every four years, when the national spotlight shines on New Hampshire's Presidential primary, Stephen Singer implements his long-held business philosophy: take advantage of the environment around you.

Since 1972, Mr. Singer, executive vice president of Merchants Rent A Car, has been actively campaigning to get Presidential candidates and their workers to rent cars from his family's business. Merchants Rent A Car has rented to politicians on both sides of the aisle, from Ronald Reagan and George Bush to Morris Udall, the former Arizona Congressman who ran for President in the 1970's and won Mr. Singer's lifelong admiration for handing over his personal credit card to insure that Mr. Singer got paid for his cars.

When campaigns were flush with funds, the New Hampshire primary meant about $100,000 in additional revenue for Merchants, which is only a part of the Singer family business. Besides its fleet of 7,000 rental cars and vans, it operates new- and used-car dealerships in southern New Hampshire from its headquarters here near Manchester.

But this year, with the recession, the candidates, the staff members and the press seem to be using fewer cars for shorter amounts of time, Mr. Singer said. Merchants said it would bring in only about $25,000 renting cars and vans to its customers here for the primary. The clients include President Bush, who is also a steady customer when his entourage is in Kennebunkport, Me., and Gov. Bill Clinton of Arkansas. Merchants is also renting to several of the lesser-known candidates (there are 62 announced candidates in the New Hampshire primary) and media crews from the Cable News Network and other television networks.

Surprisingly, the politicians are not insisting on renting American cars. Their chief concern is for cars and vans with air bags on the passenger side, where candidates often ride, Mr. Singer said.

Just how a candidate's staff arranges for a rented car -- and takes care of it -- says a lot to Mr. Singer's experienced eye. The Bush campaign had big plans for a recent weekend and reserved 10 of Merchants' 15 passenger vans. But then it scaled back its order to take the vehicles for one day only, presumably to go easy on the budget, Mr. Singer said. Clinton Plans Ahead

The Clinton campaign, on the other hand, seems to be able to plan ahead and save, Mr. Singer said. It wanted 45 vans, which Merchants usually rents for $100 each a day. But it negotiated early for the vehicles and got a package deal, renting 30 for $60 rates and 15 for $70 a day.

"By far he has the strongest and best organization in the primary," Mr. Singer said of Mr. Clinton. Mr. Singer labels himself an independent.

The Presidential primary means an infusion of cash for businesses throughout the state. George Mandis, general manager of the Center of New Hampshire Holiday Inn in Manchester, believes the primary will pour $10 million into New Hampshire restaurants, hotels, newspapers, broadcasters, rental-car offices and local service businesses like printers.

His hotel, which is the traditional media headquarters during the campaign as well as the home away from home for many of the candidates, is booked solid from early February until the primary next Tuesday. Things are so tight that even Vice President Dan Quayle could not get a room recently. Tighter Travel Budgets

Just how much money pours into the state is hard to measure. The state's Office of Resources and Economic Development would not hazard a guess, nor would Thomas Duffy, a senior planner with the Office of State Planning. Whatever the amount, "it helps," Mr. Duffy said, "but it isn't going to turn our economy around."

Mr. Mandis believes visitors are spending less than in 1988, in part reflecting tighter travel budgets for the news media.

"The television networks used to buy whatever they needed," he said. "Now they either rent it or bring it with them. In 1988 they bought a bunch of brand new refrigerators and then just left them here. You won't see that happen now."

At Merchants Rent A Car, Mr. Singer does not appear particularly concerned about this year's slowdown. Merchants remains profitable, he said, because of its diversity. Though new-car sales in its foreign-car dealerships are "very soft," the used-car and car-rental business is up 50 percent from a year earlier.

Mr. Singer, who runs Merchants along with four younger brothers and a brother-in-law, aggressively pursues new accounts and builds repeat business with a philosophy instilled by his father, Irving, who died last year. The elder Mr. Singer, who once drove chickens from farms to processing plants, opened a used-car dealership in 1962.

When Mr. Singer was finishing business school at New York University in 1971, his father asked him to join the business. One of the son's first ideas was to pursue the primary candidates as customers.

He put together a list of the candidates and wrote letters offering special packages of discount rates and unlimited mileage to the campaign managers. He promised the coveted station wagons, and more recently he offered the vans and mini-vans that are essential to moving armies of volunteers and voters in the weeks leading up to the primary.

Mr. Singer said business in the primary election took off in 1976 when Merchants rented "hundreds of vehicles" to the campaigns of Jimmy Carter, Morris Udall and most of the other leading candidates. "Udall had so many of our vehicles that we said 'if he got elected President, someone from Merchants would be named Secretary of Transportation,' " Mr. Singer recalled.

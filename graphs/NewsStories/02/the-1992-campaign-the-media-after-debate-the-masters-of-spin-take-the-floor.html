THE 1992 CAMPAIGN: The Media; After Debate, the Masters of 'Spin' Take the Floor

By MICHAEL SPECTER
Published: February 18, 1992

Correction Appended

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

For the voters of New Hampshire, the Democratic debate on Sunday offered a final chance to watch five solemn men engage in an intellectual Olympiad, a dense burst of meaning in the waning moments of the primary contest here.

But for the principal architects of campaigns -- the political henchmen, the minders and puppeteers who make their living by calling the Titanic the Love Boat -- the evening offered something better: an opportunity to hype, twist, contort, convulse and, if talent failed, bludgeon a punch-drunk press corps into understanding how only their candidate could possibly have been the winner.

"Nobody watched the first debate," said William Schneider, currently a commentator for the Cable News Network, but a longtime political insider and a historian on this phenomenon of "spin." "Nobody listened. But within two days Senator Paul E. Tsongas had won. Spin did it. Spin is everything."

Spin, the term, derives from sports like pool, basketball or soccer, where the right spin can make the ball go where a player wants it to. Spin, the blatant art of bending the truth, is not new in politics. When, according to legend, young George Washington confessed to chopping down that cherry tree, he inoculated himself against credibility problems by converting a bad deed into humble honesty. Reaching New Heights

This year spin has reached new heights, or torque intensity, as dozens of campaign aides bounce from reporter to reporter like pinballs laced with the antidote to truth serum.

They were deployed, by the squadron, for the debate at Saint Anselm's College. And after the post-debate manipulation was completed, they gathered at the Sheraton Wayfarer Hotel to deliver their spontaneously joyous lines again and again into the morning.

"Obviously it was a wonderful night for Bob Kerrey," said Tad Devine, the Nebraska Senator's campaign manager. He then became the first of four Kerrey staff members to just sort of conclude, in a casual, offhand way, the following:

"For Senator Tsongas though, it was a little tougher. Obviously he was a victim of a debate in New Hampshire where there was a 20-minute discussion of nuclear power. Not a good thing you have to admit."

Mr. Tsongas did not admit it, and Joseph Grandmaison, a former state Democratic chairman who supports Gov. Bill Clinton, said he did not think it would be much of an issue for the voters in New Hampshire.

Early in the evening, shock troops from each camp were inserted into the gymnasium that served as press center for the debate to perform the traditional pre-debate down-hype, a ritual in which the supporters essentially argue that if their man makes it through the evening without drooling he will have triumphed.

"You can't finish third in the election," said Frank Greer, the media consultant for Mr. Clinton. "If nothing had happened to this campaign and we were struggling for second we would be in trouble. But everyone knows that at the beginning of this week Bill Clinton was dead, so if he emerges as a solid No. 2, you have to look at him with some real respect."

In other words, if the Arkansas Governor, once the front-runner in the polls and now second to Mr. Tsongas, does exactly as everyone expects, it can be viewed as a glorious resurrection. That's down-hype. In Conversational Bouquets

There is also gale-force tornado spin. The moment the debate ended, the candidates began to roll into the media center, preceded by spin doctors bearing quotations. Bands of reporters engaged political consultants on the basketball court. They gathered, to use a term Tom Wolfe invented to describe the cocktail habits of the rich and famous, in conversational bouquets. There were dozens of them in the gym, with the spinners in the center and the spun in a circle shoving tape recorders and sound booms in their faces.

The duller the debate, the more powerful the vortex that the spin doctors think they need to create afterwards. Like everything else in politics, the professionals even have a cute name for it up here: Saint Anselm's Disease, a condition whose symptoms include profound and unexpected deep sleep. It was first reported in 1984 after Walter Mondale turned in a particularly sonorous performance at the college.

"I wouldn't call this debate the most electrifying event," said Don Sweitzer, the political director for Senator Tom Harkin of Iowa as he moved into automatic spin mode. "But our guy clearly emerged as the most Presidential. I think the main thing the voters are going to take away is that Tom Harkin, really when you look at it, is the man who has the best chance of moving on."

And he continued, rapidly, seemingly without breathing: "I don't think we have to tell you how badly Paul Tsongas got beaten up on out there tonight. Tsongas is not prepared to move on to the next step. Kerrey doesn't have the organization and Clinton has problems."

They call that overspin, says Michael McCurry, a highly trained Kerrey operative, who has spent much of the last 24 hours dishing out counterspin to offset the efforts of his colleagues, many of whom had gyrated wildly out of control after Senator Kerrey was judged to have made his best appearance yet in such a forum. "We peaked way too soon," Mr. McCurry said. "We should have peaked on Monday afternoon." Each Is 'Most Presidential'

As one might expect, each of the men in the debate emerged as the "most Presidential," in the interpretations offered by every campaign. Only former Gov. Edmund G. Brown Jr., whose spin patrol consists of himself and several California college students, pretends not to care.

"We are taking a message to the people," Mr. Brown said after the debate. "The rest of it really means nothing."

Perhaps the most unusual operatives at the debate were the editors and the publisher of The New Republic magazine. Having vigorously endorsed Senator Al Gore of Tennessee in 1988 to no effect, they have now used their magazine's editorials and cover to promote another Southerner, Mr. Clinton.

"One of these days were going to get it right," said Hendrick Hertzberg, a senior editor of the magazine. "But I think Bill's obituary came out a little early. He was pretty good tonight, don't you think?"

Correction: February 20, 1992, Thursday An article on Tuesday about the molding of political messages by campaign "spin doctors" rendered the name of a senior editor of the New Republic magazine incorrectly. He is Hendrik Hertzberg.

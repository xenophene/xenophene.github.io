THE 1992 CAMPAIGN: New York; Petition Challenge May Keep Tsongas Off the State Primary Ballot

By SAM HOWE VERHOVEK
Published: February 19, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

Even as Paul E. Tsongas basks in his victory in the Democratic Presidential primary in New Hampshire, his prospects of even appearing on the New York State primary ballot in April were thrown into considerable doubt today by a legal challenge to his petitions to enter the contest.

The challenge was filed by the New Alliance Party, a fringe political group. But in a strong indication of the tensions between camps of the first- and second-place finishers in New Hampshire today, the head of Mr. Tsongas's New York campaign charged that the group had acted under the "direction and orchestration" of aides to Gov. Bill Clinton of Arkansas.

Regardless of who was masterminding the effort, several experts agreed that under New York's notoriously technical election laws, Mr. Tsongas had a bare minimum of signatures and might well be unable to withstand the legal challenges to them. If he is knocked from the ballot, he would be cut off from the 268 Democratic delegates at stake in the primary, the largest block of any state except California. Arcane Letter of the Law

The dogfight over who gets to be on the ballot precedes the dogfight over who wins the primary, and in this first battle Gov. Mario M. Cuomo, an announced noncandidate, has already been drawn into the fray. His handpicked state Democratic chairman, John A. Marino, sought last week to get all the major Democratic candidates to agree to a "nonaggression pact" in which they would not challenge any of the other's petitions for the primary.

But such an accord was largely rendered meaningless today by the New Alliance Party's challenge, since under New York law anybody can challenge the validity of petition signatures. If the signatures do not conform precisely to the arcane letter of the law, the State Board of Elections is required to invalidate them, even if they came from duly registered voters.

The Tsongas campaign filed by far the fewest number of signatures of any of the five major Democratic candidates, just 3,952 more than the 10,000 required to be on the ballot. Many election lawyers say that because of all the technicalities in the law, candidates should gather at least twice the minimum number of signatures needed to be assured of surviving challenges.

Mr. Clinton filed 48,281 signatures; Senator Tom Harkin of Iowa had 38,803; Senator Bob Kerrey of Nebraska filed 20,685, and former Gov. Edmund G. Brown Jr. of California had 19,413.

Technically, the notice of "general objections" filed today at the Board of Elections applied to all five candidates. But a spokeswoman for the New Alliance Party, Madelyn Chapman, said in a telephone interview that the specific challenges would be lodged mainly against the Tsongas signatures.

Ms. Chapman said the action amounted to "a bit of hardball" against the major Democratic candidates, who she said had kept the New Alliance Presidential candidate, Lenora Fulani, from appearing at debates and other public functions. But she rejected as "an absolute lie" a charge from Mr. Tsongas's New York State chairman, Jim Armenakis, that the group's action had been prompted by the Clinton campaign.

Mr. Armenakis offered no documentation for his charge, saying he was not at liberty to disclose his sources.

The Clinton campaign chief in New York, Harold Ickes Jr., also dismissed the Tsongas campaign's charge as "absolutely untrue," but stopped short of saying he wanted to see Mr. Tsongas on the ballot for the New York primary on April 7. "If somebody knocks him off, somebody knocks him off," Mr. Ickes said in a telephone interview from New Hampshire.

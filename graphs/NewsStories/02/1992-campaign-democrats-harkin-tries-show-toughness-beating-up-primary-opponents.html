THE 1992 CAMPAIGN: Democrats; Harkin Tries to Show Toughness by Beating Up Primary Opponents

By RICHARD L. BERKE
Published: February 15, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

Senator Tom Harkin showed up at a toxic waste dump here today to attack Gov. Bill Clinton of Arkansas for "sweeping environmental problems under the rug."

Thursday he "did a number on" former Senator Paul E. Tsongas of Massachusetts, as one Harkin aide put it, for supporting nuclear power.

The day before that, he beat up Senator Bob Kerrey of Nebraska for proposals that would "decimate essential programs."

There are more unkind words to come. Mr. Harkin put his targets on notice today that he was not about to turn sweet in the closing days before the New Hampshire primary. "If you can't stand the heat," he declared, invoking the words of another scrappy Democrat, Harry Truman, "get out of the kitchen." A Contact Sport

The man who has built his winning campaigns in Iowa on the credo that "politics is a contact sport" is bent on slashing and burning his way to the Democratic Presidential nomination.

For months, Mr. Harkin has assaulted "George Herbert Hoover Bush," casting himself as the only Democrat who has the stomach and the mettle to take on win-at-any-cost Republicans. But in the past few days, the combative liberal has sought to break from near the bottom in the polls here, and had mixed results, by tearing down the other Democrats.

As the self-appointed hatchet man of the 1992 Democratic campaign, Mr. Harkin sounds off as much these days about his opponents' deficiencies as his own proposals. He and his aides are unapologetic about it.

"This is tough," said Donald R. Sweitzer, an adviser to the campaign. "We're not playing tiddlywinks here."

Taking pride in his aggressiveness, Mr. Harkin insisted that he has merely exposed his opponents' vulnerable spots. "There's been a lot of static in the air," he said in an interview, "and I think it's time to talk about records."

While he may be getting heard, Mr. Harkin risks offending voters who are put off by the negative approach and are left wondering how he would approach issues differently from his opponents.

"I think he's grasping at straws," said John Wright, a bartender at the Granite Street Bar and Grille in Manchester, after he served Mr. Harkin soup and bread for lunch on Thursday and then saw him on the television above the bar slashing Mr. Tsongas on nuclear power.

"I'd like to see him overtaking Tsongas with his programs rather than with attacks," said Mr. Wright, 31, who said that he is torn between Mr. Harkin and Mr. Kerrey. "But that's what's going to get him in the news."

For those who miss his swipe-of-the-day on the news, Mr. Harkin unleashed a television commercial assailing his three major opponents for backing "tax giveaways to the rich."

And for good measure, the Harkin campaign today began broadcasting radio advertisements telling viewers that Mr. Tsongas has a "scheme to increase gasoline taxes" and that Mr. Clinton's state "had to be sued because it didn't provide adequate protection to prevent child abuse." Attack and Counterattack

Another danger facing Mr. Harkin is that attacks invite counterattacks. The Kerrey campaign, accusing Mr. Harkin of misrepresenting Mr. Kerrey's record, decided late today to broadcast television commercials that hit back.

"Tom Harkin is attacking Bob Kerrey," the announcer says. "But he's wrong on the facts. Kerrey's not for tax breaks for the rich at all." The advertisement goes on to say that, unlike Mr. Kerrey, Mr. Harkin opposes a middle-class tax cut. "Tom Harkin," the announcer continues, "old-style Democrat, old-style negative campaign."

At the news conference in Nashua today, Mr. Harkin denied that his attacks were prompted by desperation, saying, "As a matter of fact, our tracking polls show us going up every day." He backed down after he was told that his aides said they were not conducting tracking polls, and said he was referring to "the other tracking polls."

Most polls show Mr. Harkin making limited upward movement in New Hampshire but still far behind Mr. Tsongas and Mr. Clinton and competing with Mr. Kerrey for fourth place.

In an effort attract voters, the verbal punches -- Tim Raftis, the Harkin campaign manager, calls them "comparisons" -- are continuing.

"I don't see the comparisons as nasty," Mr. Raftis said. "I see them as the facts."

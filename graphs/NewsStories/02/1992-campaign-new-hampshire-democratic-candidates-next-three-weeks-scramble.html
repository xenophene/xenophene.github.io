THE 1992 CAMPAIGN: New Hampshire; Democratic Candidates' Next Three Weeks: a Scramble Around America

By ROBIN TONER
Published: February 20, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

The race for the Democratic nomination rolled out of New Hampshire today with a newly credible Paul E. Tsongas, a revived Bill Clinton and a far more complicated course ahead, with primaries and caucuses in 25 states over the next three weeks.

Mr. Tsongas, a former Senator from Massachusetts, got the opportunity to build a national campaign on his New Hampshire victory and demonstrate that he can carry his message beyond his region. Declaring that he had "great potential to move," he began a trip to Maryland, New York, South Dakota, Maine and Georgia, a scramble emblematic of the days to come, and the challenge he faces in quickly and substantially expanding his campaign.

New Hampshire gave Governor Clinton of Arkansas a second chance, after two weeks of harrowing publicity over allegations about marital infidelity and his draft record. A variety of Democrats also agreed that Mr. Clinton's performance in the primary freezes talk of new candidates entering the race any time soon. Those signals came from several party members including Speaker of the House Thomas S. Foley, who told reporters, "I personally don't expect to see it." A Long, Tough Struggle Ahead

Beyond that, New Hampshire simply set the stage for a long, tough struggle for the nomination, when the candidates will have to spread out, target their opportunities, appeal to voters from across a much broader spectrum, and rely to a much greater extent on money and news media than they did in the confines of this tiny state.

"We'll know a lot more by mid-March," said Ronald H. Brown, the chairman of the Democratic National Committee. Representative Steny H. Hoyer of Maryland, the chairman of the House Democratic Caucus and a supporter of Mr. Clinton, said, "Clinton did well enough to be in a holding pattern and not be written off, and Tsongas did well enough to make him a real factor."

Most Democrats look to March 3 for the next sorting out, when primaries and caucuses will be held in Colorado, Georgia, Idaho, Maryland, Minnesota, Utah and Washington. An additional 11 states will have contests a week later, on March 10. First, however, come the Maine caucuses on Sunday and South Dakota primary on Tuesday. Mixed Verdicts

The verdicts were less clear for the rest of the field, which finished well behind Mr. Tsongas and Mr. Clinton. The two Midwesterners in the race, Senators Tom Harkin of Iowa and Bob Kerrey of Nebraska, will be hustling for a victory in the South Dakota primary. Mr. Kerrey acknowledged today that he needed to "do very well" in South Dakota to stay in the race, but felt that he was "hitting my stride" as a candidate.

Mr. Harkin said he also hoped to "pick up steam" in South Dakota and indicated he planned to escalate his already aggressive attacks. Referring to Mr. Clinton, the Iowa Democrat asserted, "I'm going to start talking about the Arkansas mirage. If that's the way he runs Arkansas, God help us if he ever gets to the Presidency."

Former Governor Edmund G. Brown Jr. of California did better than most of the polls here had predicted, finishing in the neighborhood of Mr. Kerrey and Mr. Harkin.

The final results in New Hampshire showed Mr. Tsongas with 33 percent, Mr. Clinton with 25 percent, Mr. Kerrey with 11 percent, Mr. Harkin with 10 percent, Mr. Brown with 8 percent, and the write-in effort for Gov. Mario M. Cuomo at 4 percent. Splitting Delegates

Both Mr. Tsongas and Mr. Clinton emerged from New Hampshire with 9 delegates each. None of the other candidates cracked the 15 percent threshold that, under party rules, is required to qualify for delegates. Because of the Democratic rules apportioning delegates based on Congressional districts, both Mr. Tsongas and Mr. Clinton ended up with the same number of delegates even though their overall vote totals differed.

Surveys of voters leaving the polling place showed that Mr. Tsongas did particularly well among the most educated voters, getting 47 percent of those who had more than a college education. He also got four in 10 of those with income over $75,000, while three in 10 from that group went for Mr. Clinton.

The polling data also showed that Mr. Tsongas ran well among those voters who said that a candidate having specific ideas was important to them; 43 percent of that group went for Mr. Tsongas, and 22 percent for Mr. Clinton.

Mr. Tsongas predicted today that he and Mr. Clinton would, at some point, "go head to head on economic issues." Mr. Tsongas cites his economic proposals as basic truth-telling and a bold challenge to Democratic orthodoxy; he rejects a tax cut for the middle class and endorses a cut in the tax on capital gains to encourage investment. How well that will play among Democratic primary voters around the country, some analysts said, is an open question. Chilly Reception Predicted

Stan Greenberg, Mr. Clinton's polltaker, asserted, "This is an ecnomic plan that I don't think many Democrats will warm to."

The candidates are still making decisions on how to direct their resources in the coming weeks, although Mr. Tsongas has clearly targeted Maryland and Mr. Clinton will focus on Georgia. Mr. Greenberg said the Clinton campaign recognizes it needs to do well both inside and outside the South. "This is not going to be a bunker campaign," he said.

Money will be critical in the weeks to come. Rahm Emmanuel, Mr. Clinton's finance director, said the campaign will have raised $7 million by the end of February, adding, "We had always raised money and garnered resources based on a long-term strategy. Our worst day is Paul Tsonga's best day in terms of money."

A spokesman for Mr. Tsongas declined to furnish fundraising totals, but said that in the wake of the New Hampshire victory, the money was beginning to flow in.

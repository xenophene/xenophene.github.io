THE 1992 CAMPAIGN: Political Week; Democrats' Hopes Fade As Front-Runner Slips

By R. W. APPLE Jr
Published: February 11, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

Only a couple of weeks ago, senior people in the Democratic Party were telling each other that this time they would not waste their energies squabbling. This time they would choose a nominee early. Then they could unite, plot the strategy and raise the money needed to beat President Bush.

That soothing vision was fading fast today as the Iowa caucuses took place and the New Hampshire primary campaign entered its final week.

The people of this state are as proud of their predilection for surprising the experts as they are of their low-tax government. They have a habit of making up their minds who to support very late in the game, as they did with George S. McGovern in 1972 and then with George Bush four years ago.

This time, they seem determined to wait until the last minute once again. Of 58 Democratic voters questioned in this city in the southwestern part of the state over the last few days, only 18 said they had decided who to back, and of those 5 said they might still switch before voting on Feb. 18.

In that kind of situation, today's conventional wisdom can be tomorrow's misapprehension. But the professionals, at least, see Gov. Bill Clinton of Arkansas slipping, day by day. They see former Senator Paul E. Tsongas of Massachusetts gaining. They see the rest of the field still scrambling for a foothold. And it adds up, in their view, to trouble.

Their view is a pragmatic one, which is why the word "electability" buzzes through Democratic circles these days the way "momentum" used to. Many party leaders and many voters in New Hampshire have backed Mr. Clinton in large part because they thought that he, as a telegenic Southern moderate capable of articulating economic and social policies in sharp contrast to Mr. Bush's, would have the best chance of winning in November.

But in recent days that argument seems to have been working against Mr. Clinton. In the wake of charges of marital infidelity and manipulation of his draft status, both of which the Governor has denied, some voters here and many influential people in Washington seem to be edging away from him not so much because of his alleged transgressions but because they fear that the charges would make him a sitting duck for the Republicans later in the year if he can still win the nomination.

The New Hampshire electorate has it in its power either to confirm or overturn that view. A solid victory would quiet the talk; a narrow one might turn the volume down. But a loss, when a big victory had seemed so likely only two weeks ago, would be terribly damaging, even if Mr. Clinton could recover his balance in the South, his home base where many states hold primaries on March 10.

Only Mr. Tsongas, it is widely believed here, is in a position to defeat Mr. Clinton. If that happened, the party would be left with a wounded former front-runner, a victor who has done no organizing to speak of outside New England, and three also-rans whose reputations have not been enhanced very much by the New Hampshire campaign -- Senators Bob Kerrey of Nebraska and Tom Harkin of Iowa and former Gov. Edmund G. Brown Jr. of California.

Not a happy prospect for the Democrats, especially with Mr. Bush apparently headed for a relatively comfortable win over Patrick J. Buchanan.

Mr. Clinton is expected to open a new television campaign in the next few days, intended to make voters think twice about how to beat Mr. Bush and improve their economic lot -- in other words, to persuade them once again, as he did earlier this year, that he is the only electable Democrat. His aides are talking about the primary as if it were Armageddon, considering attacks on Mr. Tsongas and preparing to deal more directly with the attacks on Mr. Clinton's own character if need be.

Mr. Tsongas has drawn the support of liberals like Dudley Dudley, a power in New Hampshire politics for decades, as well as those with fresh doubts about Mr. Clinton, though the former Senator from Massachusetts is no knee-jerk liberal. For example, he backs nuclear power, opposes tax cuts and favors only limited gun control.

Confident that the tide is running with their man, his strategists foresee a final week devoted to countering the notion that he cannot win by emphasizing how far he has come since his forlorn beginnings here.

In 1976, when the previously unknown Jimmy Carter won in both Iowa and New Hampshire, money and support in states where he had done little organizing rapidly flowed to him. But he had shown strength in two parts of the country not his own, the Midwest and Northeast, and the next stops on the political calendar were in the South.

Even if he wins next Tuesday, Mr. Tsongas will have won only at home.

Few pros believe that both Mr. Harkin and Mr. Kerrey will do well enough here to raise the money to compete in other states, although both speak of fighting on to the convention. Both made biting comments about their rivals last week, and either might decide this week to turn to negative commercials in a last-ditch bid for survival.

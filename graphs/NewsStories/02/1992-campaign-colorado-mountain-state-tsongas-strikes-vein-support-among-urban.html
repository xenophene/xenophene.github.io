THE 1992 CAMPAIGN: Colorado; In Mountain State, Tsongas Strikes Vein of Support Among Urban Voters

By DIRK JOHNSON
Published: February 28, 1992

Correction Appended

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

Its icy mountain peaks and vast, lonely plains have always given Colorado a sort of flannel-shirt image, a place where rosy-cheeked workers breathe air freshened by ponderosa pine.

But far more typically, a Colorado voter lives in a city or suburb, holds a college degree and wears a business suit to work behind a computer terminal. Young, relatively affluent, liberal on social issues, but fairly conservative on economic issues, these are the voters who represent the heart of the Democratic electorate here.

It is a profile that suits Paul E. Tsongas, the former Massachusetts Senator who is leading in public opinion polls here five days before next Tuesday's primary.

Mr. Tsongas, whose Colorado campaign had been run from a cousin's kitchen until this week, has been gaining in a daily poll being conducted by The Denver Post and now has the support of 30 percent of Democrats, as against Gov. Bill Clinton's 22 percent. The survey of 310 registered Democrats, conducted Monday through Wednesday and published today, had a margin of sampling error of plus or minus six percentage points. A Neighbor Is Lagging

Bob Kerrey, the Senator from next-door Nebraska who had hoped to win some neighborly affection, was the favorite of only 8 percent in the survey. Former Gov. Edmund G. Brown Jr. of California ranked next with 9 percent, followed by Senator Tom Harkin of Iowa with 3 percent.

On the Republican side, President Bush is leading Patrick J. Buchanan by 65 percent to 26 percent, the survey of 308 registered Republicans found. Mr. Buchanan, the conservative columnist who is proving to be an irritating burr under Mr. Bush's saddle, has canceled appearances here to devote more time to Georgia, which also holds its primary on Tuesday.

Colorado's population is concentrated along the Front Range of the Rocky Mountains, a north-south strip that includes Denver, Colorado Springs and Pueblo. The bulk of jobs in the state are white collar, predominantly in information processing, insurance and banking. The largest employer is US West, the telecommunications giant. Improving Economy

Colorado's economy, which was struggling in the late 1980's after oil prices fell, has been improving, thanks to growth in the white-collar sector. It has averted much of the recessionary problems felt in places like New Hampshire, and its unemployment rate is 5.6 percent, compared with about 6 percent nationally.

These white-collar workers, who outnumber unionized factory workers, have been precisely the kind of voter that Mr. Tsongas attracts.

"His message has resonated here in the same way that Gary Hart's did in the 1980s, appealing to the white-collar, highly educated and culturally liberal," said Cal Jillson, chairman of the political science department at the University of Colorado in Boulder.

Mr. Tsongas comes across as the sort of thoughtful, dry-witted corporate executive that many young business people in Boulder or Denver can see themselves joining for a glass of white wine and interesting conversation. He is seen as "the flipside of Tom Harkin," said Professor Jillson, who is more of a shot-and-beer Democrat. Libertarian Streak

The traditional liberalism of the Democratic Party does not fare well here. A libertarian streak runs through Colorado's electorate, perhaps a legacy of the independent Westerner who prizes freedom above all. It was one of the first states to legalize abortion, but it soundly defeated a proposal to provide state money for them.

It is quality of life, rather than availability of jobs, that tends to dominate voter concerns in Colorado. Environmental issues are salient in Colorado, especially in the college town of Boulder. Mr. Brown, in particular, has focused on environmental concerns. In many states, revenue from lotteries go to education. In Colorado, the money goes for parks and recreational features like bike trails.

Today Mr. Tsongas got another boost, winning the endorsements of two popular Denver Representatives, Patricia Schroeder and David E. Skaggs. In an appearance here on Wednesday, Mr. Tsongas was introduced by Lee Hart, the wife of former Senator Gary Hart. Mr. Hart has not endorsed a candidate. Soaring Since New Hampshire

Mr. Hart has not endorsed a candidate. But political analysts here say Mr. Tsongas has thus far appealed to Colorado voters who had supported Mr. Hart's call for "a new Democratic Party," independent of the party's traditional liberalism.

Until he arrived in Denver on Wednesday, Mr. Tsongas had not visited Colorado since September. Before the New Hampshire primary, he was near the bottom in surveys here, but has been soaring since then. His strength in Colorado prompted his campaign to step up its efforts here, opening a headquarters on Wednesday.

As Mr. Tsongas has emerged as the front-runner here, he has drawn the barbs of his opponents, especially from Mr. Clinton. In television ads, the Arkansas governor describes Mr. Tsongas as a "Reagan clone" and believer in trickle-down economics. Democratic Drought

Colorado has not supported a Democratic Presidential candidate since 1964 when it gave President Lyndon B. Johnson 61 percent of the vote. President Ronald Reagan rolled up huge margins of victory in 1980 and 1984, helped largely by the importance of the military to the state's economy. But President Bush did not fare nearly as well in 1988, defeating Michael S. Dukakis here by eight percentage points.

Gov. Roy Romer, a popular Democrat who embarrassed President Bush during a meeting with the nation's governors at the White House earlier this month, has not endorsed a candidate. But former Gov. Dick Lamm is supporting Mr. Clinton.

The state's strongest Democratic base is Pueblo, a city of steel mills and unionized factory jobs 109 miles south of Denver where Hispanic voters represent nearly 30 percent of the electorate. Republicans are strongest on the Western Slope of the Continental Divide, and in Colorado Springs, home of the Air Force Aacademy where voters are strongly pro-military.

As a Senator from nearby Nebraska, Mr. Kerrey had hoped to take advantage of regional loyalties here as he did in South Dakota.

But that has not been the case. Coloradans tends to look to the West, rather than the Midwest. Indeed, said Walter Stone, a political science professor at the University of Colorado, "people in Colorado have been known to tell Nebraska jokes."

Correction: March 4, 1992, Wednesday An article on Friday about the Democratic primary campaign in Colorado referred to an advertisement for Gov. Bill Clinton of Arkansas that was critical of Paul E. Tsongas. The article quoted the ad as calling Mr. Tsongas a "Reagan clone." That was a paraphrase provided by a Tsongas aide; the words do not appear in the ad. The writer should have checked the ad firsthand. It said Mr. Tsongas favored "another capital gains tax cut for the rich, more nuclear power plants and a 5-cent-a-year hike in the gas tax," and added, "but that's what went wrong in the 80's."

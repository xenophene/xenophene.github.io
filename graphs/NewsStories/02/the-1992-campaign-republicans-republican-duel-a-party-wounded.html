THE 1992 CAMPAIGN: Republicans; Republican Duel: A Party Wounded

By ANDREW ROSENTHAL
Published: February 29, 1992

Correction Appended

  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

Patrick J. Buchanan's challenge to President Bush is threatening to turn an orderly, behind-the-scenes struggle over the future of the Republican Party into an open civil war, Administration officials and senior Republican strategists say.

Mr. Buchanan's television commercials linking Mr. Bush to erotic art films involving homosexuals, and today's counterattack from the White House, seemed to guarantee that the bloodletting between the men would last all spring.

And that is leading to a situation in which party workers, elected officials and likely future Presidential candidates are choosing sides and clubbing one another with the weapons they used on Democrats throughout the 1980's -- the wedge issues of race, religion, taxes, pornography, abortion, welfare and patriotism.

At times, Republican strategists said this week, the immediate task of holding the White House in 1992 seems almost secondary to the larger struggle in the party over which wing, conservatives or moderates, will dominate and who will lead the party in the 1996 campaign.

"An awful lot of conservatives have been doing an awful lot of things other than worrying about the election this year," said a senior party strategist.

Bush supporters said their immediate fear was that Mr. Buchanan was successfully painting the President as a counterfeit conservative and political opportunist whose word cannot be trusted. They fear that voters in the general election will remember what Mr. Buchanan is telling them in the primary struggles. Already, there are signs that the battering inflicted on Mr. Bush is denting the enthusiasm of the party's base of volunteers, organizers and donors.

Another effect of Mr. Buchanan's candidacy has been to push Mr. Bush to the right on issues such as abortion and welfare. Throughout his career, Mr. Bush has wavered between conservative stances and moderate positions that have served him well in general elections when Presidential candidates need to command as much of the center as possible.

In the longer term, senior Republican strategists said they worried that the Bush-Buchanan fight could weaken the party as it seeks a leader for 1996 and beyond.

Republicans said Mr. Buchanan's candidacy had opened that inevitable contest sooner than they wanted, and in a way that party leaders are finding difficult to manage. Prominent Republicans with Presidential aspirations are suddenly torn between the imperative of supporting an incumbent and a desire to save their party and promote their own ambitions. With Clenched Teeth

These Republicans, including Vice President Dan Quayle, Secretary of State James A. Baker 3d, Housing Secretary Jack F. Kemp Jr., Senator Phil Gramm of Texas, former Gov. Pete du Pont of Delaware, William Bennett, the former drug policy chief, and Secretary of Defense Dick Cheney, have lined up behind Mr. Bush.

But some are obviously doing so with clenched teeth, and their efforts seemed aimed primarily at preventing Mr. Buchanan from dominating the party, maintaining unity and keeping their options open.

"These people are the future of the conservative movement, but their unhappy dilemma is that they find themselves without a candidate," a Republican strategist said. High Stakes for Quayle

This has put Representative Newt Gingrich of Georgia, who spent three years deriding Mr. Bush as a tax raiser, in the position of defending his tax policies. Mr. Kemp and Mr. Bennett have had to hold back -- and in Mr. Kemp's case retract -- more radical domestic policies than Mr. Bush supports.

The stakes in 1992 are the highest for Mr. Quayle, whose hopes of winning the nomination in 1996 seem to rest on Mr. Bush's re-election. Analysts say Mr. Quayle's standing has been hurt by his need to defend Mr. Bush on policies that have drawn conservatives' ire, including the 1990 budget compromise that broke Mr. Bush's "no new taxes" pledge.

The Vice President has been deputized to appeal to conservatives for party unity, and to counter talk among Washington conservatives that a Democratic victory might be preferable to another four years of Mr. Bush, since it would give them a chance to regroup.

Mr. Quayle is said to have been arguing within the White House for a more positive campaign toward Mr. Buchanan because, as one Republican put it, "He's worried about the effects of such a civil war on the President in November and on the party in the future." Weak on Right Flank

Mr. Buchanan's candidacy strikes at Mr. Bush's weakest point. Republican conservatives have never quite trusted Mr. Bush, and Mr. Buchanan's candidacy has forced the grumbling into the open.

The Administration has responded with gestures intended to show Mr. Bush's conservatism, like getting rid of John E. Frohnmayer, the head of the National Endowment for the Arts, who had been criticized for permitting Federal financing of art that Mr. Bush himself denounced as obscene and blasphemous.

Conservatives derided such moves as an election year conversion.

Last Friday, Samuel K. Skinner, the White House Chief of Staff, was greeted with a blast of anger at a lunch with the trustees of the Heritage Foundation, a conservative research organization whose board includes executives of major corporations that donate heavily to Republican causes. A List of Dislikes

A member of the board, Frederick B. Krieble, the retired chairman of Loctite Corporation, an adhesive maker, said: "We told him we don't like Bush's foreign policy. We're upset that he hung on to Gorbachev as a buddy long after anyone who knew anything about the Soviet people knew that was a losing position."

Another board member, Grover Coors, vice president of Adolph Coors, the beer maker, and grandson of the founder, said: "It's hard not be caustic. In his heart, Bush believes in big government. They're regulating industry to death."

So far, the White House has looked awkward in its response to the rumblings of revolt among wealthy conservatives and ideologists. Pointing out that Mr. Buchanan has failed to unify the right, Mr. Bush and his advisers warned that conservatives have no choice but to vote for the President.

That argument simply inflamed anti-White House sentiment, especially after Mr. Skinner made it this week in an interview with The Washington Times. Over His Head?

Alarmed at the reaction to Mr. Skinner's remark, the White House argued that he was quoted out of context, but Mr. Krieble said the Heritage board got the same message. "They seem to assume that we're hip-pocket voters for Bush," he said.

Among Republican insiders, the flap contributed to the impression of Mr. Skinner as a man out of his depth. He has yet to smoothly mesh the President's White House and campaign staff, and now he has disrupted the President's relationship with the hard-line conservatives.

Party leaders are also worried about the supply of foot soldiers for the fall campaign. They say conservatives have formed the core of the party's organizers.

"Elections are matters of passion, and just as the Democratic Party finds its passion with liberal activists, the Republican Party depends on the passion of conservative activists," said Gary Bauer, President of the Family Research Council, an anti-abortion group.

Party officials insist their fund raising has not been hurt significantly, but the number of contributors to the Republican National Committee is down to 1.1 million from 1.3 million in 1988.

William J. Gasser, president of an Arlington Heights, Ill., company that makes car washes for dealers and rental companies, said: "I sent a lot of money to George Bush in the past, but will I do that this year? No sir. Emphatically no."

Correction: March 5, 1992, Thursday Because of an editing error, an article on Saturday about conservatives' dissatisfaction with President Bush misidentified a Heritage Foundation board member who criticized Mr. Bush's foreign policy. He is Robert H. Krieble, former chairman of the Loctite Corporation.

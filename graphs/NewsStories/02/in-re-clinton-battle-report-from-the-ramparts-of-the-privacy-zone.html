In Re: Clinton; Battle Report From the Ramparts of the Privacy Zone

By HOWELL RAINES
Published: February 02, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

VICTOR GOLD, a veteran of Beltway politics by way of the rough ceremonies of Louisiana and Alabama, thinks he knows when the nation's press and politicians began their current Dance of Confusion. "Stop and think what's happened," says Mr. Gold, a speech writer for Barry Goldwater and George Bush. "The tabloids 10 or 15 years ago never touched politicians. They went after Hollywood. But sooner or later, they were bound to say, 'The politicians are celebrities and they're fair game.' David Brinkley used to say Washington is a town full of people who think they're celebrities. But that's no longer true. Washington is a town of celebrities."

Once politicians moved from being leaders to also being celebrities, Mr. Gold adds, the tabloid press lost the fear that kept President Kennedy's star-chasing out of print. "They used to think, 'That's real power. Don't touch that.' But they'll touch anything now -- a President, a Supreme Court justice, a candidate. As long as you've got a Geraldo and a tabloid press, we're pretty much driven by that."

Mr. Gold's bleak formulation does not fully explain -- or excuse -- the mad muddle that has swept over the Democratic Presidential campaign. But it does invite a deeper consideration of the fearsome symbiosis that binds together both the high-minded and low-minded citizens of the national media village.

Most analysts point to the victory of John F. Kennedy over Richard M. Nixon in 1960 as the point at which television took over politics. But what of the currents that ran like silent floods from that watershed? Everyone is bobbing along these days on huge tidal surges of change that get little attention. One change has been the approach-avoidance addiction of candidates to what Gary Hart, in the palmy spring of 1984, called the "swinging spotlight" of television. Another has been the long death agony of the old Democratic Party and its attempt to craft a 21st-century identity. Still another has been the 20-year-old trend, spawned in the universities and adopted by the mainstream press, to introduce character and personality as central concerns in the selection of national leaders. Finally, there has been the development in this country of a layered mass media on the British model -- a "quality press" that sees itself as the custodian of traditional political values and a trash press that knows no ethic except to pander without mercy.

Undergirding all of this is a fundamental shift in political demographics -- the arrival of women as a shaping force in a partisan game for which the unofficial scorekeepers are still the middle-aged men on the weekend news shows. The skewing of commentary that results from this male domination surfaced in the confrontation between Clarence Thomas and Anita F. Hill. The men on "The McLaughlin Group" complained that "feminist propaganda" in the news reports was unfair to Justice Thomas. But there was no male outrage over a serious question of governance -- the Republicans' use of Justice Department attorneys and a forensic psychiatrist to try to crush Professor Hill's credibility.

Now a rush is on among male commentators to announce that "the voters do not care" about whether Governor Bill Clinton of Arkansas was faithful to his wife. Already this has produced blasts of cheerleading intended to help Mr. Clinton win his battle against what The Boston Globe called "the scandal machine." "That is why I am rooting like a fanatic for Bill and Hillary Clinton to prevail over a naked attempt by pornographers, learned thumb suckers and go-along hacks to hijack democracy," wrote Tom Oliphant, a Globe columnist.

Female columnists have been slower to announce what the voters care about, and with reason: A New York Times/CBS News Poll among likely Democratic voters showed that 39 percent of women and 31 percent of men said they were likely to consider marital fidelity as one factor among many in casting a vote. There does seem to be consensus on one point. The barking on the panel shows and the rise of print and television tabloids have undermined the credibility and muddied the ethics of the "quality" press. "It is perceived as arrogant and manipulative," Katherine Fanning, former editor of the Christian Science Monitor, said recently at a Harvard seminar.

Bill Kovach, director of the Nieman program for journalists at Harvard, wrote last week that any unfairness to Mr. Clinton is lodged amid a larger unfairness to the other Democratic candidates in declaring Mr. Clinton -- or anyone -- a pre-emptive front-runner before a single vote is cast.

Indeed, Mr. Hart's "swinging spotlight" blessed Mr. Clinton with an extraordinary burst of journalistic fawning. New York magazine detected in his routine speeches "a master politician strutting his stuff." The New Republic, aware of festering rumors about his personal life, tried to inoculate him against disclosures with an editorial arguing that personal questions from back home were out of bounds. This was journalistic midwifery -- unconscious perhaps, but real -- designed to give the Democratic Party an early favorite who could cast off Roosevelt's old suit and refit himself with the conservative cut needed to take on the Republicans.

Whenever politicians start philosophizing about journalism, it is a good idea to check on what is really happening out on the trail. Bill Clinton is a very good Southern politician, and at this decisive moment in his career he is reading from the oldest page in the region's populist play book. He is running against the newspapers, and much of this process is invisible to the public. His Dixie spinners, Frank Greer of Tuscaloosa, Ala., and James Carville of Carville, La., are working the telephones and the campaign plane, exhorting reporters toward self-flagellation and re-education. After Hillary Clinton declared the need for a "zone of privacy" around candidates, the Clinton team tried to put the sights and sounds of the campaign plane itself "off the record."

Ms. Clinton, a lawyer, has established herself as a canny presence who can summon a Nancy Reagan gaze and a gambler's nerve. And of all the commentators on her husband's situation, she may have provided the best map to the road ahead. The question of her husband's sex life, she said on ABC News's "Prime Time Live," is the "daughter of Willie Horton," a diversion that "keeps real issues out."

The reference invites a glance toward the fall and serves as a reminder that the Democrats are now mired in the intramural psychodrama of their own nominating process and may find a resolution that looks better before Labor Day than after.

Republican strategists are patient. They are watching to see if Mr. Clinton can generate enough backlash to bury this issue. But they are also quietly contemplating a general election campaign that features George and Barbara and Marilyn and Dan and a lot of joint appearances built on "family values." It does not take a very clear crystal ball to fill in the scenario. At some point, perhaps, a Republican wag might suffer a slip of the tongue about Mrs. Bush never having to worry about where to find her husband. The remark, the First Lady's grandmotherly disclaimer and a new debate on the "privacy zone" would get big play.

Victor Gold may be right that we are caught in a system run amok. But the academic dissections of feeding frenzies miss an important point. Curiosity is humankind's most distinguishing characteristic. In politics, unresolved questions create a vacuum, and the winds of curiosity keep pulling at celebrities as long as that vacuum endures. Who killed J.F.K.? What did Clarence Thomas say? Who, as New York magazine asked in worshipful tones last month, is this guy Bill Clinton? It is an understandable impulse of political self-preservation to try to put the campaign plane off the record. But that is not likely to fill the vacuum or still the winds. Especially if the Republicans crank up their own wind machine in the fall.

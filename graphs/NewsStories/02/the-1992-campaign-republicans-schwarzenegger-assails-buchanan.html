The 1992 Campaign: Republicans; SCHWARZENEGGER ASSAILS BUCHANAN

By ANDREW ROSENTHAL
Published: February 16, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

The Bush campaign finally got out the "B" word today, but it took a bigger man than the President to do it.

Appearing at President Bush's side in this suburb of Manchester, Arnold Schwarzenegger, Mr. Bush's loyal heavy lifter, did what Mr. Bush has been avoiding for weeks: He mentioned Patrick J. Buchanan, the conservative commentator who is challenging the President in the primary on Tuesday, by name.

Mr. Buchanan's attacks have clearly stung Mr. Bush, and the President today indirectly accused him of spreading "flat, outright lies." But Mr. Bush, who ran a heavily negative campaign here and across the country in 1988, has made a tactical decision not to do that this year in New Hampshire, and Mr. Buchanan's name can never be read on the President's lips.

"We want to make sure that when it comes Tuesday, February 18th, that you all go out there and help the President and pump up his vote," Mr. Schwarzenegger told a crowd in a school gymnasium that got to its feet, cheered, whistled, squealed and generally gave the movie star a much bigger ovation than it granted the President. 'Send a Message to Pat'

"I want you to vote, and at the same time send a message to Congress," said Mr. Schwarzenegger, who is chairman of the President's Council on Physical Fitness and Sports. He grinned as he borrowed a line from "Terminator 2," his latest hit. "At the same time, send a message to Pat Buchanan: 'Hasta la vista, baby.' "

After the event Bush aides simply smiled with satisfaction when asked if the campaign had put Mr. Schwarzenegger up to it. But the Bush campaign was counting on Mr. Schwarzenegger's appearances this weekend to build up an enthusiasm that has been notably lacking from the President's campaign events here.

Mr. Schwarzenegger accompanied Mr. Bush to New Hampshire for a final weekend of campaigning in which he scrambled today to recover from accusations that he had abandoned a popular proposal to increase the tax deduction for children.

Mr. Buchanan pursued the attack today, running a new television advertisement that accused the President of not taking the extra $500 deduction seriously because he dropped it from a seven-point list of urgent economic measures that he gave Congress this week. "Don't be fooled again," the ad says. "It's George Bush that's taxing and spending your future away." Some Indirect Criticism

But having made the purely tactical decision not to use a negative commercial that had been prepared on Mr. Buchanan, Mr. Bush confined himself today to indirectly criticizing Mr. Buchanan for criticizing him. Although Mr. Bush lumped his Republican challenger with the Democrats, it was clear that the President's comments were aimed chiefly at the man who had spoiled his hopes of coasting through the primary season unscathed.

"I really honestly believe that the people of New Hampshire are a little bit tired of all these negative advertisements and all the attack-dog tactics coming from the left and coming from the right," said Mr. Bush, who made a last-minute rally here four years ago with commercials that attacked Senator Bob Dole and then ran a heavily negative general election campaign against Michael S. Dukakis, the Democratic nominee.

Just as Mr. Dole complained that Mr. Bush's ads in 1988 distorted his record, Mr. Bush complained today that Mr. Buchanan's ads were misleading.

The President did not miss a chance to take an indirect slap at Mr. Buchanan. At the Goffstown school, he criticized "some of my opponents" who are calling for a freeze on Federal spending. That, he said, would take money from the Social Security checks of the elderly. The only candidate calling for such a freeze is Mr. Buchanan. Bush on the Defensive

The charge that Mr. Bush was not seriously pushing the extra $500 deduction has become the focus of Mr. Buchanan's last-minute assault on the President, who has been battling accusations that he is not doing enough to help ordinary Americans. And much of Mr. Bush's day was devoted to defending himself against the charge instead of taking the offensive on his own proposals, as he had hoped.

At each stop today, Mr. Bush waved above his head a copy of the thick bill that he submitted to Congress. "Yesterday, I'm told New Hampshire voters were subjected to flat, outright lies about this plan," the President said. "This plan includes many things, including deductions for student loans and tax relief for families for children."

"It's in there," he said over and over again. "It's in this bill."

Responding to Mr. Buchanan's accusations, Bush aides acknowledged that the President had said in his State of the Union Message that the $500 deduction was needed "right away." They said he did not include it in his smaller package, which he wants Congress to pass by March 20, because the deductions would not be recorded until next year and therefore do not immediately stimulate the economy.

THE 1992 CAMPAIGN: The Victor; TSONGAS MAKES BID TO WIDEN SUPPORT

By KAREN DE WITT
Published: February 20, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

Paul E. Tsongas, the Democratic winner of the New Hampshire primary, began an intense search yesterday for the money and votes he needs to take his campaign beyond New England.

The former Senator from Massachusetts had a private fund-raising event and rally in Baltimore, then three fund-raising events with the Greek-Americans yesterday evening in Astoria and Brooklyn.

Mr. Tsongas, who for the first time in his 10-month quest for the Democratic Presidential nomination had a campaign plane, told reporters that it was critical for him to do well in Maine, South Dakota and Maryland.

A victory in these states, he said, "can get us to the next tier," because they are small enough to do the kind of one-on-one voter campaigning that has marked his campaign. Winning these states would enhance his fund-raising ability, he said. 'We Need Money'

"We got $50,000 in unsoliticed funds yesterday," said Mr. Tsongas, who likes to brag that his campaign was outspent six to one by that of Gov. Bill Clinton of Arkansas, who came in second in the primary.

"We need the money," to take his economic message to voters with television commercials instead of the 85-page booklet he has been using, Mr. Tsongas told reporters on his plane. With money, he said, "we can reduce the book to 30 seconds."

Maryland will be the first test of Mr. Tsongas's appeal in the South or a border state. He appeared to do well with a crowd of supporters here at a rally in Hopkins Plaza, where he gave his stump speech of economic and generational responsibility.

One supporter gave him an oar.

"I think we've found our campaign symbol," Mr. Tsongas said, lifting the oar in the air. Mr. Tsongas often likens America's present economic situation to that of a crew standing in a boat and bashing one another with oars while German and Japanese rivals row together with precision.

In the evening at the Crystal Place in Astoria, nearly 2,000 people jammed into a ballroom to see him. Nearly 5,000 others stood in the street to meet him. Inside there were more chants of his name and people hoisted banners, one of which said, "Paul Tsongas: They called Clark Kent mild mannered, but we know better."

Michael Chronos, a real estate broker, said he was sure that Mr. Tsongas would be President in November. "He has the guts to go after Bush," he said.

Mr. Tsongas then went to a Greek Orthodox church in Brooklyn where he was met by another crowd and chants of "Tsongas, Tsongas, Tsongas."

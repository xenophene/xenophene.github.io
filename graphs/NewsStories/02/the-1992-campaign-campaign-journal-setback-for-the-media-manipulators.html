THE 1992 CAMPAIGN: Campaign Journal; Setback for the Media Manipulators

By JOHN TIERNEY
Published: February 20, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

The most overused slogan in the Presidential campaign here probably was "take back." Orators differed on what precisely needed to be taken back -- the election, the system, the Government, the country -- but they agreed on what had taken it away: money and media. Politics was being corrupted by slick television images, quick sound bites, sleazy attacks and generally superficial media manipulation.

Well, the votes are in here, and it's not looking good for the media manipulators. The Democratic primary was won by a homely candidate with a small advertising budget who was initially written off by the press and did not run any negative or even very interesting commercials. The candidate with the most dramatic spot advertising -- and the meanest attack advertisements -- finished fourth, just two percentage points ahead of a candidate who barely did any spot advertising at all.

And all the President's media consultants could not match the impact of Patrick J. Buchanan's homespun commercials, which had the graphic look of late-night commercials for carpet-warehouse clearance sales. The candidate himself helped write them, and his sister provided crucial inspiration. The advertisements were certainly negative, but it would be hard to call them sleazy, since they lacked any subtlety and consisted mostly of President Bush speaking.

. . .

The election results may reflect either the voters' discrimination or the manipulators' ineptitude, probably a little of both. New Hampshire voters always get an unusually close look at the candidates, and this year there was special emphasis on the issues. Voters were exposed to four televised debates, a variety of half-hour commercials and stacks of written plans from the candidates.

So they could rely on more than just 30-second spots and 10-second sound bites, few of which seemed memorable anyway. In what must have been some kind of record, the most noteworthy new sound bite was a letter, written in 1969 by Gov. Bill Clinton of Arkansas, that took Ted Koppel seven minutes to read on ABC's "Nightline."

The most noteworthy old sound bite, of course, was Mr. Bush's 1988 pledge: "Read my lips. No new taxes." It was being recited by 4-year-olds across New Hampshire by the end of the campaign. Mr. Buchanan's advertisements showing it kept running long after the public was sick of them, which many media consultants would consider a mistake.

But the Buchanan campaign reveled in unorthodoxy, and soon expanded the advertisement to include people reciting, "Read our lips." Then the advertisement's graphic style -- mismatched red and white capital letters -- was continued in a second advertisement inspired by a newspaper article spotted by Angela (Bay) Buchanan, the candidate's sister. She, Mr. Buchanan and other campaign staff members quickly turned the article into a commercial that ran the last three days of the campaign and may have been the crowning blow.

The commercial showed Mr. Bush in the State of the Union Message promising a $500 increase in the personal tax exemption for a child, followed by a subsequent headline, "President drops middle-class tax cut." After the announcer castigated Mr. Bush for yet another deception, the commercial ended with a photograph of the President's face covered with red-letter words: TAXER and SPENDER.

Mr. Buchanan's last-minute surge in popularity did not surprise Kathleen Hall Jamieson, dean of the Annenberg School for Communication at the University of Pennsylvania. She interviewed a focus group of 10 New Hampshire voters through the primary, and she found that they recalled Mr. Buchanan's advertising better than anyone else's.

"Buchanan had a very simple message," Dr. Jamieson said, "and it was reinforced in the final days because it ran across the second ad as well. The message was that Bush is a hypocrite, and it was the kind of plain, straightforward ad that resonated this year, because people were seeking substance."

Mr. Bush's advertisements were short on substance and long on symbols like the Oval Office and fighter planes. They mainly showed him speaking in mild-mannered tones and asking for the chance to "lead America back to prosperity" -- an interesting request, since presumably he was the one who had led everyone away from prosperity in the first place.

Dr. Jamieson said the Democrat whose advertising was best recalled by her focus group was Paul E. Tsongas's, even though he ranked fourth in advertising spending and ran relatively unexciting commercials. They showed him swimming, noted that "he's no movie star," and promised that he would make "tough choices." His unslick message helped him win a third of the vote.

. . .

Senator Tom Harkin of Iowa had striking commercials that showed him in an abandoned factory and his deaf brother endorsing him in sign language. He also lambasted his rivals for supporting "tax cuts for the rich," a questionable claim. Although some of his rivals do support cutting the capital gains tax, they also support raising tax rates for the rich and giving the middle class a tax cut.

Mr. Harkin also put on other negative commercials with debatable claims, but neither they nor his positive advertisements seemed to overcome voters' distrust of his basic message. He finished in fourth place. In fifth place with 8 percent of the vote was former Gov. Edmund G. Brown Jr. of California, who spent the least on advertising, relying mainly on talk radio.

Senator Bob Kerrey of Nebraska spent more than any candidate on advertising on WMUR-TV, the state's major station, but he got few votes. He adopted a protectionist message that seemed only to hurt his standing in the polls.

Mr. Clinton, whose campaign was declared in trouble by many experts in Washington because of his negative publicity, managed to reverse his decline in the last few days. During this time he tried several novel media strategies, including answering questions from voters on two live 30-minute television programs bought by his campaign.

The audiences for the shows were not large: Of New Hampshire's 365,000 households, according to Arbritron ratings, only 19,000 watched his program on Thursday, and only 2,000 on Friday. But then, it took only 41,522 votes to put Mr. Clinton in second place, with 25 percent of the vote.

Talk-Show Host as Mr. Nice Guy

By WALTER GOODMAN
Published: February 12, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

Charlie Rose, who became Channel 13's late-night man this season, is in the early-morning mode. His hourlong program, at 11 on week nights (the show is rebroadcast nationally the next night at 10 on the Learning Channel), may start with a discussion of an issue of moment and then ease into interviews with entertainers, writers, sports figures and other more and less prominent people, who are generally treated like adults.

Among the most prominent so far: Gov. Mario M. Cuomo, Lieut. Col. Oliver L. North, Paul McCartney and Penn and Teller. Tonight you can see a few of the less prominent: Ed McCabe, an advertising man; Charles Woods, identified as a candidate for the Democratic Presidential nomination, and Diane Ackerman, a writer and naturalist.

Mr. Rose, an experienced talk-show manager, has an amiable presence. Almost movie-actor handsome at some angles, with an accent that may remind you of a less intense Bill Moyers, for whom he once worked, he never presses a guest beyond the limits of hospitality and has a generous word for anybody willing to talk to him.

One of his stronger programs last week started with a four-man discussion of health care, pegged to the announcement of President Bush's plan. It was in the spirit of the "MacNeil/Lehrer Newshour," though less tightly directed, and viewers should at least have come away with the outlines of the arguments over the main election-year proposals.

Then came interviews with the jazz trumpeter Wynton Marsalis (who would be featured on a PBS special the next night) and the actor Paul Sorvino. The programming calculation may be that fans of the "MacNeil/Lehrer Newshour" will not mind more at 11 P.M. of the sort of thing they are given at 7 P.M. (the other night they could see Jim Lehrer himself being interviewed by Mr. Rose) and will sit still for actors and musicians, too. Or maybe this is Channel 13's effort to turn on viewers who haven't been turning on "MacNeil/ Lehrer."

It is a well produced hour that can move fast onto a late-breaking subject. On Monday, three New York reporters were brought in to talk about that night's rape conviction of Mike Tyson. Among the other subjects addressed in recent weeks have been Japanese competition, the President's State of the Union speech and Roe v. Wade.

Mr. Rose ranges beyond the political mainstream. On Friday he came up with two other little publicized aspirants for the Presidency, Lenora Fulani of the New Alliance Party, and Larry Agran, a former Mayor of Irvine, Calif. Mr. Rose asked Ms. Fulani about charges that her party is some sort of cult and asked Mr. Agran what makes him think that six years as a small-city mayor constitute credentials for the Presidency, but did not demand real answers. He is not out to score points.

In the show-biz segments, Mr. Rose can be a first-name dropper. Talking to Jeff Zucker, the producer of "Today," he referred to Bryant and Katie and Willard and even Oliver (Stone). (NBC received more than its quota of plugs last week: for "L.A. Law," David Letterman and the sportscaster Bob Costas, as well as for "Today.")

Mr. Rose spreads compliments as fertilizer for conversation. He and Mr. Zucker seemed to be competing for which could express greater enthusiasm for the renovated "Today." Mr. Rose did evince concern over a list, run on a recent program, of 37 people who had been "somehow related" to the Kennedy assassination and soon after died under "mysterious circumstances." No further information was provided by "Today," and Mr. Rose seemed to be implying in his mild way that this may have been a dip into what he called the tabloidization of television. But he did not get nasty about it, and ended the interview with a typical morning-show warble: "What a great time to be involved in the news business!"

The free-flowing flattery (Mr. Zucker was splashed with "meteoric rise," "hard work and creativity" and more) may be excused by the generally upscale choice of guests, most of whom have earned a few compliments. But Mr. Rose can also succumb to the mere pitch, as in an interview with Charles J. Givens, the author of "More Wealth Without Risk," a how-to-get-rich best seller. Mr. Rose had already interviewed Mr. Givens on the CBS show "Nightwatch" and apparently couldn't get enough. This time round he asked, in his professionally earnest way, "What does one learn from the life that Givens has lived?"

So there you have it. If Ted Koppel seems overly stimulating before bedtime and Arsenio Hall seems frivolous, Charlie Rose should keep you awake for an hour without spoiling a comfortable night's sleep. The station-plug breaks are relatively brief. 'Edge' Channels 13 and 49 9 and 11 tonight

Tonight's edition of "Edge," at 9 on Channel 13 and at 11 on Channel 49, offers a particularly lively potpourri. You can watch the lawyer Alan M. Dershowitz and the writer James B. Stewart and others disagreeing about the character and punishment of the junk-bond impresario Michael Milken; the writer Dennis Potter ("Pennies From Heaven" and "The Singing Detective") and the television producer Steven Bochco ("L.A. Law" and "Hill Street Blues") talking about television; Art Spiegelman ("Maus II") elaborating on his cartoons; well-known women speculating on the effects of the Clarence Thomas and Bill Clinton stories, and some good jazz.

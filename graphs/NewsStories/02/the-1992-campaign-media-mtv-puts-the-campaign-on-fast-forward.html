THE 1992 CAMPAIGN: Media; MTV Puts the Campaign on Fast Forward

By KAREN De WITT
Published: February 08, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

The latest forum for the Presidential candidates includes rock, rap, reggae, fast forwards, slow motions and electronic special effects.

MTV has hit the campaign trail, mixing interviews and reports on the candidates into its 24 hours of music programming tailored to an audience of 25 million, mostly 18 to 24 years old.

To insure that the viewers do not zap the political season right off the air by remote control, MTV's interviews are packaged with the glitz, sound and speed of a rock video.

"We know the latest, hippest music to put something with," said Dave Sirulnick, the 27-year-old director of the MTV news operation. "That's our strength."

So the rap beat of Digital Underground, the techno-pop flash of Simple Minds, or the bouncy dance rhythms of Red Hot Chili Peppers can be heard in the background as the candidates talk. News From Youth Angle

Though more accustomed to news that chronicles the latest on Guns 'n' Roses or Tom Petty and the Heartbreakers, the MTV audiencehas been prepared for the political reports, the news teams says.

"We've eased into enough hard news that when we all of a sudden start doing the election, our audience isn't going to flip out," Mr. Sirulnick said. "We covered the Gulf war, did demonstrations and stuff. They're getting news, but it's not going to be CNN. We're going to give them MTV, which isn't the mainstream way of looking at things."

The reports are about three or four minutes long and begin with snippets of interviews with a few young people. The cuts are fast. A couple of words. Then a change: A reporter. A candidate. A question. Three seconds each. Then back to the young people's impressions. Clinton: "Big hair." Harkin: "F.D.R. wannabe." Tsongas: "Uh, uh, old." Behind the videotape is the music. In front of the videotape are words, superimposed, giving more information on who, what and why.

In 1984, Lee Atwater, the Reagan-Bush campaign strategist who died last year, said that music videos had established a new youth culture of untapped voters. In the 1984 campaign, Mr. Atwater sent a messenger to MTV to bring back a copy of a John Cougar Mellencamp video to show Mr. Reagan. He wanted to familiarize the President with the phenomenon.

It is not known whether Mr. Reagan ever actually watched the video, but it has surely not been lost on this year's candidates that for a week in 1984, interspersed with MTV's music programming, announcers reported Mr. Atwater's request and flashed a picture of Mr. Reagan. The picture asked, "Does this man like MTV?"

Market research suggests that fewer than 10 million of the 25 million MTV viewers are registered to vote, the channel says, and so it has begun working with a youth coalition called "Rock the Vote" to encourage registration.

In the reporting from the campaign here, the music channel has concentrated on the concerns of young people in New Hamphsire, asking the candidates questions about what it calls the three E's: the economy, the environment and education.

When Mr. Tsongas left a voter's home in Salem recently, he was approached by Tabitha Soren, MTVs's 24-year-old campaign reporter.

The former Massachusetts senator explained how his schedule for paying back college loans would take smaller payments from someone who had a public service job. "But if you're a rock star, we're going to make a fortune," Mr. Tsongas added.

Other candidates, too, tried to play to MTV's audience.

Edmund G. Brown Jr., the former Governor of California, told MTV viewers that if every one watching voted, they could "turn the country around."

Bob Kerrey, the Senator from Nebraska, greeted the MTV crew with the channel's slogan, "I want my MTV." During his interview, he advised the audience to watch an hour of television, "but then hit the books."

Mr. Sirulnick said MTV was avoiding identifying candidates with particular pieces of music. Rejection by Performers

Mr. Reagan tried to associate himself with Bruce Springsteen's popular "Born in the U.S.A.," but Mr. Springsteen issued a statement disassociating himself from the President. More recently, Christine McVie of the group Fleetwood Mac, requested that Gov. Bill Clinton of Arkansas not use her song "Don't Stop." And Mr. Mellencamp also asked that Mr. Kerrey not use his song "Small Town."

The New Hampshire reports are just the beginning of MTV's election coverage; it will continue through the election. The channel says it will also try to educate viewers about the process as well as the issues. Reports are being prepared to answer such questions as, What is a "spin doctor?" What does a campaign manager really do? And who are all those people surrounding a candidate?

Mr. Sirulnick said that all the candidates "had to put on a different thinking cap" for the MTV audience.

"They're talking to millions of young voters," he said, "and they have to figure out what to say to keep them from hitting the remote."

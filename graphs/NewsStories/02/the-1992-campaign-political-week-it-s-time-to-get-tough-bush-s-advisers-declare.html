THE 1992 CAMPAIGN: Political Week; It's Time to Get Tough, Bush's Advisers Declare

By ANDREW ROSENTHAL
Published: February 25, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

For weeks, White House strategists put out bullying talk about "going negative" on Patrick J. Buchanan. After New Hampshire, the President's strategists concluded they had talked too hard and hit too softly. This week, they say, they really mean it.

They are ready to spend whatever they have to on commercials, including attack ads, and to accuse Mr. Buchanan of "cheating" and question his patriotism to show how tough they can be.

The first blast against the conservative commentator came from a fellow fire-eater, Representative Newt Gingrich of Georgia, the state singled out by the Bush team as their killing ground. Working on President Bush's behalf, Mr. Gingrich denounced Mr. Buchanan for running a campaign commercial accusing Mr. Bush of abandoning a promised tax break.

The advertisement was "dishonest" and "hit below the belt," said Mr. Gingrich, whose sudden affection for a President he has derided for three years as a sell-out on taxes is one sign of how topsy-turvy the Republican campaign has become.

Senator Phil Gramm of Texas has undertaken the task of portraying the tough-talking Mr. Buchanan as a wimp by reminding Southerners that the commentator opposed the war against Iraq. "Let him drop by Fort Benning and explain to the people there why when they were in the desert, he was opposing the President," Mr. Gramm said today.

And Charles Black, senior adviser to the Bush campaign, said that after holding its fire in New Hampshire, the campaign was almost certain to run commercials criticizing Mr. Buchanan in the South.

The motivation for this sudden toughness is simple: The stakes are high in the South. By March 10, eight Southern or border states will have held nominating primaries or caucuses, beginning with Georgia and Maryland next Tuesday, and the Bush campaign hopes to accomplish no less than eliminating Mr. Buchanan as a candidate.

Southern poll takers, political scientists and politicians say the ingredients are there for Mr. Bush to do that. The region has well-heeled and well-organized Republicans who have been a minority so long that they cling loyally to a Republican White House, and it has a better economy than the Northeast. And Mr. Bush has millions to spend on advertising.

"If the President does not do substantially better in the South than in New Hampshire, he better buy him a real house in Texas, because he's going to need it," said Claiborne Darden, a poll taker in Atlanta.

Mr. Bush's supporters, who frantically tried to play down the importance of New Hampshire, acknowledge that the next two weeks are a clear test of the President's ability to drag his campaign out of its current state of confusion, reassert control over his own party and prepare for the fall campaign against the Democratic nominee.

So, the Bush camp is angry. Are the other guys scared?

Even though his decision to pull his punches in New Hampshire clearly cost Mr. Bush votes, no one doubts his ability to get nasty. He did that in 1988 against Michael S. Dukakis.

But Mr. Dukakis did not fight back. This time, Mr. Bush is calling a proven street fighter outside.

Mr. Buchanan has shown no hesitation to attack Mr. Bush, and in the fall, Mr. Bush could be running against someone like Gov. Bill Clinton of Arkansas, who likes to remind everyone that he has already shown he can take a punch.

Mr. Buchanan's strategy in the South is clear. He has nowhere near as much money as Mr. Bush and has been concentrating on Georgia. Mr. Bush seems secure in South Carolina, thanks to the efforts of Gov. Carroll A. Campbell Jr., who delivered the state to him in 1988 and to Ronald Reagan in 1984, and Mr. Buchanan will have a difficult time getting enough money to compete in large states like Florida and Texas.

In his Southern campaigning, Mr. Buchanan will go on hitting hard on racial tensions, which he has been doing by accusing Mr. Bush of signing a bill that would create quotas in hiring. He will also remind Southern voters that Mr. Bush broke his 1988 "no new taxes pledge."

In Georgia, Mr. Buchanan is running a commercial that proved effective in New Hampshire. It accuses Mr. Bush of reneging on a promise to push for a $500 increase in the tax exemption for children. The proposal was included in Mr. Bush's economic plan, but not in a list of the seven most urgent measures that the President wants passed by March 20. Today, Mr. Bush wrote to Representative Robert H. Michel, the House minority leader, saying that the proposal was a "major part" of his policy that he wants passed this year.

But Merle Black, professor of political science at Emory University in Atlanta, criticized Mr. Bush, saying "The business with the $500 is classic stupid politics and stupid politics doesn't play well anywhere."

Mr. Black and others said Mr. Buchanan's task in the days ahead is to focus on the 40 percent of Georgia Republicans who, according to state polls, are not firmly committed to Mr. Bush. Also, he must pull in some conservative Democrats, a possibility since Georgia voters do not have to decide which primary they will vote in until they enter the polling place.

"These are the white swing voters, who could have voted for Reagan, could have voted for George Wallace, might even have voted for David Duke, but Buchanan's blown Duke out of the water by talking about quotas," Mr. Black said.

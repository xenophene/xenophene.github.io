THE MEDIA BUSINESS; Sponsors to Review a Project on Fairness in Political Ads

By STUART ELLIOTT
Published: February 20, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

AN innovative program to police the content of political advertising is being reviewed after serious faults became evident in a test during the New Hampshire primary.

Sponsors of the program, called Project Run Fair, are the League of Women Voters, the American Association of Advertising Agencies and the Markle Foundation. They hope to revise the program, which ran from Feb. 5 through Tuesday, Primary Day, and to continue it through the Presidential campaign.

The program's goal was to try to improve the tenor and tone of the estimated $3.5 million worth of ads that inundated New Hampshire by offering voters an opportunity to register complaints about unfair and misleading messages.

Despite the shortcomings, the test "was very useful in giving voters a feeling they have a way to influence the standards of how campaigns should be conducted," Lloyd N. Morrisett, president of the foundation in New York, said yesterday.

The program comes amid continuing concerns about negative advertising and its impact on American politics, particularly since a series of ads attacking Michael S. Dukakis were run on behalf of George Bush in 1988. Some political scientists fear that such ads can affect public opinion and even reduce voter turnout.

Project Run Fair sought to monitor the advertising for the Democratic and Republican Presidential candidates by giving New Hampshire voters a chance to call a telephone hotline to identify those broadcast commercials and print advertisements they liked or disliked.

One of the problems discovered in the test, said Ginger Culpepper, president of the New Hampshire chapter of the League of Women Voters, in Hanover, N.H., was that people were unhappy that the program's number -- 900-RUN-FAIR -- cost $1.15 a minute to call. The funds raised from the calls were used to defray the cost of the test, Ms. Culpepper said, adding that charging for calls seemed to eliminate candidates' organizing faked complaints about their opponents' ads.

Another problem with the test, she continued, was the difficulty in obtaining exposure for public-service radio spots and print ads that the sponsors had asked media to run, free of charge, to publicize the test.

The program was publicized in advertising, produced by Wunderman Worldwide in New York, that featured patriotic symbols, like the Statue of Liberty clutching a telephone receiver.

To compensate for the dearth of free advertising, Ms. Culpepper said, the sponsors found themselves scrambling to generate media coverage for the program. While they were pleased by the amount of that coverage, she added, the absence of an intensive level of free ads meant that "not enough people heard the word."

Even so, said Beverly McFarland, a spokeswoman for the League of Women Voters in Washington, the sponsors believed there was widespread awareness of the test within the political community.

That, she added, might have acted as a deterrent against flagrant inaccuracies and contributed to the "fairly benign" overall content of the campaign's ads.

The most complaints during the test, Ms. Culpepper said, were touched off by ads run for Patrick Buchanan, the conservative Republican challenger to President Bush, and for Senator Tom Harkin, Democrat of Iowa. Those candidates were also identified by the political journalists covering the New Hampshire Presidential primary as engaging in attack advertising more than their opponents.

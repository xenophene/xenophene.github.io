THE 1992 CAMPAIGN: South Dakota; Democrats, in Midwestern Debate, Aggressively Court the Farm Vote

By RICHARD L. BERKE
Published: February 24, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

In a free-wheeling, often chaotic debate that was marked by personal barbs, six Democratic candidates for President virtually tripped over one another tonight trying to assure South Dakotans that they were most sympathetic to the needs of rural America.

While the primary election on Tuesday is viewed as a showdown between two farm state Senators, Tom Harkin of Iowa and Bob Kerrey of Nebraska, neither wasted any time in attacking his rivals as insensitive to family farmers. The other Democrats insisted that they understood the importance of agriculture to the nation's economy.

At the outset of the televised debate, Mr. Kerrey assailed Gov. Bill Clinton of Arkansas for having an adviser, Robert Shapiro of the Progressive Policy Institute, who has "made some provocative and negative statements about agriculture."

Mr. Clinton denied that Mr. Shapiro had advised him on such matters, and said he was surprised that Mr. Kerrey would make it an issue in radio advertising here. "It's amazing to me that Senator Kerrey would attack me on the ads," Mr. Clinton said, "since we served on the Agriculture Committee of the National Governors Association together."

Not willing to concede the farm vote to his rivals from the heartland, Mr. Clinton declared: "Senators Harkin and Kerrey know that I've been a farm state Governor for 11 years."

Sponsored by the South Dakota Democratic Party, the 90-minute event was billed as a forum that would offer the candidates no chance for rebuttal. But the audience at the Howard Johnson Hotel here, and the viewers on South Dakota's public television stations and the national C-Span cable television network, were treated to the liveliest of the five national debates held so far.

Mr. Harkin, for one, called on former Senator Paul E. Tsongas of Massachusetts, who was the top vote-getter in the New Hampshire primary and was a leader in today's caucuses in Maine, to renounce his proposal for a 5-cent-a-gallon gasoline tax.

But Mr. Tsongas challenged Mr. Harkin for interrupting him, saying, "We talk about personal decency; let's practice it." He also said he did not know much about agriculture, but said that did not mean he did not understand rural issues.

"I acknowledge that Bob Kerrey and Tom Harkin know a lot more about agriculture than I do," Mr. Tsongas said. "But I know how to listen, how to learn. I don't compete with you, Bob."

Mr. Kerrey shot back, accusing Mr. Tsongas of knowing more about agriculture than he lets on, and of not being a friend of the heartland. "It's not like you're coming into the state and hearing it all right now," Mr. Kerrey said. Saying, "I'm not trying to be personal," Mr. Kerrey went on to accuse Mr. Tsongas of having a poor record on farm issues in the Senate.

The attacks were punctuated by laughter. At one point, the moderator, Ken Bode, an analyst for Cable News Network and graduate of the University of South Dakota, repeated what he said was a line from Mr. Kerrey that Mr. Tsongas thought "a combine is used to mix a martini."

"Great line, but I did not say that," Mr. Kerrey said.

With comic timing, Mr. Tsongas asked later, "Answer me this: What is a martini?"

Coming off a strong showing today in Maine, former Gov. Edmund G. Brown Jr. of California said the United States should not spend any more money on foreign aid until farmers and small businesses were better taken care of. He devoted most of his time to repeating his themes of overhauling a corrupt political system.

But he did not escape the attacks of Larry Agran, the former Mayor of Irvine, Calif., who is usually excluded from debates of the major candidates but was invited the state party to this event. He accused his fellow Californian of hypocrisy in deriding special-interest donations.

"When I hear Jerry Brown, the grand champion of raising special-interest money in the 70's and 80's," Mr. Agran said angrily, "I'm up to here with it." Turning to Mr. Brown, his voice rising, Mr. Agran said, "You have raised tens of millions of dollars in California and elsewhere."

At the end of the debate, Mr. Agran mimicked Mr. Brown by holding up a sign with his "800" number for contributions. "I want to make Jerry Brown feel more comfortable," he said of Mr. Brown, who often irritates his rivals by giving out his own "800" number at debates.

The candidates also debated American Indian affairs, the environment and health care, but spent far more time than they have in previous encounters outlining the differences among themselves.

Each of the Democrats took a shot at Mr. Tsongas, who, they complained, has not been held responsible for the consequences of his positions.

Mr. Clinton, who accused Mr. Tsongas on Saturday of engaging in "pious rhetoric" on the campaign trail, lashed out.

"It is frustrating for me to be told the only morally appropriate way to wean America off cheap foreign oil is a nickel-a-gallon gasoline tax," Mr. Clinton said. "Your incomes went down and your taxes went up in the 80's and Senator Tsongas wants to feed you more of the same in the 90's. And it's wrong." The Men to Beat

But some of the candidates seemed at a loss in competing with Mr. Harkin and Mr. Kerrey, both of whom must do well in order to continue credible campaigns for the Democratic nomination.

Mr. Clinton as much as acknowledged in his closing statement that he does not expected to win in the South Dakota contest. "I wish my state bordered South Dakota, but it doesn't," he said.

And Mr. Tsongas boasted that his wife, Niki, and children "have South Dakotan blood in their veins." Mr. Tsongas' father-in-law is from South Dakota.

Mr. Kerrey's campaign is widely viewed as the best organized in South Dakota, and a poll published Sunday by The Argus Leader, the state's largest newspaper, found that he was ahead of the pack. The newspaper also endorsed Mr. Kerrey.

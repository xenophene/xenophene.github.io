THE 1992 CAMPAIGN: Republicans; Buchanan's Run Exposes Fissures in the Right

By STEVEN A. HOLMES
Published: February 04, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

Whatever the outcome of the fight over the Republican nomination, Patrick J. Buchanan is exposing fault lines in the conservative movement at a time when the twin epoxies that bound conservatives -- Ronald Reagan and international communism -- have melted away.

As he campaigns in New Hampshire, Mr. Buchanan, a former White House speechwriter who is at times to the right of the two Presidents he served, has made clear that his goal is not only to unhorse President Bush as the titular head of the Republican Party, but also to change the direction of the conservative movement.

At a recent campaign stop in Littleton, N.H., Mr. Buchanan acknowleged the ideological fracturing among conservatives and argued that his campaign had become its focal point. "There is tremendous ferment in America, politically and ideologically," he told a gathering at the Littleton Elks Club. "The old conservative movement is breaking apart. Some of my friends are barely on speaking terms with each other; some aren't on speaking terms with me."

There is little doubt that many in the right wing of the Republican Party, especially those outside government, and not beholden to the Administration, yearned for a conservative besides David Duke, a former leader of the Ku Klux Klan and Nazi sympathizer, to challenge Mr. Bush. These conservatives view Mr. Bush as nothing short of a Judas who sold them out when he reneged on his pledge not to raise taxes and when he signed the civil rights bill. Some Balk at Buchanan

But by espousing positions on trade, immigration and the role of the United States in the world, views tinged with a kind of isolationism and protectionism that are anathema to many free-market and globalist conservatives, Mr. Buchanan is seen by some as an imperfect spear with which to impale the President.

"One has this almost irresistible urge to leap out of your chair and say, 'Pat, this guy deserves to be socked. Do it!' " said David Keene, chairman of the American Conservative Union. "But Pat's mission is not just to sock the President, but to change conservatism in a way I don't like."

For some conservatives, supporting Mr. Buchanan therefore seems like a Faustian bargain. Their long-shot hope is for the challenger to bloody Mr. Bush in New Hampshire so that the President withdraws from the fray or a more acceptable conservative like Pete DuPont, the former Delaware Governor or William J. Bennett, the former Education Secretary, is enticed to enter the race.

But if Mr. Buchanan embarrasses the President and no other conservative takes up the challenge, "then conservatives are stuck with either Buchanan or Bush," said Burton Yale Pines, vice president and director of research for the Heritage Foundation. "It is not a pleasant choice." Worries on the Right

Equally worrying to some conservatives is that a strong showing by Mr. Buchanan, while felling the President, also gives impetus to Mr. Buchanan's style of conservatism.

Mr. Keene said a debate had raged for a time in the Buchanan camp with some advisers urging the candidate to tone down some of his language to avoid being tagged as an isolationist or a protectionist. But Mr. Buchanan rejected that strategy, Mr. Keene said, saying voters should see the "whole package."

Officials with the Buchanan campaign deny there was an internal debate. But they say the candidate decided early that he would not modulate his views to increase his appeal to some conservatives.

At stops throughout New Hampshire Mr. Buchanan has bashed the Japanese for conducting what he terms "predatory trade policies," railed against the free-trade agreement being negotiated with Mexico, advocated possible United States withdrawal from multinational agencies like the World Bank and the International Monetary Fund, criticized the invasion of Panama and the economic blockade of Haiti and said he was against extending $10 billion in housing loan guarantees to Israel. Not Cowed by Labels

He has refused to be cowed by the neo-isolationist or protectionist labels some have tried to pin on him and has asserted that the tough economic times and voter discontent will force conservatives who disagree with him to adopt similar positions.

The core of Mr. Buchanan's message is that with the end of the cold war the prevalent force in the world today is nationalism. He argues that with countries like Japan centering their policies on their own national economic interests, the United States would be foolish to adopt purely free-trade practices or surrender authority to multinational agencies.

Such themes are reminiscent of the conservative isolationism advocated in the 1940's and 1950's by Robert A. Taft, the Ohio Senator who fought Wendell Willkie and Dwight D. Eisenhower for leadership of the Republican Party. The rise of the Soviet Empire, however, caused Mr. Taft's brand of conservatism to be subordinated to the goal of defeating Communism. Some See Anti-Semitism

"With the decline of the cold war, the underlying strain of isolationism among conservatives is now feeling more freedom to express itself," said A. James Reichley, a former researcher at the Brookings Institution who wrote "Conservatives in an Age of Change.".

Some conservatives, especially neo-conservatives like Norman Podhoretz, editor of Commentary Magazine, are also troubled by what they see as anti-Semitism in some of Mr. Buchanan's statements about Israel and what he calls Jewish influence on United States policy in the Middle East. These conservatives see in Mr. Buchanan a re-emergence of the type of anti-Jewish attitudes that haunted much of the conservative movement for years and that figures like William F. Buckley have since the mid-1950's sought to expel from American conservatism.

Last month in a long essay on anti-Semitism that appeared in National Review, Mr. Buckley after reviewing remarks Mr. Buchanan made on television talk shows, in interviews and in his columns, wrote, "I find it impossible to defend Pat Buchanan against the charge that what he did and said during the period under examination amounted to anti-Semitism."

Still, Mr. Buckley said, Mr. Buchanan's alleged anti-Semitic outbursts were probably motivated by "an iconoclastic temperament," rather than bigotry. And in a recent issue of Human Events, a conservative weekly newspaper, Mr. Buckley said that if he lived in New Hampshire he would probably vote for the challenger.

But polls show that Mr. Buchanan's campaign has so far failed to ignite voters in New Hampshire despite the state's sorry economic condition. And the ambivalence of the right wing of the Republican Party toward Mr. Buchanan's insurgency is a marked contrast to support in 1976 for Mr. Reagan's challenge to Gerald Ford.

"This is not a division among Republicans like Taft versus Eisenhower or Dewey," Mr. Reichley said. "It's not a battle royal like it was in 1948 or 1952. Buchanan would like it to be, but what he has is a splinter group, rather than a large wing of the party."

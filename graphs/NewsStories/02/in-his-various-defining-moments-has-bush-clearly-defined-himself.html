In His Various Defining Moments, Has Bush Clearly Defined Himself?

By R. W. APPLE Jr
Published: February 23, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

GEORGE BUSH has been in public life since the 1960's, and he has been President of the United States, scrutinized every day in every way, for more than three years. Yet as he labored last week to recover from a Republican primary in New Hampshire in which he was able to attract only 53 per cent of the vote, his managers were still struggling to find some way to present a clearer impression of the man and his policies.

A New York Times/CBS News poll conducted last week dramatized the depth of the President's problem: the poll showed that more than half of registered voters find it hard to say what Mr. Bush hopes to accomplish in a second term -- although, of course, Mr. Bush still has several months in which to refine his message to the nation.

Coming up with an agenda is usually a challenger's problem. But Mr. Bush has never struck most people as a man with a solid core of political beliefs. Personal character and values, yes; he is well known as a family man, a modest and self-effacing man, by the standards of big-time politicians, at least. But deep political roots like Ronald Reagan's or Harry S. Truman's? Not really.

In the battle for the 1980 Presidential nomination, George Bush ran as something of a Rockefeller Republican, dismissing the program of his rival, Mr. Reagan, with the caustic jab, "voodoo economics." When Mr. Reagan chose him as his running mate (which says a lot for Mr. Bush's ability to utter harsh words without making enemies), that sort of talk was quickly shelved.

Mr. Bush quickly took on the ideological coloration of the Reagan Administration; not once during its whole eight years did he distance himself from any of its policies in any significant way. George Bush the Planned Parenthood advocate became George Bush the foe of abortion, for example. Ambiguous Record

In the 1988 primary and general election campaigns, it was George Bush the Reaganite who showed up, painting Bob Dole as a spineless wonder with a dangerous predilection for big government spending, and Michael S. Dukakis as a softie who let violent criminals out of jail to commit more crimes.

Lately, the record has been ambiguous. As Patrick J. Buchanan kept reminding the voters of New Hampshire, Mr. Bush reneged on his campaign slogan, "no new taxes," and he has a confusing record on civil rights. He signed the Civil Rights Act of 1991 while continuing to appoint judges and make speeches that civil rights leaders could not stomach.

Contradictions abound. The self-proclaimed education and environment President, he habitually takes what experts in those fields deem half measures. A foe of censorship, he dumped the head of the National Endowment for the Arts the moment that Mr. Buchanan threatened to make an issue of arts policy in the culturally conservative Southern states.

The useful aspect of such pragmatism, of course, lies in opening the way to compromise -- always valuable in foreign affairs, long Mr. Bush's prime interest, and increasingly valuable in the new world of domestic affairs, where a Republican President and a Congress controlled by the Democrats has come to be the rule, not the exception.

Less welcome is the harsh political fact that pragmatists have trouble building constituencies, especially in this era of single-issue politics. Mr. Reagan had a band of die-hard supporters who could be counted upon no matter what. Mr. Bush's fans come and go with the changing political weather, which is why he fell so rapidly in the polls when the economy turned sour. And he has never quite convinced conservatives that he is one of them.

It may be that the President's pragmatism derives from his early life, when, as a son of the liberal Eastern Establishment, he found himself in Texas, a land of tough, self-reliant conservatives.

Mr. Buchanan derided Mr. Bush's patrician background in a speech last week, mocking his prep school education, his membership in Skull and Bones, the toniest Yale club, and his wealth. The Mercedes-driving Mr. Buchanan even took a shot at the President's summer house, where, he said, "the Walker's Point G.O.P. salves its social conscience at other people's expense" by backing "reverse discrimination," the challenger's term for affirmative action.

He doesn't mention George Bush the Texan, the horseshoe-tosser, the nibbler of pork rinds. Those are the symbols that the President has used to conjure up the ordinary-guy image he deems politically preferable, to which he adds conservative political language, if not always conservative actions. Indecisive Tactics

The White House said last week that there would be no change in the President's policies, not even the economic proposals that failed to stir the country in the State of the Union speech. What was needed, it was said, is some limited tactical fine-tuning.

With voters still blaming the Administration, not Congress, for the recession, that is a debatable judgment. But even on tactics, there was indecision; at first it was said Mr. Bush would attack Mr. Buchanan, a vulnerable foe with a long paper trail of contentious and questionable statements. Then the handlers decided that going on the attack would "demean the office," as one said. Snarling-dog duties would be performed by Vice President Dan Quayle and perhaps by some less anodyne television ads.

If Mr. Buchanan can be dispatched without too much self-definition by the President, the ambiguity might prove beneficial in holding the party together in November. Mr. Bush has hoped, and apparently still hopes, that he can avoid offending the right-wingers whose support he will need in the fall.

The Democrats, meanwhile, struggle with their own problems of definition. Former Senator Paul E. Tsongas of Massachusetts and Gov. Bill Clinton have jumped clear of the pack, the Times/CBS poll shows, and Democratic voters, if not Democratic leaders, are starting to get a bit more comfortable with the idea of one of the active contenders as the nominee.

But not one of the five hopefuls has made enough of a splash that even half the voters are willing to rate them in the most general way: Do you approve or disapprove of this guy? In addition, Mr. Clinton has high negative numbers, a legacy, perhaps, of allegations of marital infidelity and draft avoidance.

Unless things change radically in the next few months, those factors could give Mr. Bush and the Republicans a chance to daub the opposing nominee with colors of their own choosing, something they did to the hapless Mr. Dukakis in 1988.

Perhaps slogans and reputation-busting will be less successful this year. But don't count on it. Dwight D. Eisenhower observed hopefully four decades ago that the American people wouldn't buy a President sold like toothpaste; he hasn't been proved right yet.

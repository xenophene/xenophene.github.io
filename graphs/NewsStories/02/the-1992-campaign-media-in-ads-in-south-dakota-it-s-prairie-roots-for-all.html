THE 1992 CAMPAIGN: Media; In Ads in South Dakota, It's Prairie Roots for All

By ELIZABETH KOLBERT
Published: February 24, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

Senator Tom Harkin of Iowa will be a President "who knows exactly where we're coming from -- 'cause that's where he's coming from." South Dakotans won't have to guess "whose side he's on," his radio advertising assure listeners; it's "ours."

Senator Bob Kerrey of Nebraska is similarly in touch with "our" feelings. He is "from the Great Plains" and "fighting to save the family farm." Most importantly, as his advertisements say, he is "one of us."

Former Senator Paul E. Tsongas of Massachusetts is not "one of us," but luckily for him, his father-in-law, Russ Sauvage, is. "I was born and grew up in South Dakota," a smiling Mr. Sauvage says in Mr. Tsongas's latest television advertisement.

Perhaps President John F. Kennedy said it best because he said it in a foreign language: "Ich bin ein Berliner." In politics, it always helps to establish a connection with your audience, even if you have to call in your in-laws. And now that the Democratic Presidential primary has moved from the East Coast out here to the heartland, everyone, it seems, is from the prairie -- or least talks as if he wishes he were. Pledge to Heartland

"I understand how important it is for us to continue to aggressively support farm exports," says Gov. Bill Clinton of Arkansas in his most recent radio advertisement. "South Dakota's got a strong, strong history of supporting the family farm. That's what I believe in, that's what I've fought for, and that's what I'll stand for if I'm President."

In a televised debate here tonight, the candidates reiterated their pledge of fraternity with the heartland, sometimes to comic effect. "I wish my state bordered South Dakota," Mr. Clinton said.

New Hampshire voters, who were assured that restoring the country's manufacturing base was the candidates' top priority, would be justifiably disappointed to hear the candidates talking now. If the rallying cry in New Hampshire was "jobs, jobs, jobs," here it is more like "hogs, hogs, hogs."

"It's good to be back in South Dakota, where you can say 'barrows and gilts,' and people don't think you're talking about some fancy Boston law firm," Mr. Harkin said on Saturday. (Hint for effete Easterners: barrows and gilts are male and female pigs.)

Coming just a week after New Hampshire and a week before Georgia, the South Dakota primary has received short shrift from most of the Democratic candidates, who are now struggling to establish intimacy with the voters here without actually having to meet them. They have been assisted in this effort by cheap television time here (for about $30,000 a candidate can saturate the airwaves from Sioux Falls to Rapid City).

For Senators Kerrey and Harkin, affecting a rapport with voters who live in big, flat states is not that tough. Both come from states that border on South Dakota, both have strong agricultural records and both, it can be assumed, have a keen understanding of porcine terminology. And when Mr. Kerrey says he is "one of us" and Mr. Harkin says he knows "exactly where we're coming from," many South Dakotans seem willing to overlook the obvious note of opportunism.

For Mr. Tsongas, who gamely visited a stockyard on Thursday for what The Argus Leader here called the "requisite candidate-with-cow" photograph, the challenge of relating to a prairie state mentality has been more daunting. But instead of laughing at Mr. Tsongas's tenuous South Dakota ties several people here said the television spot featuring Mr. Sauvage had intrigued them.

"It was kind of impressive," said David Crissman, a railroad foreman.

Because the South Dakota primary is widely viewed as an elimination race between Mr. Kerrey and Mr. Harkin, the two men were considered the most likely to broadcast negative advertisements here. And they have, but not always against each other.

While Mr. Harkin is broadcasting a commercial comparing his record with Mr. Kerrey's, Mr. Kerrey, who is leading in the polls, ignores Mr. Harkin altogether. Instead, he goes after Mr. Tsongas and Mr. Clinton, suggesting they will be bad for farmers.

If being "one of us," as Mr. Kerrey puts it, were the main criterion voters here were using, the Nebraska Senator would probably not have to worry about winning the primary, since South Dakota and Nebraska share a border several hundred miles long. But as it is, there are still some South Dakotans who seem to believe that being from the Great Plains, or having a father-in-law who is, is not really relevant to being a good President.

"Kerrey's from Nebraska, sure," said Pat Cypher, who works for a trucking company here. "But there's a lot of idiots from Nebraska, too."

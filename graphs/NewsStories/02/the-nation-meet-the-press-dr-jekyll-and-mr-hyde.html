THE NATION; Meet the Press: Dr. Jekyll and Mr. Hyde

By ALEX S. JONES
Published: February 09, 1992
  • Sign In to E-Mail
  • Print

In a broadcast that may have set a new standard for tastelessness, the syndicated tabloid television program "A Current Affair" interviewed Gennifer Flowers last week about the affair she says she had with Gov. Bill Clinton of Arkansas.

In the interview Ms. Flowers was asked to rate the Presidential candidate as a lover on a scale of one to ten, which she earnestly did. Then, to close the show, Ms. Flowers, a sometime nightclub singer, warbled "Stand By Your Man," the Tammy Wynette song Mr. Clinton's wife, Hillary, slighted when she defended her husband on "60 Minutes."

When it comes to shows like "A Current Affair," many newspaper editors and television news producers like to consider themselves in another realm. But the case of Gennifer Flowers -- Mr. Clinton insists she was just a "friendly acquaintance" -- has been a public relations problem for the news business as a whole.

For one thing, millions of Americans continue to see the press as a monolithic force, lumping "A Current Affair" and "The Oprah Winfrey Show" together with "NBC Nightly News," and Star, the grocery store tabloid that paid Ms. Flowers about $150,000 for her story, with The New York Times and The Wall Street Journal.

But it is also true that a symbiotic relationship has arisen between the two extremes of American journalism, with charges trumpeted by supermarket tabloids picked up by serious news organizations and converted from dross into journalistic gilt.

Ms. Flowers's charges and Mr. Clinton's denial might have been a momentary obsession that quickly passed, but both wings of the press have effectively worked in tandem to keep the story alive. The sensational wing has kept stoking the flames and repeating the story with every mention of Mr. Clinton. The more mainstream press has reported on the impact the story is having on Mr. Clinton's candidacy, and in doing so they have repeated the accusations as well. As a result Ms. Flowers's accusations have been all but grafted onto Mr. Clinton's campaign for the Presidency. Creating Reality

"We like to say we cover the news, but in fact we create the reality, and we have created this event," said Joan W. Konner, dean of the Graduate School of Journalism at Columbia University. Because the impact of the story now threatens Mr. Clinton's candidacy, no serious news organization can cover his prospects without at least making reference to Ms. Flowers.

For instance, the "CBS Evening News" devoted a segment last week to fears by some Democrats that while President George Bush may be vulnerable, the present crop of candidates may not be able to beat him. In the few sound bites and video clips devoted to Mr. Clinton's prospects, Ms. Flowers's picture was included and her charges repeated yet again.

At times, news organizations almost seem to be trying to catch up to the lightning-fast medium of word-of-mouth. Indeed, when Knight-Ridder in recent years conducted a long series of focus groups at The Boca Raton News in Florida to help determine how people get their news, they discovered that one of the most important sources was being told by friends.

"Part of what we do is set the agenda of what people will talk about, and part of what we do is follow the agenda of what people set by virtue of what they are already talking about," said Louis Heldman, executive editor of The Tallahassee Democrat and the incoming chairman of the Future of Newspapers Committee of the American Society of Newspaper Editors.

On Jan. 23, copies of Star first appeared with Ms. Flowers's picture on the cover under the headline "My 12-Year Affair With Bill Clinton: Mistress Tells All." As a grocery-store tabloid, Star does not command much credibility among major news organizations, but the article had three elements that made it hard to ignore: it was apparently a first-person account by someone willing to be identified who claimed to have tapes of conversations; it was plausible because Mr. Clinton had already said he had had a troubled marriage; and it was so sensational that it was certain to be reported elsewhere. A Juicy Tale

While many mainstream news organizations reported the story cautiously and minimally, most gave it prominent attention. When Mr. and Mrs. Clinton decided to appear on "60 Minutes" to rebut the charges, the story became more legitimate news, and was even more prominently covered. There followed a news conference by Ms. Flowers to respond to Mr. Clinton's response, and both wings of the press were there.

For the more sensational media, such a juicy tale is manna from heaven and keeping it alive means attracting readers and viewers, something the more mainstream press is also eager to do.

After John Terenzio, executive producer of "A Current Affair," successfully negotiated an exclusive appearance by Ms. Flowers on the show, he was told by her representative that a storm of other offers had come in. Mr. Terenzio declined to say whether he paid Ms. Flowers, but he said the show sometimes pays for exclusivity.

Mr. Terenzio, who five months ago was executive producer of weekend newscasts for NBC News, said that "A Current Affair" is normally seen by about 21 million people and that the Flowers show, which was heavily promoted, attracted a host of requests for video clips, pictures and sound bites from other news organizations.

He said "A Current Affair" is "very much a show of heartland U.S.A."

"Our viewers expect we would have a story like this," he said. " 'A Current Affair' is to television tabloid news magazines what Kleenex is to tissue."

"Will we find her newsworthy in the future?" he asked. "Certainly that's possible."
